## Understanding PTSD VA Disability Ratings

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) is one of the most commonly claimed and service-connected conditions among veterans. The VA rates PTSD under 38 CFR § 4.130, Diagnostic Code 9411, using specific criteria based on the severity of symptoms and their impact on occupational and social functioning.

### What is PTSD?

PTSD develops after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. For veterans, common triggers include:
- Combat exposure
- Military sexual trauma (MST)
- Serious accidents or injuries
- Natural disasters during deployment
- Witnessing death or severe injury
- Threats to personal safety
- Exposure to human remains

PTSD symptoms typically fall into four categories:
1. **Intrusive thoughts:** Flashbacks, nightmares, distressing memories
2. **Avoidance:** Avoiding trauma-related thoughts, places, or people
3. **Negative changes in cognition and mood:** Memory problems, negative beliefs, detachment
4. **Alterations in arousal and reactivity:** Hypervigilance, irritability, sleep disturbances, exaggerated startle response

## VA Rating Criteria for PTSD

### 0% Rating
**Criteria:** A mental condition has been formally diagnosed, but symptoms are not severe enough to interfere with occupational and social functioning or require continuous medication.

**Typical Symptoms:**
- Diagnosis confirmed but minimal functional impairment
- Occasional mild symptoms during high stress
- No medication required or only as needed
- Normal work and social functioning

**Monthly Compensation:** $0 (but establishes service connection for future increases)

---

### 10% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment due to mild or transient symptoms which decrease work efficiency and ability to perform occupational tasks only during periods of significant stress, or symptoms controlled by continuous medication.

**Typical Symptoms:**
- Mild anxiety or depression
- Occasional difficulty sleeping
- Minor social withdrawal
- Symptoms manageable with medication
- Able to maintain employment
- Mild suspiciousness or panic attacks (not frequent)

**Monthly Compensation:**
- Veteran alone: $175.51
- With spouse: $175.51 (no dependent increase at 10%)
- **20-year value:** $42,122

---

### 30% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment with occasional decrease in work efficiency and intermittent periods of inability to perform occupational tasks (although generally functioning satisfactorily, with routine behavior, self-care, and conversation normal).

**Typical Symptoms:**
- Depressed mood
- Anxiety
- Chronic sleep impairment
- Mild memory loss (e.g., forgetting names, directions)
- Suspiciousness
- Panic attacks occurring weekly or less often
- Difficulty establishing and maintaining effective work and social relationships
- Difficulty adapting to stressful situations (including work)

**Monthly Compensation:**
- Veteran alone: $537.42
- With spouse: $601.42
- With spouse & 1 child: $650.42
- **20-year value:** $128,981 (veteran alone)

---

### 50% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment with reduced reliability and productivity.

**Typical Symptoms:**
- Flattened affect
- Circumstantial, circumlocutory, or stereotyped speech
- Panic attacks more than once a week
- Difficulty understanding complex commands
- Impairment of short- and long-term memory
- Impaired judgment
- Impaired abstract thinking
- Disturbances of motivation and mood
- Difficulty establishing and maintaining effective relationships
- Difficulty adapting to stressful circumstances (including work or a worklike setting)

**Monthly Compensation:**
- Veteran alone: $1,102.04
- With spouse: $1,180.04
- With spouse & 1 child: $1,243.04
- With spouse & 2 children: $1,300.04
- **20-year value:** $264,490 (veteran alone)

---

### 70% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment, with deficiencies in most areas, such as work, school, family relations, judgment, thinking, or mood.

**Typical Symptoms:**
- Suicidal ideation
- Obsessional rituals which interfere with routine activities
- Near-continuous panic or depression affecting the ability to function independently, appropriately, and effectively
- Impaired impulse control (unprovoked irritability with periods of violence)
- Spatial disorientation
- Neglect of personal appearance and hygiene
- Difficulty adapting to stressful circumstances (including work or a worklike setting)
- Inability to establish and maintain effective relationships

**Monthly Compensation:**
- Veteran alone: $1,759.19
- With spouse: $1,861.19
- With spouse & 1 child: $1,938.19
- With spouse & 2 children: $2,009.19
- **20-year value:** $422,206 (veteran alone)

**Note:** 70% is often considered the threshold where veterans can no longer maintain substantial gainful employment due to PTSD symptoms.

---

### 100% Rating (Total Occupational and Social Impairment)
**Criteria:** Total occupational and social impairment due to such symptoms as:

**Typical Symptoms:**
- Gross impairment in thought processes or communication
- Persistent delusions or hallucinations
- Grossly inappropriate behavior
- Persistent danger of hurting self or others
- Intermittent inability to perform activities of daily living (including maintenance of minimal personal hygiene)
- Disorientation to time or place
- Memory loss for names of close relatives, own occupation, or own name

**Monthly Compensation:**
- Veteran alone: $3,831.30
- With spouse: $4,019.30
- With spouse & 1 child: $4,123.30
- With spouse & 2 children: $4,221.30
- **20-year value:** $919,512 (veteran alone)

## Evidence Requirements for PTSD Claims

### 1. Current Diagnosis
You must have a current PTSD diagnosis that meets the criteria in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5). This diagnosis must be made by:
- VA psychiatrist or psychologist
- Private psychiatrist or psychologist
- Licensed clinical social worker
- Licensed professional counselor

**Key DSM-5 Criteria for PTSD:**
- Exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence
- Presence of intrusion symptoms
- Persistent avoidance of trauma-related stimuli
- Negative alterations in cognitions and mood
- Marked alterations in arousal and reactivity
- Duration of symptoms more than one month
- Symptoms cause significant distress or impairment

### 2. In-Service Stressor Event
You must prove that a stressful event occurred during military service. The level of evidence required depends on the type of stressor:

**Combat Veterans:**
- If you served in combat, you only need to show that your stressor is consistent with the circumstances of your service
- Evidence of combat can include service records, awards (CAR, CAB, CIB), or unit records

**Non-Combat Stressors:**
- Personal assault (including MST): Can provide markers of behavior change
- Fear of hostile military or terrorist activity: Must be related to service in a war zone
- Other stressors: Need credible supporting evidence

**Supporting Documentation:**
- Service treatment records
- Buddy statements from fellow service members
- After-action reports
- Unit histories or command chronologies
- Personal journals or letters from deployment
- Awards and decorations
- Personnel records showing duty locations

### 3. Medical Nexus (Link)
You must establish that your current PTSD is connected to your in-service stressor. This can be shown through:
- VA examiner's opinion (after C&P exam)
- Private nexus letter from qualified mental health professional
- Service treatment records showing PTSD symptoms began during service
- Medical records showing continuity of treatment after service

**Strong Nexus Statement Example:**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's current PTSD is caused by or aggravated by the combat experiences during deployment to Afghanistan in 2011, as evidenced by the veteran's service treatment records documenting anxiety and sleep disturbances, and the continuous treatment for PTSD-related symptoms since discharge."

### 4. Severity Documentation
To support your desired rating, provide evidence of symptom severity:
- **Detailed treatment records:** Frequency of therapy sessions, medication trials, hospitalizations
- **Employment records:** Job changes, terminations, reduced hours, disability accommodations
- **Educational records:** Inability to complete courses, need for extensions or accommodations
- **Social functioning:** Statements about relationship difficulties, social isolation
- **Daily functioning:** Evidence of difficulty with self-care, household management
- **Police or court records:** If applicable, showing impulsivity or anger issues
- **Financial records:** Showing inability to manage finances due to PTSD

## Common Secondary Conditions to PTSD

Veterans with service-connected PTSD can file for secondary conditions caused or aggravated by their PTSD. These secondary claims can significantly increase overall disability compensation.

### 1. Sleep Apnea (Obstructive)
**Connection:** PTSD causes hypervigilance, nightmares, and disrupted sleep patterns that can lead to or worsen sleep apnea. Studies show veterans with PTSD have 2-3 times higher rates of sleep apnea.

**Rating Potential:** 0%, 30%, 50%, or 100%
- 50% rating: Requires use of CPAP machine with chronic respiratory failure or cor pulmonale
- 30% rating: Persistent daytime hypersomnolence

**Evidence Needed:**
- Sleep study (polysomnography) showing apnea-hypopnea index (AHI)
- Nexus letter explaining how PTSD-related sleep disturbances caused or aggravated sleep apnea
- Treatment records showing CPAP prescription

### 2. Migraines/Chronic Headaches
**Connection:** PTSD causes chronic stress, tension, hypervigilance, and poor sleep, all of which trigger or worsen migraines and tension headaches.

**Rating Potential:** 0%, 10%, 30%, or 50%
- 50% rating: Prostrating attacks occurring weekly or less often
- 30% rating: Characteristic prostrating attacks occurring on average once a month

**Evidence Needed:**
- Headache diary documenting frequency and severity
- Treatment records showing migraine medications
- Nexus letter connecting headaches to PTSD-related stress and tension

### 3. Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
**Connection:** PTSD affects the gut-brain axis, causing chronic gastrointestinal symptoms. Anxiety and stress directly impact digestive function.

**Rating Potential:** 0%, 10%, or 30%
- 30% rating: Severe symptoms with frequent episodes of bowel disturbance and abdominal distress
- 10% rating: Moderate symptoms with occasional episodes

**Evidence Needed:**
- GI evaluation and diagnosis
- Colonoscopy or other testing ruling out other conditions
- Treatment records showing IBS medications
- Nexus letter explaining PTSD-IBS connection

### 4. Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)
**Connection:** PTSD medications (especially SSRIs) and chronic stress increase stomach acid production and GERD symptoms.

**Rating Potential:** 0%, 10%, 30%, or 60%
- 30% rating: Persistently recurrent epigastric distress with dysphagia, pyrosis, and regurgitation
- 10% rating: Two or more of the symptoms of epigastric distress, dysphagia, pyrosis, and regurgitation

**Evidence Needed:**
- Upper endoscopy results
- Treatment records showing PPI medications
- Nexus letter connecting GERD to PTSD medications or stress

### 5. Erectile Dysfunction (ED)
**Connection:** PTSD medications (SSRIs), psychological factors (anxiety, depression, avoidance), and chronic stress all contribute to erectile dysfunction.

**Rating Potential:** 0% with Special Monthly Compensation (SMC-K: $127.19/month)

**Evidence Needed:**
- Urological evaluation ruling out other causes
- Documentation of PTSD medication use
- Nexus letter connecting ED to PTSD or PTSD medications

### 6. Temporomandibular Joint Disorder (TMJ)
**Connection:** PTSD causes teeth grinding (bruxism), jaw clenching, and facial tension, leading to TMJ dysfunction.

**Rating Potential:** 0%, 10%, 20%, 30%, 40%, or 50%
- 30% rating: Interincisal range of 11-20 mm
- 40% rating: Interincisal range of 0-10 mm

**Evidence Needed:**
- Dental records documenting TMJ dysfunction
- Evidence of teeth grinding or night guard use
- Nexus letter connecting TMJ to PTSD-related bruxism

### 7. Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
**Connection:** PTSD and depression commonly co-occur. PTSD symptoms of negative mood, anhedonia, and isolation overlap with and worsen depression.

**Rating:** Typically rated under the same diagnostic code as PTSD (9411) with a combined rating for both conditions.

**Evidence Needed:**
- Diagnosis of MDD from mental health professional
- Treatment records showing depression-specific symptoms
- Documentation that MDD symptoms are distinct from or worsen baseline PTSD

### 8. Generalized Anxiety Disorder
**Connection:** PTSD causes hypervigilance and hyperarousal, which manifest as generalized anxiety about everyday situations.

**Rating:** Typically rated under the same diagnostic code (9411) or separately if symptoms are distinct.

**Evidence Needed:**
- GAD diagnosis
- Treatment records showing anxiety beyond PTSD-specific triggers
- Nexus letter if filing as separate condition

### 9. Chronic Pain Syndrome
**Connection:** PTSD causes chronic muscle tension, stress, and hypervigilance, which lead to widespread chronic pain.

**Rating Potential:** Varies by affected body part

**Evidence Needed:**
- Pain management records
- Multiple body system evaluations
- Nexus letters connecting each pain condition to PTSD

### 10. Substance Abuse Disorders
**Connection:** Veterans with PTSD often self-medicate with alcohol or drugs to manage symptoms.

**Rating Potential:** 0%, 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, or 100%

**Evidence Needed:**
- Substance abuse treatment records
- Nexus letter showing self-medication pattern
- Documentation of PTSD symptoms predating substance use

## C&P Exam Preparation

The Compensation and Pension (C&P) exam is critical to your PTSD claim. The examiner will complete a Disability Benefits Questionnaire (DBQ) that directly impacts your rating.

### What to Expect at Your PTSD C&P Exam

**Duration:** 30-90 minutes

**Examiner:** Psychiatrist, psychologist, or licensed clinical social worker

**Exam Components:**
1. Review of your medical history
2. Discussion of your in-service stressor(s)
3. Mental status examination
4. Assessment of 31 PTSD symptom areas
5. Evaluation of functional impairment

### 31 Symptom Areas Assessed

The examiner will evaluate you on these symptoms:
1. Depressed mood
2. Anxiety
3. Suspiciousness
4. Panic attacks (weekly or less, more than weekly)
5. Chronic sleep impairment
6. Mild memory loss
7. Flattened affect
8. Circumstantial, circumlocutory, or stereotyped speech
9. Impaired judgment
10. Impaired abstract thinking
11. Disturbances of motivation and mood
12. Difficulty establishing/maintaining effective relationships
13. Difficulty adapting to stressful circumstances
14. Inability to establish/maintain effective work relationships
15. Suicidal ideation
16. Obsessional rituals interfering with routine
17. Near-continuous panic or depression
18. Impaired impulse control
19. Spatial disorientation
20. Neglect of personal appearance/hygiene
21. Difficulty with work or work-like settings
22. Gross impairment in thought processes or communication
23. Persistent delusions or hallucinations
24. Grossly inappropriate behavior
25. Persistent danger of hurting self or others
26. Intermittent inability to perform ADLs
27. Disorientation to time or place
28. Memory loss for names of close relatives
29. Memory loss for own occupation
30. Memory loss for own name
31. Other symptoms affecting occupational/social functioning

### How to Prepare

**1-2 Weeks Before:**
- Review your service records and remind yourself of stressor details
- Organize medical records chronologically
- Ask family members to write buddy statements
- Keep a symptom diary documenting bad days

**Day Before:**
- Get adequate sleep (or document sleep difficulties)
- Prepare list of current medications
- Write down important dates and events you want to mention
- Review your VA claim file if possible

**Day of Exam:**
- Arrive 15 minutes early
- Bring photo ID
- Bring list of current medications and providers
- Bring buddy statements if you have them

**During the Exam:**

**DO:**
- Be honest about your worst symptoms
- Describe your bad days, not just average days
- Explain how symptoms impact work, relationships, and daily life
- Mention all symptoms, even embarrassing ones
- Provide specific examples (frequency of panic attacks, nightmares per week)
- Discuss hospitalizations or ER visits
- Mention relationship failures, job losses, isolation
- Be detailed about substance use if applicable

**DON'T:**
- Minimize your symptoms
- Try to appear "strong" or "together"
- Leave out symptoms because you're embarrassed
- Say "I'm fine" when you're not
- Exaggerate (examiners can detect malingering)
- Be hostile or argumentative with examiner

### Key Questions to Answer Thoroughly

**About Your Stressor:**
- What happened during your service that led to PTSD?
- When and where did it occur?
- How did it affect you at the time?
- Were there multiple traumatic events?

**About Your Symptoms:**
- How often do you have nightmares? (per week)
- How often do you have flashbacks?
- What triggers your PTSD symptoms?
- How do you avoid trauma reminders?
- How has your mood changed?
- Describe your hypervigilance behaviors
- How is your sleep?
- Do you have anger or irritability issues?

**About Functional Impairment:**
- How does PTSD affect your work? (Specific examples: tardiness, conflicts, reduced performance, job loss)
- How does PTSD affect your relationships? (Specific examples: divorce, estrangement from family, isolation)
- What daily activities are difficult? (Grocery shopping, crowds, driving)
- How has your social life changed since service?
- Can you maintain personal hygiene?
- Can you manage household tasks?

### Red Flags That May Lower Your Rating

- Telling examiner "I'm doing great"
- Appearing well-groomed if you claim hygiene difficulties
- Saying symptoms are "under control" with medication (this suggests lower rating)
- Denying employment problems if you've been fired or quit
- Minimizing relationship difficulties
- Not providing specific frequency of symptoms

### After the Exam

- Write down everything discussed while fresh in memory
- If you forgot important information, submit a statement to VA within 30 days
- Request a copy of the DBQ/exam report
- If the examiner didn't address key symptoms, submit additional evidence

## Claim Strategy

### Initial Claim vs. Increase Claim

**Initial Claim:**
- File VA Form 21-526EZ (Application for Disability Compensation)
- Provide service treatment records
- Provide current diagnosis
- Provide stressor documentation
- Submit buddy statements and lay evidence
- Timeline: 6-12 months for decision (varies by region)

**Increase Claim:**
- File VA Form 21-526EZ (checking "increase" box)
- Provide recent treatment records showing worsening
- Document increased functional impairment (job loss, divorce, hospitalizations)
- Submit new nexus opinion if needed
- Timeline: 4-8 months for decision

### Claim Tips for Higher Ratings

**For 50% or Higher:**
- Document panic attacks (frequency is key: "more than once a week" for 50%+)
- Provide employment evidence (terminations, FMLA use, disability accommodations, reduced hours)
- Include relationship evidence (divorce decrees, estrangement from family)
- Submit hospitalization records (inpatient psychiatric treatment)
- Document medication trials (showing multiple medications needed)
- Provide therapy frequency (weekly or more suggests severity)

**For 70% or Higher:**
- Must show "deficiencies in most areas" (work, relationships, daily living)
- Document suicidal ideation (safety plans, crisis hotline use, ER visits)
- Show obsessional rituals interfering with routine (checking locks, avoidance behaviors)
- Provide evidence of impaired impulse control (police reports, anger issues)
- Document neglect of hygiene or appearance
- Show spatial disorientation or memory problems
- Demonstrate inability to maintain employment

**For 100% Rating:**
- Document total occupational and social impairment
- Provide evidence of psychiatric hospitalizations
- Show inability to perform ADLs consistently
- Document dangerous behaviors or persistent suicidal ideation
- Provide evidence of memory loss (forgetting family names, own information)
- Show grossly inappropriate behavior or persistent delusions
- Demonstrate inability to maintain even part-time work

### Gathering Strong Evidence

**Medical Evidence:**
- Mental health treatment records (every session note)
- Psychiatric hospitalization records
- Emergency room visits for psychiatric crisis
- Medication lists showing trials and adjustments
- Psychiatric evaluations with GAF scores (if available)
- Diagnoses of comorbid conditions

**Lay Evidence - Buddy Statements:**
Best statements come from:
- Fellow service members who witnessed stressor event
- Family members who describe behavior changes
- Former employers who explain job performance issues
- Friends who describe social withdrawal

**Strong Buddy Statement Structure:**
1. Relationship to veteran and how long known
2. Observations before military service (baseline)
3. Changes noticed during or after service
4. Specific examples with dates and details
5. Current observations of ongoing symptoms
6. Statement's credibility factors (frequency of contact)

**Employment Evidence:**
- Termination notices
- Performance reviews (showing decline)
- FMLA or sick leave documentation
- Disability accommodation requests
- Resignation letters citing health
- Unemployment records

**Educational Evidence:**
- Incomplete courses or withdrawn classes
- Academic accommodation requests
- Grade transcripts showing decline
- Letters from professors or counselors

### Common Claim Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Minimizing Symptoms:** Don't downplay difficulties out of pride or military culture
2. **Incomplete Stressor Information:** Provide detailed stressor evidence, not just "I was in combat"
3. **Missing C&P Exam:** Always attend or reschedule (missing can result in denial)
4. **Poor C&P Exam Performance:** Not describing worst symptoms or functional impairment
5. **Insufficient Treatment:** Lack of ongoing treatment suggests symptoms aren't severe
6. **Not Filing for Secondary Conditions:** Missing out on additional compensation
7. **Filing Too Soon:** Filing before diagnosis or before gathering evidence
8. **Not Responding to VA Requests:** Missing development letters or evidence requests
9. **Giving Up After Denial:** Not filing appeals when denial is based on insufficient evidence
10. **Not Using VSO or Attorney:** Complex claims benefit from professional representation

### When to Hire a VA Attorney or Agent

Consider professional representation when:
- Initial claim was denied
- You received a rating lower than expected
- Your symptoms are severe but rating doesn't reflect it
- You have complex secondary conditions
- You're appealing to Board of Veterans' Appeals
- You have difficulty articulating symptoms or gathering evidence

**Cost:** VA-accredited attorneys can charge 20-33% of past-due benefits, but only if they win your case. Initial consultations are often free.

### Individual Unemployability (TDIU)

If you're rated 70% or higher for PTSD but cannot maintain substantially gainful employment, you can apply for Total Disability based on Individual Unemployability (TDIU):
- Receive 100% rate ($3,831.30/month) even with rating below 100%
- Must show inability to work due to service-connected conditions
- Can have rating as low as 60% overall (if one condition is 40%+)

**TDIU Requirements:**
- Single condition rated 60%+ OR
- Combined rating of 70%+ with one condition at 40%+
- Unable to maintain substantially gainful employment due to service-connected disabilities
- Employment earning below poverty threshold

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: From 50% to 100%

**Veteran:** Marine Corps infantryman, served in Iraq 2005-2006
**Initial Rating:** 50% for PTSD (2012)
**Increased Rating:** 100% for PTSD (2019)

**What Changed:**
- Documented multiple psychiatric hospitalizations (2014, 2017, 2018)
- Provided evidence of job loss (terminated from three jobs 2013-2018)
- Divorce decree showing PTSD as contributing factor (2016)
- Treatment records showing increased therapy frequency (weekly sessions)
- New nexus letter documenting worsening symptoms despite treatment
- Buddy statements from ex-wife and brother describing daily functioning difficulties

**Result:** Rating increased from 50% to 100%, increasing monthly compensation from $1,102.04 to $3,831.30 ($2,729.26/month increase). Seven years of retroactive pay: $229,660.

**Key Lesson:** Don't assume your rating is permanent. If symptoms worsen, file for an increase with strong evidence of functional impairment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I get disability for PTSD if I wasn't in combat?

Yes. You can receive VA disability for PTSD from non-combat stressors including:
- Military Sexual Trauma (MST)
- Fear of hostile military or terrorist activity (if you served in war zone)
- Accidents or injuries during service
- Training incidents
- Witnessing death or severe injury
- Natural disasters during deployment

The type of stressor affects what evidence is required, but non-combat PTSD is commonly service-connected.

### What if I didn't report my trauma when it happened?

This is common, especially for MST and personal assault. The VA recognizes that many traumas go unreported during service. You can still be granted service connection using:
- Behavioral markers (relationship problems, performance decline, requests for transfer)
- Buddy statements describing changes in behavior
- Medical records showing PTSD treatment after service
- Your credible testimony about the event

### How long does a PTSD claim take?

**Initial Claim:** 6-12 months on average (varies by VA regional office)
**Supplemental Claim:** 4-6 months on average
**Higher-Level Review:** 4-5 months on average
**Board of Veterans' Appeals:** 12-24 months on average

You can check your claim status at va.gov or through the VA mobile app.

### Can my PTSD rating be reduced?

Yes, but it's rare. The VA can propose a reduction if:
- You've been rated for less than 5 years, AND
- The VA schedules a reexamination, AND
- The reexamination shows sustained improvement

**Protections:**
- **After 5 years:** Rating is "stabilized" and harder to reduce
- **After 20 years:** Rating is "protected" and very difficult to reduce
- **You must receive notice:** VA must notify you of proposed reduction and you can submit new evidence

**If proposed reduction occurs:** Continue treatment, gather evidence of ongoing symptoms, and submit evidence within the time period allowed (usually 60 days).

### Can I work with a 100% PTSD rating?

**For schedular 100% (based on symptoms alone):** Yes, you can work. The VA cannot reduce your rating just because you're employed.

**For TDIU (unemployability):** You generally cannot work, or earnings must be below poverty threshold. Working above the threshold may result in TDIU termination (though you'd keep your schedular rating).

### What if I also have depression or anxiety?

Mental health conditions (PTSD, depression, anxiety) are typically rated together under a single rating for "mental disorder." The VA will assign one rating that encompasses all your mental health symptoms.

Exception: If you have a completely separate and distinct mental health condition that arose from a different cause, it may be rated separately.

### Should I get a private PTSD evaluation?

A private evaluation can be valuable:
- **Pros:** More time with provider, detailed report, supportive nexus opinion, helpful for appeals
- **Costs:** $2,000-$5,000 typically
- **When most helpful:** Appeals, when VA exam was inadequate, for complex cases

The VA is not bound by private opinions but must consider them along with VA examiner's opinion.

### Can I file for PTSD if I'm still active duty?

Yes, through the Integrated Disability Evaluation System (IDES) or Benefits Delivery at Discharge (BDD):
- **IDES:** For service members found unfit for duty
- **BDD:** File 90-180 days before discharge for faster processing

### What's the difference between VA disability and Social Security disability?

**VA Disability:**
- Based on service-connected conditions
- Can work while receiving benefits
- Monthly tax-free compensation
- Healthcare through VA

**Social Security Disability (SSDI):**
- Based on inability to work (any cause)
- Cannot perform substantial gainful activity
- Taxable income
- Healthcare through Medicare after 24 months

You can receive both VA and SSDI simultaneously.

### How do I find my VA rating decision?

- **Online:** Log in to va.gov and check "Claim Status"
- **Mail:** VA will mail decision letter to your address on file
- **Phone:** Call 1-800-827-1000
- **VSO:** Your Veterans Service Officer can access your file

Request your C-file (claims file) to see all evidence the VA considered.

### Can I appeal a PTSD rating decision?

Yes. You have three options within one year of decision:
1. **Supplemental Claim:** Submit new and relevant evidence
2. **Higher-Level Review:** Have a senior reviewer look at same evidence
3. **Board Appeal:** Have case reviewed by Veterans Law Judge at Board of Veterans' Appeals

Most veterans choose supplemental claim first (if they have new evidence) or higher-level review (if VA made clear error).

### How often can I file for an increase?

Anytime your condition worsens. There's no mandatory waiting period between claims for increase. However, filing too frequently without evidence of worsening may result in denials.

**Best practice:** File for increase when you have clear evidence of worsening, such as:
- New hospitalizations
- Job loss due to symptoms
- Relationship breakdown
- Increased treatment frequency
- New symptoms emerging

### What if I disagree with my C&P examiner?

If you believe the C&P exam was inadequate:
1. Obtain a copy of the exam report (DBQ)
2. Submit a statement explaining why the exam is inadequate
3. Provide additional evidence (treatment records, buddy statements)
4. Consider obtaining an Independent Medical Opinion (IMO)
5. File Higher-Level Review or Supplemental Claim

Common exam inadequacies:
- Examiner didn't review records
- Exam was too brief to properly evaluate
- Examiner didn't address specific symptoms
- Examiner's opinion conflicts with treatment records

## Next Steps

### If You Haven't Filed Yet:
1. Obtain your service treatment records
2. Get a current PTSD diagnosis
3. Gather stressor evidence
4. Collect buddy statements
5. File VA Form 21-526EZ
6. Consider working with a VSO

### If You're Awaiting a Decision:
1. Continue treatment and document symptoms
2. Gather additional evidence if needed
3. Check claim status regularly at va.gov
4. Prepare for possible C&P exam
5. Keep copies of all submitted evidence

### If You Received a Lower Rating Than Expected:
1. Request copy of decision letter and C&P exam
2. Review evidence VA considered
3. Gather new evidence showing greater severity
4. File Supplemental Claim within one year
5. Consider VSO or attorney assistance

### If You Have an Established Rating:
1. File for secondary conditions
2. Continue treatment to document ongoing symptoms
3. File for increase if symptoms worsen
4. Consider TDIU if unable to work
5. Protect your rating by attending requested exams

## Conclusion

PTSD is one of the most significant service-connected disabilities affecting veterans today. Understanding the VA rating criteria, gathering strong evidence, and advocating for an accurate rating can make a life-changing financial difference—potentially over $900,000 across 20 years for a 100% rating.

Key takeaways:
- Ratings range from 0% to 100% based on severity and functional impairment
- Strong evidence includes treatment records, employment history, and buddy statements
- Secondary conditions significantly increase overall compensation
- Don't minimize symptoms during C&P exams—describe your worst days
- Appeal or file for increase if your rating doesn't match your symptoms
- Consider TDIU if unable to work due to PTSD
- Work with a VSO or attorney for complex claims

Your service mattered. Your sacrifice mattered. Fight for the benefits you've earned.
