## **4. Service Areas and Specializations**

### **Defining Your Service Areas**

**Geographic Coverage**

You can set service areas by:
1. **County** (e.g., "All of San Diego County")
2. **City** (e.g., "San Diego, Chula Vista, Oceanside")
3. **Zip Code** (e.g., specific zip codes you serve)
4. **Radius** (e.g., "30-mile radius from my business location")

**Strategic Considerations:**

**Larger Service Area = More Leads, More Drive Time**
- Pros: More lead opportunities, higher revenue potential
- Cons: Travel time reduces profitability, harder to provide fast service

**Smaller Service Area = Fewer Leads, Higher Profitability**
- Pros: Less drive time, better margins, faster response times
- Cons: Fewer lead opportunities, may limit growth

**Best Practice:**
- Start with your primary county
- Expand to adjacent areas once you're consistently booking projects
- Set maximum drive time (e.g., "I won't drive more than 45 minutes")

- Project scope: Smaller modifications
- Best for: Contractors who want high volume, quick turnaround projects

- Grant amount: Up to $20,929
- Lead fee: $600
- Project scope: Mid-sized remodels
- Best for: Contractors who want balanced project size and timeline

**SAH (Special Adaptive Housing)**
- Grant amount: Up to $104,648
- Lead fee: $800
- Project scope: Complete home renovations or new construction
- Best for: Contractors who want large, high-profit projects

**Recommendation:**
- New contractors: Start with HISA and SHA to build experience

## **11. Compliance and VA Grant Regulations**

### **Understanding VA Grant Requirements**

**All Three Grant Types Have Specific Rules:**

**SAH (Special Adaptive Housing) - Up to $104,648**
- **Eligible Veterans**: Those with permanent and total service-connected disabilities, including:
  - Loss or loss of use of both lower extremities
  - Blindness in both eyes
  - Loss or loss of use of one lower extremity with residuals of organic disease or injury
- **Allowed Modifications**:
  - Special fixtures for wheelchair accessibility
  - Widened doorways, ramps, accessible bathrooms
  - Lowered countertops, accessible kitchens
  - Elevators, lifts, automation systems
  - New construction or complete renovation

**SHA (Special Housing Adaptation) - Up to $20,929**
- **Eligible Veterans**: Those with permanent and total service-connected disabilities, including:
  - Blindness in both eyes with 5/200 visual acuity or less
  - Loss or loss of use of both hands
  - Severe burn injuries
- **Allowed Modifications**:
  - Accessible bathrooms (roll-in showers, grab bars)
  - Accessible kitchens (lowered counters)
  - Ramps, widened doorways
  - Accessible entry and exit
  - Limited to adaptations, not new construction

**HISA (Home Improvements & Structural Alterations) - Up to $6,976**
- **Eligible Veterans**: Those receiving VA healthcare with a service-connected or non-service-connected disability
- **Allowed Modifications**:
  - Necessary for entering/exiting home
  - Necessary for use of essential facilities (bathroom, kitchen)
  - Grab bars, ramps, accessible entry
  - Bathroom safety modifications
  - Does not cover new construction or additions

### **VA Inspection Requirements**

**Before Construction:**
- VA must approve plans and specifications
- Contractor must be licensed and insured
- Estimates must be submitted to VA
- Timeline must be provided

**During Construction:**
- Work must follow approved plans
- Changes require VA approval (change orders)
- VA may conduct site visits/inspections
- Documentation and photos required

**After Construction:**
- Final VA inspection required
- All work must meet code and VA standards
- Warranty information submitted
- Payment released after final approval

### **Common Compliance Mistakes to Avoid**

‚ùå **Starting work before VA approval** (will not be reimbursed)
‚ùå **Deviating from approved plans** (without change order)
‚ùå **Using non-approved materials** (must meet VA specifications)
‚ùå **Skipping permits** (all work must be permitted and inspected)
‚ùå **Cutting corners to save money** (VA inspections are thorough)
‚ùå **Not documenting changes** (everything must be in writing)

### **Required Documentation**

**You Must Provide:**
- Detailed scope of work
- Itemized cost estimate
- Timeline with milestones
- Materials specifications
- Contractor license and insurance
- Permits (building, electrical, plumbing)
- Change orders (if scope changes)
- Photos (before, during, after)
- Final invoice and warranty

### **Payment Process**

**How Veterans Get Paid by VA:**
1. **Reimbursement Model** (most common):
   - Veteran pays contractor
   - Veteran submits receipts/invoices to VA
   - VA reimburses veteran (up to grant amount)

2. **Direct Payment Model** (less common):
   - VA pays contractor directly
   - Requires pre-approval and agreement
   - More paperwork, slower payment

**Your Payment Terms:**
- Be flexible (veterans may need to pay in phases)
- Consider progress payments tied to VA reimbursement
- Some veterans may need financing (offer options)
- Always use written contracts and receipts

- **VA Form 26-4555**: Application for SAH/SHA grants
