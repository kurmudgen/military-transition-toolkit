[INTRO]
**Cold Open:** C&P exam is number one most important day in VA claim process. Results make or break your disability rating. Difference between thirty percent and seventy percent: fourteen thousand eight hundred dollars annually. Missing exam equals automatic denial. Downplaying symptoms costs veterans thousands per year. Here's how to prepare and maximize your rating.

[MAIN CONTENT]

**What Is a C&P Exam?**

C&P = Compensation and Pension exam. Medical examination conducted by VA or contracted provider (QTC, VES, LHI, others) to evaluate severity of your service-connected disabilities. Examiner completes Disability Benefits Questionnaire (DBQ) for your condition, which VA rater uses to assign disability rating percentage.

**Critical Understanding:** C&P exam does NOT determine service connection (whether condition is military-related). That's determined by evidence in your claim. C&P exam determines SEVERITY (rating percentage: 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, 100%).

**Exception:** For presumptive conditions (PACT Act burn pits, Agent Orange), service connection presumed. C&P exam only determines rating.

**Who Conducts Exam:**
- VA medical professionals at VA facilities
- Contract companies: QTC Medical Services, VES (Veterans Evaluation Services), LHI (Logistics Health Inc)
- Private doctors (sometimes - less common)

You don't choose examiner. VA assigns. If exam scheduled with contractor, it's legitimate - VA uses contractors for capacity.

**When You'll Get C&P Exam:**

- After you file disability claim (if VA needs severity evaluation)
- When you file for rating increase (to see if condition worsened)
- For re-examinations (if VA questions whether condition improved)
- For appeals (if you request new exam due to errors)

**You'll receive letter 2-4 weeks before exam with:**
- Date, time, location of exam
- Examiner/company name
- What condition(s) being evaluated
- Instructions to bring medical records, medications list

**DO NOT MISS THIS EXAM.** Missing C&P exam = automatic claim denial. No exceptions without exceptional circumstances (hospitalization, emergency). Rescheduling difficult - may delay claim months.

**How Long Exam Takes:**

Depends on condition:
- Simple conditions (tinnitus, scar ratings): 15-30 minutes
- Joint/musculoskeletal: 30-60 minutes
- Mental health (PTSD, depression): 1-4 hours (most comprehensive)
- Multiple conditions same day: 1-3 hours total

**What Happens During Exam:**

Examiner follows DBQ (Disability Benefits Questionnaire) - standardized form with specific questions for each condition. Format:
- Review your medical records and claim file
- Ask questions about symptoms, history, daily impact
- Perform physical examination if applicable (range of motion, reflex tests, etc.)
- Observe your behavior and presentation
- Document findings on DBQ form

**DBQ has checkboxes for severity levels matching rating criteria.** Examiner checks boxes describing your condition, which directly translates to rating percentage.

**Example - Knee Condition DBQ:**
- [ ] Slight limitation of motion = 10%
- [ ] Moderate limitation with pain = 20%
- [ ] Severe limitation, instability = 30%+

Examiner's job: Gather information and complete DBQ accurately. Not to approve or deny claim - that's VA rater's job.

**The #1 Critical Rule: Describe Your WORST Days, Not Your Best:**

Military culture: Never show weakness. Push through pain. "I'm fine." "No problem, sir."

**This attitude destroys disability claims.**

VA rater sees only what examiner documents. If you say "my knee hurts sometimes but I can still walk fine," examiner documents "occasional pain, full mobility" = 0-10% rating.

If you say "my knee gives out 2-3 times per week causing falls, I can't run anymore, stairs are painful, I wake up every night with throbbing pain," examiner documents "frequent instability, functional limitations, chronic pain affecting sleep" = 30-50% rating.

**Same knee. Different rating. Difference = how you communicated.**

You're not exaggerating. You're communicating reality. Your worst days ARE your reality. Examiner needs to hear about those days, not the occasional good day when pain is manageable.

**Common Mistake: The "Good Day" Trap:**

Day of exam, you're nervous, adrenaline high, pain temporarily reduced. You walk into exam feeling okay. Examiner asks "How's your back?" You say "Not bad today."

**Disaster.**

Examiner documents "reports feeling 'not bad' at exam." VA rater sees this, assigns low rating.

**Correct Answer:** "Today is actually a better day than usual - I'm probably at 5/10 pain. Most days I'm 7-8/10, and I have 2-3 days per week where pain is 9/10 and I can barely get out of bed. Let me describe my typical week..."

Always provide context. Explain typical symptoms, frequency, severity, even if today you're having better day.

**Before the Exam - Preparation Steps:**

**Step 1 - Document Your Symptoms (2 Weeks Before):**

Keep daily symptom log for 2 weeks before exam. Note:
- What symptoms you experienced each day
- Severity (1-10 scale)
- How symptoms affected activities (couldn't work, couldn't sleep, etc.)
- Medications taken and effectiveness
- Any incidents (falls, panic attacks, flare-ups)

Bring this log to exam. Shows pattern of symptoms, not just single day snapshot.

**Step 2 - Review Rating Criteria for Your Condition:**

Google "[condition] VA rating criteria" or "38 CFR Part 4 [condition]." Understand what VA looks for at each rating level.

**Example - PTSD Rating Criteria:**
- 30%: Mild occupational and social impairment
- 50%: Occupational and social impairment with reduced reliability, productivity
- 70%: Occupational and social impairment with deficiencies in MOST areas

Know criteria. During exam, explain symptoms matching higher rating levels.

**Step 3 - Gather Supporting Documents:**

Bring to exam:
- List of all current medications (names, dosages, frequency)
- Recent medical records (last 6-12 months)
- Emergency room visits or hospitalizations related to condition
- Buddy statements from family/friends describing how condition affects you
- Symptom log from Step 1
- List of doctors treating you for this condition

Examiner may not review all of this, but having it available helpful if questions arise.

**Step 4 - Review Your Medical Records:**

Request and review your Service Treatment Records (STRs), VA medical records, private treatment records. Know what's in them. Be prepared to discuss:
- When condition started (during service or after)
- How it's progressed over time
- Treatments tried and results
- Current status

**Step 5 - Understand Functional Limitations:**

"Functional limitation" = activities you can't do or have difficulty doing because of condition. VA cares more about functional impact than pain alone.

Write list of functional limitations:
- Can't stand more than 20 minutes
- Can't lift more than 10 pounds
- Can't sleep through night
- Can't be in crowded places without anxiety
- Can't focus at work for more than hour without breaks
- Can't maintain friendships due to social withdrawal

Be specific. Quantify when possible. Bring list to exam.

**Step 6 - Practice Describing Your Symptoms:**

Say out loud: "My back pain is 7/10 most days, I can't bend to tie shoes, I wake up 3-4 times nightly, stairs cause sharp shooting pain down left leg, I can't stand more than 15 minutes before needing to sit."

Feels uncomfortable? Good. Military trained you to minimize. Exam is time to speak truth.

**Practice with spouse, friend, or record yourself.** Get comfortable explaining impact of your conditions.

**Day of Exam - What to Do:**

**Arrive 15 Minutes Early:**

Late arrival = canceled appointment = automatic denial. Traffic, parking, finding office takes time. Leave early. Arrive early.

**Dress Appropriately:**

Dress comfortably but don't try to "prove" disability with extreme appearance. Regular clothes fine. If you use assistive devices (cane, brace, etc.), bring and use them - they're evidence of your condition.

**Bring Your Documents:**

- Photo ID
- VA claim number (if you have it)
- Medication list
- Symptom log
- Supporting medical records

**Be Respectful but Honest:**

Examiner is medical professional doing job. Be polite, cooperative, but honest. Don't try to impress them or act tougher than you feel. This isn't boot camp. This is medical evaluation.

**Answer Questions Thoroughly:**

Examiner asks: "How often does your knee give out?"

**Bad answer:** "Sometimes."
**Good answer:** "2-3 times per week on average, usually when going down stairs or standing up from sitting. I've fallen twice in the past month because of it."

**Bad answer:** "It hurts."
**Good answer:** "Sharp, stabbing pain rated 8/10 that shoots from knee down to ankle, especially with weight-bearing. It's constant dull ache at 5/10 baseline, spikes with activity."

Specificity matters. Examiner documents what you say. Vague = low rating. Specific = accurate rating.

**During Physical Exam - Joint/Musculoskeletal:**

For joint conditions (knees, back, shoulders, etc.), examiner tests range of motion using goniometer (angle measuring tool).

**Critical Technique: Stop When Pain Starts:**

Examiner: "Raise your arm as high as you can."

**STOP at point of pain.** Don't push through. Don't try to prove toughness. If arm reaches 90 degrees before sharp pain, stop at 90. If examiner says "try to go further," say "I can force it but it causes severe pain."

Military mindset: Push through pain. VA exam mindset: Stop at pain. Pain-limited range of motion = higher rating. Full range of motion through pain = lower rating.

**Ensure Examiner Uses Goniometer:**

If examiner eyeballs your range of motion without measuring tool, that's error. Politely say: "Will you be measuring with a goniometer?" If they refuse or say unnecessary, note this. Grounds for requesting new exam later.

**Test WORST Condition First:**

If testing multiple joints, request worst joint tested first. Reason: You're fresh, not tired. Worst joint gets accurate assessment. If you do 10 range-of-motion tests before worst joint, you're fatigued, adrenaline kicked in, pain might temporarily reduce. Test worst first.

**Mental Health C&P Exams:**

Longest exams (1-4 hours). Most comprehensive. Most important to prepare for.

**What Examiner Evaluates:**

- Stressor events (combat, trauma, incidents)
- Current symptoms (flashbacks, nightmares, hypervigilance, avoidance, depression, anxiety, anger)
- Frequency and severity of symptoms
- How symptoms affect work, relationships, social life, daily functioning
- Treatment history (medications, therapy, hospitalizations)
- Coping mechanisms
- Suicidal ideation (if applicable - be honest, this is protected information for rating purposes)

**Be Vulnerable:**

Mental health exams require emotional vulnerability. Describe worst moments:
- Panic attacks in crowded stores
- Nightmares every night waking you up
- Avoiding family gatherings because you can't be around people
- Anger outbursts that damage relationships
- Inability to hold jobs
- Alcohol use to cope (be honest - examiner needs to know)

This is hardest part for veterans. Admitting mental health struggles feels like weakness. **It's not.** It's medical reality. Examiner can't rate what they don't know about.

**Bring Support Person:**

For mental health exams, you can bring spouse, family member, friend. They can provide additional perspective on how your symptoms affect daily life. Examiners sometimes interview support person separately.

**Example:** You say your PTSD causes irritability. Spouse says "He's had three road rage incidents, punched a hole in wall during argument, kids are afraid of him when he's angry." Different picture. Higher rating.

**What Examiner Documents:**

Examiner completes DBQ noting:
- Your appearance and behavior during exam
- Symptoms you reported
- Frequency/severity of symptoms
- Functional limitations
- Treatment response
- Overall assessment of occupational and social impairment

**For Mental Health DBQ, key assessment:**
- Occupational impairment (ability to work)
- Social impairment (ability to maintain relationships)
- Deficiencies in areas like work, school, family relations, thinking, mood

Examiner checks boxes corresponding to rating criteria:
- Mild impairment = 30-50%
- Moderate impairment = 50-70%
- Severe impairment with deficiencies in most areas = 70-100%

Your job during exam: Provide evidence of severity matching higher rating criteria.

**After the Exam:**

**You Won't Know Rating Immediately:**

Examiner doesn't tell you rating. They complete DBQ and send to VA. VA rater reviews DBQ plus all other evidence and assigns rating.

**Request Copy of DBQ:**

You have right to see completed DBQ. After exam, ask examiner or company: "How do I request copy of DBQ?" Or request through VA after decision. Reviewing DBQ shows what examiner documented. If errors, you can address in appeal.

**Document Exam Experience:**

Immediately after leaving exam, write notes:
- What questions examiner asked
- How you answered
- What physical tests performed
- Any concerns (examiner seemed rushed, didn't use goniometer, interrupted you, etc.)
- What you forgot to mention

If you get low rating later and believe exam was flawed, these notes help request new exam.

**Common C&P Exam Mistakes Veterans Make:**

**Mistake 1: Minimizing Symptoms (Most Common):**

"I'm fine. Just a little pain." = 0-10% rating. Be honest about severity. This is medical exam, not job interview.

**Mistake 2: Not Preparing:**

Walking in without reviewing symptoms, medical records, or rating criteria = forgetting important details, incomplete picture to examiner.

**Mistake 3: Rushing Through Answers:**

Examiner asks "How's your sleep?" You say "Not great." That's it. Examiner documents "reports poor sleep."

**Better:** "I wake up 4-5 times every night from pain, take 1-2 hours to fall asleep initially, average 3-4 hours total sleep per night, exhausted all day, affects my ability to focus at work."

Take your time. Answer thoroughly.

**Mistake 4: Forgetting Secondary Symptoms:**

Claiming back pain, but you don't mention the leg numbness, erectile dysfunction, or depression caused by chronic pain. Examiner doesn't know to ask. You miss additional ratings.

Before exam, list ALL symptoms related to condition - direct and secondary.

**Mistake 5: Trying to Prove You're Disabled:**

Exaggerating, acting more impaired than reality, being theatrical. Examiners trained to spot this. Destroys credibility.

Opposite extreme also problem: Acting tougher than reality. Middle ground: Honest, accurate description of symptoms and limitations.

**Mistake 6: Not Mentioning Medications and Treatment:**

Some veterans think "If I mention medication, examiner will think I'm fine." Wrong. Medications are evidence of condition severity. Fact you need medication to function = disability, not proof you're healthy.

List all medications, dosages, frequency. Explain if medications help, don't help, or have side effects.

**Mistake 7: Accepting Bad Exam:**

Examiner was rushed, didn't perform physical exam, only reviewed records for 5 minutes, dismissed your symptoms. You leave thinking "that seemed wrong" but do nothing.

**You can request new exam** if you document specific errors. More on this below.

**Red Flags - Bad C&P Exams:**

**Chart Review Only:**

Examiner reviews your records but doesn't conduct physical examination or ask questions. For physical conditions requiring assessment (range of motion, reflexes, etc.), chart review alone insufficient. You can challenge this.

**Examiner Dismissive or Hostile:**

Makes comments like "Everyone has back pain," "That's not that bad," "You look fine to me." Unprofessional. Document and report.

**Exam Rushed:**

Mental health exam finished in 15 minutes. Joint exam with no range-of-motion testing. Examiner clearly going through motions. Document time, what was/wasn't done.

**Examiner Lacks Specialty:**

General practitioner conducting complex PTSD exam. Orthopedic condition evaluated by internist without musculoskeletal expertise. VA supposed to match examiner specialty to condition. Not always happens.

**Examiner Applies Wrong Standard:**

For service connection opinions, standard is "at least as likely as not" (50/50). Some examiners incorrectly apply "more likely than not" (>50%), making it harder. If examiner states opinion on service connection and uses wrong language, note this.

**Wrong DBQ Completed:**

You're there for knee exam. Examiner completes ankle DBQ. Sounds ridiculous, but happens. Verify at end of exam that correct condition was evaluated.

**If Bad Exam Occurs - How to Challenge:**

**Step 1 - Document Issues:**

Write detailed statement immediately after exam describing what was wrong:
- Examiner didn't perform physical exam
- No range-of-motion measurement
- Examiner dismissed symptoms
- Wrong specialty examiner
- Rushed exam (provide time duration)
- Specific statements examiner made that were inappropriate

**Step 2 - Submit Statement:**

Send statement to VA Regional Office handling your claim. Subject: "Request for New C&P Examination Due to Inadequate Initial Exam."

Explain specific deficiencies and request VA order new exam with different examiner.

**Step 3 - If Claim Decided Based on Bad Exam:**

File Higher-Level Review or Supplemental Claim specifically citing inadequate C&P exam. Include your documentation of exam issues. VA will review and may order new exam.

**Step 4 - Contact Patient Advocate (If Exam at VA Facility):**

Every VA facility has Patient Advocate. You can file complaint about substandard exam. Advocate investigates and can trigger corrective action.

**Requesting New Exam:**

You generally can't just request new exam because you think you deserved higher rating. But you CAN request new exam if:
- Examiner made specific factual errors in DBQ
- Examiner didn't conduct required portions of exam
- Examiner lacked proper specialty
- You were unable to articulate symptoms properly due to mental health condition (and now want second chance)
- New medical evidence shows condition worse than DBQ documented

**Telehealth C&P Exams:**

VA increasingly offering telehealth (video) C&P exams for certain conditions, especially mental health. Pros and cons.

**Pros:**
- Convenient (no travel)
- Sometimes less stressful in your home environment
- Easier for those with mobility issues

**Cons:**
- Can't do physical exam (range of motion, etc.)
- Technical issues can disrupt exam
- Some veterans communicate better in person

You can generally request in-person if telehealth offered and you prefer in-person. Don't accept telehealth for physical conditions requiring examination.

**Final Preparation Checklist:**

**2 Weeks Before:**
- [ ] Request medical records if you don't have them
- [ ] Start symptom log (daily documentation)
- [ ] Research rating criteria for your condition
- [ ] Write list of functional limitations

**1 Week Before:**
- [ ] Review symptom log, identify patterns
- [ ] Compile medication list
- [ ] Practice describing symptoms out loud
- [ ] Gather all documents to bring

**Day Before:**
- [ ] Confirm exam time and location
- [ ] Plan route, parking
- [ ] Prepare documents in folder to bring
- [ ] Get good sleep (if possible - note if insomnia prevents this, that's evidence)

**Day Of:**
- [ ] Arrive 15 minutes early
- [ ] Bring ID, documents, medication list, symptom log
- [ ] Stay calm but honest
- [ ] Answer all questions thoroughly
- [ ] Describe worst days, not best days
- [ ] Stop at pain during physical tests
- [ ] Document exam experience immediately after

**Remember:** C&P exam is YOUR opportunity to show VA severity of your disabilities. Preparation and honest communication = accurate rating = fair compensation for your service-connected conditions.
