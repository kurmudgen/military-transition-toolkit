[MAIN CONTENT]

**What is GI Bill Transferability?**

The Post-9/11 GI Bill provides education benefits to veterans - covering tuition, fees, books, and housing while you attend college or pursue approved training. Transferability is the ability to transfer some or all of your unused benefits to your spouse or children.

**Why this matters:** If you're not planning to use your GI Bill, or you won't use all 36 months, transferring it to family members ensures those benefits don't go to waste. A college degree that might cost your family $80,000-$150,000 can instead cost zero or close to it using transferred GI Bill benefits.

**Important clarification:** Only the Post-9/11 GI Bill (Chapter 33) is transferable. If you have Montgomery GI Bill (MGIB) or other education benefits, those are NOT transferable to dependents. When people talk about "transferring the GI Bill," they're always referring to Post-9/11 GI Bill.

**The Service Requirements - Can You Transfer?**

Not everyone can transfer GI Bill benefits. You must meet specific service requirements.

**Requirement #1 - Six years completed:** You must have completed at least 6 years of active duty or Selected Reserve service as of the date you request the transfer. This is total time in service, not necessarily continuous.

**Requirement #2 - Four-year service obligation:** You must agree to serve an additional 4 years in the Armed Forces from the date your request is approved. This creates a total of 10 years of service (6 already completed + 4 more committed).

**How the 4-year obligation works:**
- If you have 4 or more years remaining on your current contract when you request transfer, you don't need to extend or re-enlist. Your existing contract satisfies the obligation.
- If you have less than 4 years remaining, you must extend your enlistment or re-enlist to add enough time to meet the 4-year requirement.

**Example:** You have 7 years of service and 3 years left on your contract. To transfer benefits, you must extend or re-enlist for at least 1 additional year (bringing your remaining obligation to 4 years total).

**Exception - Purple Heart recipients:** If you received a Purple Heart, you don't need to meet any service requirement. You can transfer benefits without the 6-year completion or 4-year obligation. But you still must request the transfer while on active duty.

**Requirement #3 - Still in service:** You MUST request the transfer through milConnect while you're still on active duty or in the Selected Reserve. Once you separate or retire, you lose the ability to transfer. There's no grace period. Once you're out, you're out.

**This is the mistake that costs people:** Service members plan to transfer their GI Bill "after they get out." They separate. Then they learn you can't transfer once you're separated. The benefit is lost.

**Who Can Receive Transferred Benefits?**

You can transfer benefits to eligible family members.

**Eligible dependents:**
- Your spouse
- Your biological children
- Your adopted children
- Your stepchildren (if dependent on you)
- Your foster children
- Your unmarried legal wards (if they've been your legal ward for at least 12 consecutive months)

**DEERS enrollment requirement:** All eligible dependents MUST be enrolled in the Defense Enrollment Eligibility Reporting System (DEERS) before you can transfer benefits to them. If they're not in DEERS, get them enrolled immediately. Most spouses and children are already in DEERS from day one, but verify.

**Age restrictions for children:** On the date you submit your transfer request, children must be:
- Under age 21, OR
- Age 21-22 AND enrolled full-time in a degree-granting postsecondary institution, OR
- Age 21-22 AND incapable of self-support due to mental or physical disability that existed before age 18

Most cases are straightforward - your minor children qualify. If they're 21 or older when you request transfer, they need to be in college or have a qualifying disability.

**How Many Months Can You Transfer?**

You have up to 36 months of Post-9/11 GI Bill benefits (assuming you qualify for 100% benefits based on service length). You can transfer however you want within that 36-month limit.

**Option A - Transfer all months to one person:** Give all 36 months to your spouse. Or all 36 to one child. They get the full benefit.

**Option B - Split among multiple dependents:** Give 18 months to your spouse and 9 months each to two children. Or any combination that totals 36 or less.

**Option C - Use some yourself, transfer the rest:** Use 12 months for your own degree, transfer the remaining 24 months to your children.

**You decide the allocation.** You can revoke or modify transferred months that haven't been used yet. Once a family member starts using benefits, those months become theirs and you can't take them back. But unused months remain flexible.

**Strategic consideration:** Many service members transfer all 36 months to their spouse initially, then later revoke some and reallocate to children as the children get closer to college age. This preserves flexibility. Your spouse doesn't actually lose anything until they start using benefits.

**When Can Dependents Use Transferred Benefits?**

This is where the rules differ significantly between spouses and children.

**Spouses can use transferred benefits immediately** after your transfer request is approved. Whether you're still on active duty or whether you've separated doesn't matter. Once approved, your spouse can start using benefits right away.

**Children face additional restrictions:**
- They can only use transferred benefits AFTER you've completed at least 10 years of service
- They must be at least 18 years old OR have a high school diploma or GED
- They must use all benefits before they turn 26

**Example:** You have 8 years of service and 2 children ages 12 and 14. You transfer 18 months to each child now (while you're still in). They can't use the benefits yet because you haven't reached 10 years of service. Once you hit 10 years, and once each child turns 18 or graduates high school, they can start using benefits.

**The age 26 deadline for children:** Unlike spouses who have 15 years from separation to use benefits, children have a hard stop at age 26. If your child hasn't used all their months by age 26, the remaining months are lost. Plan accordingly - if you're transferring benefits to a 24-year-old, they only have 2 years to use them.

**What Transferred Benefits Cover**

When your dependents use transferred GI Bill benefits, here's what they receive:

**Tuition and fees:**
- Public schools: Full coverage up to the state's highest in-state tuition rate
- Private/foreign schools: Up to $28,937.09 per academic year (August 2024 - July 2025)
- For 2025-2026, the private school cap increases to approximately $29,920

**Books and supplies:** Up to $1,000 per academic year. Paid directly to the student.

**Monthly Housing Allowance (MHA):** This is where it gets complicated because the rules differ for spouses versus children, and whether you're still on active duty.

**For CHILDREN:**
- Children always receive the monthly housing allowance
- Amount equals the Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH) rate for an E-5 with dependents at the school's ZIP code
- Averages $1,600-$2,400/month depending on location
- For online-only courses, capped at $1,169/month (half the national average)

**For SPOUSES:**
- If you're still on active duty when your spouse uses benefits, they receive NO monthly housing allowance
- If you've separated from service, your spouse receives the full monthly housing allowance
- Amount same as for children - E-5 with dependents BAH rate

**Why the spouse restriction during active duty?** DoD assumes if you're active duty, your spouse is living with you or in military housing or receiving BAH through your pay. They don't pay housing allowance twice. Once you separate, your spouse no longer benefits from your housing situation, so they get MHA.

**The value calculation:**

4-year degree at public university, veteran separated, child using benefits:
- Tuition: $10,000/year × 4 = $40,000
- Books: $1,000/year × 4 = $4,000
- MHA: $2,000/month × 9 months/year × 4 = $72,000
- Total: $116,000 in benefits

That's the value you're transferring. At private schools, it could exceed $150,000 over 4 years.

**How to Transfer - The milConnect Process**

Let's walk through the actual steps to transfer your benefits.

**Step One - Verify eligibility:**

Before you start, confirm:
- You have 6+ years of service completed (or Purple Heart)
- You can commit to 4 more years (or already have 4+ years on contract)
- You're currently on active duty or in Selected Reserve
- Your dependents are enrolled in DEERS

**Step Two - Log into milConnect:**

Go to https://milconnect.dmdc.osd.mil/milconnect/

Log in using your Common Access Card (CAC), DS Logon, or DFAS PIN.

**Step Three - Navigate to Transfer of Education Benefits:**

Click the "Benefits" tab at the top of the page. Select "Transfer of Education Benefits (TEB)" from the menu.

**Step Four - Read and accept service commitment:**

The system will display your service commitment requirement (usually 4 additional years unless you already have 4+ on your contract). Read carefully and accept the obligation.

**Step Five - Add dependents:**

Click "Add Dependent." Select each family member you want to transfer benefits to from your DEERS list. If a family member doesn't appear, they're not in DEERS - go add them first.

**Step Six - Allocate months:**

For each dependent, enter the number of months you're transferring. You can allocate 1-36 months per person, as long as the total doesn't exceed your available months.

**Step Seven - Submit request:**

Review everything carefully. Once satisfied, click "Submit Request." You'll receive a confirmation that your request was submitted.

**Step Eight - Wait for approval:**

Your branch of service reviews and approves transfer requests. This typically takes 4-8 weeks. Check your milConnect TEB page periodically for status updates.

**Step Nine - Notify family members:**

Once your request shows "Approved" status, notify your family members. They now need to apply for their benefits through VA.

**Step Ten - Family members apply to VA:**

Each dependent who received transferred benefits must complete VA Form 22-1990E (Application for Family Member to Use Transferred Benefits). They file this online at VA.gov. VA will issue them a Certificate of Eligibility showing how many months of benefits they have.

**Important timing note:** Request the transfer as early as you can once you hit 6 years of service. Don't wait until you're about to separate. Process it years ahead if possible. You can always revoke and reallocate later, but you can't transfer at all once you're out.

**Common Transfer Mistakes**

**Mistake #1: Waiting until separation to transfer.**

The single biggest mistake. You cannot transfer after you separate. It must be done while in service. Thousands of service members learn this too late.

**Mistake #2: Transferring to children who won't use it before age 26.**

You transfer benefits to your 24-year-old who's not in school and doesn't plan to go. They turn 26 with unused benefits. Those months are wasted. Transfer to dependents who will actually use the benefits within the allowed timeframe.

**Mistake #3: Not understanding the spouse housing allowance restriction.**

You transfer benefits to your spouse, expecting them to get $2,000/month housing allowance while you're still on active duty. They don't get it because you're active duty. This creates financial surprise. Plan accordingly.

**Mistake #4: Forgetting the 10-year requirement for children.**

You have 8 years of service, you transfer benefits to your kids, then you separate at 10 years. Your kids can't use benefits yet because you hadn't reached 10 years when they needed it. Wait until after you hit 10 years to transfer to children, or ensure you'll complete 10 years before separating.

**Mistake #5: Not keeping transfer allocations updated.**

Your family situation changes - divorce, remarriage, kids age out. Update your TEB allocations in milConnect to reflect current reality. Don't leave 36 months allocated to an ex-spouse or a child who's now 27.

**Mistake #6: Assuming children have 15 years to use benefits like veterans do.**

Veterans have 15 years from separation to use their Post-9/11 GI Bill. Children only have until age 26 regardless of when they received the transfer. Don't confuse the two timelines.

**Strategic Decisions About Transferring**

Should you transfer your GI Bill? To whom? How many months? Here's a framework for thinking through these decisions.

**Decision #1: Will you use it yourself?**

If you're 100% certain you'll use all 36 months for your own education, don't transfer. Use your benefits. But most service members overestimate how much they'll use. If there's any chance you won't use all 36 months, transfer some amount to preserve the value.

**Decision #2: Spouse vs. children - who needs it more?**

**Transfer to spouse if:**
- Your spouse wants to complete or advance their education
- Your children are already through college or on scholarships
- Your spouse's career would benefit significantly from a degree
- You want your spouse to be able to use benefits immediately (no 10-year wait)

**Transfer to children if:**
- Your spouse has a degree or doesn't want to pursue education
- Your children will be college-age within the next few years
- You want to avoid the "no housing allowance while on active duty" spouse restriction
- College costs for children are a major family financial concern

**Many families split:** 18 months to spouse for a master's degree, 9 months each to two children. Everyone gets something.

**Decision #3: Transfer now or wait?**

Once you have 6 years and can commit to 4 more, you're eligible to transfer. But should you do it immediately or wait?

**Transfer early if:**
- You're certain about your family situation
- Your dependents will use benefits soon
- You want to lock it in and not risk forgetting

**Wait if:**
- Family situation is uncertain (potential divorce, children's education plans unclear)
- You might use more benefits yourself than you currently think
- You haven't decided on allocations

**But don't wait past your re-enlistment window.** If you're approaching separation and you haven't transferred, do it NOW. Don't risk losing the ability.

**Transfer vs. Not Transferring - The Money**

Let's be blunt about the financial value of this decision.

**Scenario 1: Service member with 8 years in, not planning to use GI Bill, 2 children ages 10 and 12.**

Transfers 18 months to each child now. Each child uses benefits for 2 years of college.

Value per child: $30,000 tuition + $2,000 books + $36,000 MHA = $68,000
Total for both children: $136,000 in benefits used

Alternative if not transferred: $0. The service member separates at 12 years, benefits unused, they expire. Children pay full price for college.

**Net family value of transferring: $136,000.**

**Scenario 2: Service member transfers 36 months to spouse. Spouse completes bachelor's degree after service member separates.**

Value: $40,000 tuition + $4,000 books + $72,000 MHA = $116,000

Alternative if spouse pays out-of-pocket: Student loans, years of payments, interest.

**The transfer is worth six figures.** This isn't a small benefit. This is one of the most valuable things you can do for your family's financial future.

**What If You're Approaching Separation?**

If you're within a year of separating and you haven't transferred your GI Bill yet, here's your action plan:

**Step 1: Decide NOW if you'll use any benefits yourself.** If yes, keep those months. If no, transfer them all.

**Step 2: Can you commit to 4 more years?** If you're within 4 years of separating, you may not be able to meet the service commitment for transfer. Check with your retention NCO or career counselor. If you can't extend/re-enlist, you can't transfer.

**Step 3: If you can transfer, do it immediately.** Don't wait another week. Log into milConnect today. Process the request. It takes 6-8 weeks for approval. Do it now.

**Step 4: If you can't transfer because you're too close to separation, accept that those benefits will expire.** You can still use them yourself if you act quickly. But you can't transfer once you're out.

**This is why early planning matters.** Don't be the person who learns about transferability a month before terminal leave starts.
