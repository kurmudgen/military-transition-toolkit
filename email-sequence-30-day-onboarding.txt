EMAIL 19: C&P Exam Prep: What to Actually Expect
Send: Day 29 at 11:00 AM
Subject Line: Your C&P exam: What they don't tell you
Preview Text: Preparation guide for VA compensation exams...
From: Jacob at Military Transition Toolkit

Body:

If you've filed a VA claim, you'll eventually get a C&P exam.

This is where most veterans screw up their rating.

**Common mistakes:**

**Downplaying symptoms:** "My knee bothers me sometimes."
(That sounds like 0%. Tell them your worst days, not your average days.)

**Forgetting details:** "I hurt it in 2015... I think?"
(Know your dates. Know your treatments. Bring documentation.)

**Trying to tough it out:** "It's not that bad, I can still function."
(This isn't a fitness test. Be honest about your limitations.)

**Here's what actually happens in a C&P exam:**

1. Examiner reviews your file (5-10 minutes)
2. They ask about your symptoms (15-20 minutes)
3. They do a physical exam (10-15 minutes)
4. They write up their findings (this determines your rating)

**What they're looking for:**

- Frequency (how often does it bother you?)
- Severity (how bad is it when it flares up?)
- Duration (how long have you had this?)
- Impact (what can't you do because of this?)

**Your job:** Give them ammunition to grant your claim.

Describe your worst days. Explain the real impact on your life. Bring documentation. Don't minimize.

EMAIL 21: Interview Prep 101: First Impressions
Send: Day 32 at 10:00 AM
Subject Line: You get 7 seconds to make an impression
Preview Text: What civilian interviewers judge in the first minute...
From: Jacob at Military Transition Toolkit

Body:

Military interviews: "What's your rank? When can you start? Welcome aboard."

Civilian interviews: Completely different game.

You get 7 seconds before they form a first impression. By the end of the first minute, they've already decided if you're a maybe or a no.

**What they're judging:**

**Body language:** Did you shake hands confidently? Make eye contact? Sit up straight?
(Military habits help here. Use them.)

**Energy level:** Are you engaged and enthusiastic, or just going through the motions?
(They can tell if you actually want the job.)

**Communication:** Can you speak conversationally, or do you sound like you're giving a brief?
(Drop the military formality. This isn't a formation.)

**Here's what works:**

Walk in with confidence (not cockiness). Smile. Give a firm handshake. Make eye contact when you introduce yourself.

When they ask "How are you?" actually answer like a human: "I'm doing great, thanks for having me. I've been looking forward to this conversation."

Not: "Good, sir/ma'am."

EMAIL 22: Salary Negotiation: What You're Worth
Send: Day 35 at 1:00 PM
Subject Line: You're worth more than they offered
Preview Text: How to negotiate your first civilian salary...
From: Jacob at Military Transition Toolkit

Body:

They made you an offer. Congrats.

Now don't accept it immediately.

**"But I'm grateful just to get an offer!"**

I get it. But gratitude and negotiation aren't mutually exclusive. You can be thankful for the opportunity and still ask for more money.

Because here's the truth: They expect you to negotiate.

If they offer $65K and you accept immediately, they think you would've taken $60K. If you counter at $72K and settle at $68K, they respect you more.

**How to negotiate:**

1. **Always ask for more than they offered**
"I really appreciate the offer. Based on my research and experience, I was hoping for something closer to [10-15% more]. Is there flexibility there?"

2. **Use data, not feelings**
"Looking at salary data for this role in [city], the range seems to be $65K-$80K. Given my [specific experience], I think $72K is fair."

3. **Negotiate total comp, not just salary**
If they won't budge on salary, ask about:
- Sign-on bonus
- Extra vacation days
- Remote work flexibility
- Professional development budget
- Earlier performance review

4. **Always be willing to walk away**
If you're not, they'll sense it. Have backup options.

The worst they can say is no. And even then, you're no worse off than before you asked.

EMAIL 27: Healthcare After Military: Don't Wing It
Send: Day 50 at 2:00 PM
Subject Line: Healthcare in 3 months: Are you ready?
Preview Text: Transition from Tricare to civilian insurance...
From: Jacob at Military Transition Toolkit

Body:

Your Tricare ends the day you separate.

Got a plan after that?

**Here are your options:**

**1. VA Healthcare** (Free if service-connected)
- Enroll within 1 year of separation for easiest approval
- Priority groups based on disability rating
- Good for service-connected issues, not great for everything else

**2. Employer Insurance** (Varies widely)
- Starts after your first day (usually)
- Check costs: premiums, deductibles, out-of-pocket max
- Compare plans if multiple options

**3. Marketplace/ACA** (If employer doesn't offer)
- Healthcare.gov or state exchanges
- Income-based subsidies available
- Not as expensive as you think

**4. COBRA** (Bridge option)
- Extends your current insurance 18 months
- Expensive (you pay full premium + admin fee)
- Good for short gaps

**Don't forget:**

- Get your medical records before you separate
- Schedule any final appointments while on Tricare
- Fill prescriptions for 90-day supplies
- Register with VA even if you don't plan to use it

Healthcare gaps are expensive. Plan ahead.
