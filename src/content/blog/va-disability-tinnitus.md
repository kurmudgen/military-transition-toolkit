---# VA Disability Rating Guide: Tinnitus

## Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

**Possible Rating:** 10% (flat rate)

**Evidence Needed:**
- Current diagnosis of tinnitus
- Audiological examination confirming tinnitus
- Service connection proof (noise exposure, head injury, etc.)
- Nexus linking tinnitus to military service
- Personal statement describing impact on daily life

**Monthly Compensation (2025 rates, veteran without dependents):**
- 10%: $171.23

**Lifetime Value:** $82,190 (over 40 years)

**Key Facts:**
- Tinnitus has only ONE rating level: 10%
- No higher rating available regardless of severity
- One of the most commonly service-connected conditions
- Often filed with hearing loss
- Can be secondary to other conditions

## Understanding Tinnitus VA Ratings

Tinnitus is rated under 38 CFR § 4.87a, Diagnostic Code 6260. Unlike most conditions, tinnitus has only a single rating level: 10%, regardless of severity.

### Rating Criteria

#### 10% Rating (Only Rating Available)
**Criteria:** Recurrent tinnitus.

**What Qualifies:**
- Ringing, buzzing, hissing, roaring, or other sounds in ears
- Recurrent (happens regularly, not just once)
- Present even if intermittent
- Any severity level (mild to severe)

**What This Means:**
- Whether your tinnitus is mild, moderate, or severe, the rating is 10%
- Whether it's in one ear or both ears, the rating is 10%
- Whether it's constant or intermittent, the rating is 10%
- No matter how debilitating, the maximum rating is 10%

**Monthly Payment:** $171.23

**Important:** While 10% may seem low, especially for severe tinnitus, this is a straightforward claim that many veterans successfully obtain. Additionally, tinnitus often comes with hearing loss, which IS rated based on severity.

## What Is Tinnitus?

**Tinnitus** is the perception of sound when no external sound is present. Common descriptions:

**Types of Sounds:**
- Ringing
- Buzzing
- Hissing
- Roaring
- Clicking
- Whistling
- Humming
- Static-like noise

**Characteristics:**
- Can be in one ear, both ears, or "in the head"
- Can be constant or intermittent
- Can vary in volume and pitch
- May worsen with stress, fatigue, or certain triggers
- May be pulsatile (rhythmic, matching heartbeat)

**Impact on Life:**
- Difficulty concentrating
- Sleep disturbance
- Difficulty hearing conversations
- Anxiety or depression
- Social withdrawal
- Difficulty working in quiet environments
- Reading/studying difficulties

**Note:** Even though tinnitus impact varies widely, the VA rating remains 10% for all cases.

## Evidence Requirements

### 1. Audiological Examination

**Required:**
- Examination by audiologist
- Hearing test (audiogram)
- Tinnitus assessment confirming presence of tinnitus

**What Audiologist Documents:**
- Presence of tinnitus
- Which ear(s) affected
- Description of sound
- Pitch matching (if done)
- Loudness matching (if done)

**Where to Get:**
- VA Audiology clinic
- Private audiologist
- Both are acceptable evidence

**Important:** You don't need to "prove" the severity. The audiologist simply confirms you have recurrent tinnitus.

### 2. Medical Diagnosis

**Documentation Needed:**
- Medical records stating tinnitus diagnosis
- Audiology report
- ENT (Ear, Nose, Throat) evaluation (helpful but not required)

**Related Conditions Often Evaluated:**
- Hearing loss (if present)
- Ear infections
- Meniere's disease
- Acoustic neuroma (rare, usually ruled out with imaging)
- Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) disorder

### 3. Service Connection Proof

Tinnitus can be service-connected through several pathways:

**A. Noise Exposure During Service (Most Common):**

**Common Military Noise Exposures:**
- Weapons fire (rifles, machine guns, artillery)
- Aircraft noise (flight deck, flight engineer, pilot, mechanic)
- Explosions (IED, RPG, combat)
- Heavy equipment (tanks, vehicles, generators)
- Ships (engine rooms, deck operations)
- Training ranges

**Evidence:**
- Military Occupational Specialty (MOS) involving noise
- Deployment to combat zones
- Service records showing noise exposure
- Buddy statements about noise exposure
- DD Form 2215 (Hearing Conservation Records) if available

**Common High-Noise MOSs:**
- Infantry (weapons fire)
- Artillery
- Aviation (all specialties)
- Combat engineers
- Armor/cavalry
- Navy (especially engineering, deck operations)
- Any combat MOS

**B. Direct Service Connection:**
- Tinnitus noted in service medical records
- Tinnitus diagnosed during service
- Continuity from service to present

**C. Secondary Service Connection:**
Tinnitus secondary to another service-connected condition:

**Common Primary Conditions:**
- **Hearing loss:** Tinnitus and hearing loss often occur together
- **TBI (Traumatic Brain Injury):** Head injuries commonly cause tinnitus
- **PTSD:** Stress and hyperarousal worsen tinnitus
- **TMJ disorder:** Jaw problems can cause tinnitus
- **Meniere's disease:** Inner ear disorder causes tinnitus and hearing loss
- **Acoustic trauma from service-connected event**
- **Medications for service-connected conditions** (ototoxic medications)

**D. Presumptive Service Connection:**
- For certain veterans with specific exposures, tinnitus may be presumptive
- Gulf War veterans with chronic unexplained tinnitus

### 4. Nexus Opinion

Medical opinion linking your tinnitus to military service or a service-connected condition.

**Nexus Statement Examples:**

**For Noise Exposure:**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's tinnitus is related to military noise exposure. The veteran served as an infantryman with documented exposure to weapons fire and explosions. Such noise exposure is a well-established cause of tinnitus. The temporal relationship supports service connection."

**For Secondary to TBI:**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's tinnitus is secondary to service-connected traumatic brain injury. Tinnitus is a common sequela of TBI, often resulting from damage to auditory pathways or the auditory cortex. The veteran's tinnitus began shortly after the TBI event, supporting this connection."

**For Secondary to PTSD:**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's tinnitus is aggravated by service-connected PTSD. While the etiology may be multifactorial, PTSD-related hyperarousal, stress, and hypervigilance are known to significantly worsen tinnitus perception. Treatment of PTSD should include consideration of tinnitus management."

## C&P Examination Preparation

### Before the Exam

**1. Prepare Description of Tinnitus:**
- What it sounds like (ringing, buzzing, etc.)
- Which ear(s)
- Constant or intermittent
- When it started
- What makes it worse
- What makes it better (if anything)
- How it affects your life

**2. Document Functional Impact:**
Even though severity doesn't change the rating, document impact for the record:
- Sleep disturbance
- Concentration difficulties
- Social situations affected
- Work impact
- Emotional impact (anxiety, frustration)

**3. Gather Evidence:**
- Any service records showing noise exposure
- MOS description
- Deployment records
- Previous hearing tests
- Buddy statements
- Personal noise exposure timeline

**4. List Service Noise Exposures:**
Write down specific exposures:
- Weapons fired (types, frequency)
- Explosions experienced
- Aircraft exposure
- Equipment operation
- Any other loud noise exposure

### During the Exam

**Audiological Exam:**
- Hearing test (audiogram) in soundproof booth
- Tinnitus assessment - confirm you have ringing/buzzing in ears
- Answer questions honestly
- Describe the sound, location, frequency

**Examiner Will Ask:**
- Do you experience ringing, buzzing, or other sounds in your ears?
- Is it in one ear or both?
- What does it sound like?
- Is it constant or does it come and go?
- When did it start?
- How does it affect you?

**DO:**
- Describe your tinnitus clearly
- Be honest about when it started
- Explain connection to military service (noise exposure, TBI, etc.)
- Describe functional impact
- Mention if it's worsened over time

**DON'T:**
- Exaggerate (not necessary - 10% rating regardless of severity)
- Minimize symptoms either
- Forget to mention service noise exposure
- Fail to connect it to service or service-connected condition

**Important:** The examiner will confirm presence of tinnitus. They're not rating severity since the rating is flat 10% regardless.

## Claim Strategy

### Initial Claim Strategy

**Step 1: Get Audiological Evaluation**
- Schedule hearing test with audiologist
- Ensure tinnitus is documented
- Get copy of results

**Step 2: Identify Service Connection Path**
- **Noise exposure:** Most common - document your MOS and exposures
- **Secondary:** Identify service-connected condition causing tinnitus (TBI, PTSD, hearing loss, TMJ, etc.)
- **Direct:** If tinnitus noted in service records

**Step 3: Gather Service Evidence**
- Review your DD-214 for MOS
- Any service medical records mentioning hearing/tinnitus
- Deployment records showing combat exposure
- Buddy statements about noise exposure

**Step 4: Obtain Nexus**
- Audiologist or ENT can provide nexus
- Primary care provider can provide nexus
- Independent Medical Opinion (IMO) if needed
- For noise exposure, sometimes straightforward based on MOS

**Step 5: File Comprehensively**
Submit:
- VA Form 21-526EZ
- Audiology report confirming tinnitus
- Service records showing noise exposure (or evidence of primary condition if secondary claim)
- Nexus letter
- Personal statement describing tinnitus and connection to service
- Buddy statements (if helpful)

### Filing with Hearing Loss

**Many veterans have both tinnitus and hearing loss.**

**File BOTH Conditions:**
- Tinnitus: 10% (flat rate)
- Hearing Loss: 0-100% based on severity

**They are rated separately and combine.**

**Example:**
- Tinnitus: 10%
- Hearing Loss: 10%
- **Combined rating: 19% (paid at 20%)**

**Strategy:**
- Always get full audiological evaluation testing both tinnitus AND hearing
- File for both conditions if both present
- Both can be connected to same noise exposure

### Secondary Claim Strategy

**Tinnitus Secondary to TBI:**

Very common. Many TBI survivors develop tinnitus.

**Evidence:**
1. Service-connected TBI
2. Audiological exam confirming tinnitus
3. Nexus: Tinnitus developed after TBI, common TBI sequela
4. Timeline showing tinnitus onset after head injury

**Tinnitus Secondary to PTSD:**

PTSD doesn't cause tinnitus, but hyperarousal and stress significantly worsen it.

**Evidence:**
1. Service-connected PTSD
2. Audiological exam confirming tinnitus
3. Nexus: PTSD hyperarousal and stress aggravate tinnitus perception
4. Statement describing how tinnitus worsens with PTSD symptoms

**Tinnitus Secondary to Hearing Loss:**

Tinnitus and hearing loss commonly occur together.

**Evidence:**
1. Service-connected hearing loss
2. Tinnitus confirmed by audiology
3. Nexus: Same damage causing hearing loss causes tinnitus
4. Both conditions often from same noise exposure

## Why Only 10%?

Many veterans wonder why tinnitus, which can be severely debilitating, only rates 10%.

**VA's Rationale:**
- Tinnitus is subjective (can't be objectively measured like hearing loss)
- No objective test proves severity
- Flat rating simplifies evaluation
- Recognizes the condition without complex severity grading

**Reality:**
- Severe tinnitus can be devastating
- Can cause sleep problems, depression, anxiety, concentration issues
- But VA rating remains 10% regardless

**What You CAN Do:**
1. **File for secondary conditions caused by tinnitus:**
   - Insomnia secondary to tinnitus
   - Depression secondary to tinnitus (if severe impact)
   - Anxiety secondary to tinnitus

2. **File for TDIU (unemployability):** If tinnitus combined with other conditions prevents you from working, file for TDIU even if your combined rating is below 100%. (Requires 60% for one condition OR 70% combined with one at 40%.)

3. **Combine with other conditions:** Tinnitus alone is 10%, but combined with hearing loss, PTSD, TBI, etc., your overall rating increases.

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: Tinnitus from Noise Exposure - 10% Rating
**Background:** Army infantry veteran, 2 combat deployments, extensive weapons fire exposure.

**Strategy:**
- Audiological evaluation confirmed bilateral tinnitus
- Personal statement described constant ringing since deployment
- Nexus based on MOS and documented combat exposure
- Buddy statement from fellow soldier confirming heavy weapons fire exposure
- Filed for direct service connection based on noise exposure

**Result:** Granted 10% for tinnitus ($171.23/month).

**Key Factors:** Clear MOS with known noise exposure, audiological confirmation, straightforward nexus.

### Case Study 2: Tinnitus Secondary to TBI - 10% Rating
**Background:** Marine Corps veteran, IED blast injury, service-connected TBI at 70%.

**Strategy:**
- Already service-connected for TBI
- Tinnitus began after blast injury
- Audiologist confirmed bilateral tinnitus
- Neurologist provided nexus: tinnitus common after TBI, likely from blast trauma to auditory system
- Filed secondary claim

**Result:** Granted 10% for tinnitus secondary to TBI. Combined rating increased from 70% to 73%.

**Key Factors:** Existing service-connected condition, clear temporal relationship, medical nexus from specialist.

### Case Study 3: Tinnitus + Hearing Loss - Combined 20%
**Background:** Navy veteran, flight deck operations, 20 years of aircraft noise exposure.

**Strategy:**
- Audiological evaluation showed both tinnitus and mild hearing loss
- Filed for BOTH conditions simultaneously
- MOS clearly involved high noise exposure
- Service hearing test records showed hearing decline over career
- Nexus based on occupational noise exposure

**Result:**
- Tinnitus: 10%
- Hearing Loss: 10%
- Combined rating: 20% ($333.01/month)

**Key Factors:** Filed both conditions together, strong noise exposure evidence, documented hearing decline.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I get more than 10% for severe tinnitus?
No. The maximum rating for tinnitus is 10% regardless of severity. However, you can file for secondary conditions caused by tinnitus (depression, insomnia) and combine ratings.

### Do I need to show my tinnitus is severe to get 10%?
No. Any recurrent tinnitus qualifies for 10%, whether mild, moderate, or severe.

### Can I get separate ratings for tinnitus in each ear?
No. Tinnitus is rated once at 10%, whether it's in one ear, both ears, or perceived "in the head."

### Does tinnitus combine with hearing loss?
Yes. Tinnitus (10%) and hearing loss (0-100%) are rated separately and then combined. This is very common.

### How do I prove tinnitus is from military service?
Show noise exposure during service (your MOS, combat exposure, equipment operation), OR file as secondary to another service-connected condition (TBI, PTSD, hearing loss, etc.). Audiologist or doctor provides nexus opinion.

### What if my tinnitus started years after service?
You can still file. If you had noise exposure during service and now have tinnitus, there's a connection. Get a medical nexus opinion linking your current tinnitus to past military noise exposure.

### Can tinnitus be secondary to PTSD?
Not directly caused by, but PTSD can significantly aggravate tinnitus. Some veterans successfully file secondary claims when PTSD stress/hyperarousal worsens tinnitus perception.

### Do I need service medical records documenting tinnitus?
No. Most tinnitus claims are based on noise exposure during service, even without service medical records mentioning tinnitus. Current diagnosis + noise exposure + nexus = service connection.

### What if I have pulsatile tinnitus (rhythmic, matches heartbeat)?
This may have different causes (vascular). Still rated at 10% if confirmed as tinnitus, but should be medically evaluated as it can indicate vascular issues.

### Can medications cause tinnitus?
Yes, certain medications are ototoxic (damage hearing/cause tinnitus). If you took ototoxic medications for a service-connected condition, tinnitus may be secondary to that treatment.

### Will VA reduce my tinnitus rating?
Highly unlikely. Tinnitus ratings are rarely reduced as it's typically a permanent condition and there's only one rating level.

### Can I get TDIU with just tinnitus?
Not with tinnitus alone at 10%. TDIU requires one condition at 60% or combined rating of 70% with one condition at 40%. However, tinnitus can be part of your combined rating toward TDIU eligibility.

### What if my tinnitus comes and goes?
As long as it's recurrent (happens regularly), it qualifies for the 10% rating. It doesn't need to be constant.

### Does tinnitus qualify for any special compensation?
Tinnitus itself only rates 10%. However, if combined with other conditions, it contributes to overall rating. If tinnitus causes or aggravates other conditions (depression, anxiety, insomnia), those can be rated separately.

## Final Thoughts

Tinnitus is one of the most common service-connected conditions, affecting hundreds of thousands of veterans. While the 10% rating may seem low, it's a straightforward claim that most veterans with noise exposure can successfully file.

**Keys to a Successful Tinnitus Claim:**

1. **Get audiological evaluation** - Confirms tinnitus diagnosis
2. **Document service noise exposure** - Your MOS, combat, equipment, weapons
3. **Consider secondary connection** - TBI, PTSD, hearing loss, TMJ
4. **File with hearing loss** - If you have both, file both for combined rating
5. **Get nexus opinion** - Medical professional links tinnitus to service
6. **File comprehensively** - Include all evidence with initial claim

**Remember:**
- Severity doesn't matter for rating (all tinnitus is 10%)
- Be honest during exam - no need to exaggerate or minimize
- Tinnitus often occurs with hearing loss - file both
- Straightforward claim for most veterans with noise exposure
- Lifetime value of $82,000+ over 40 years

Tinnitus may be "just" 10%, but it's often an easy claim to win and combines with other conditions. If you have tinnitus related to your military service, you've earned this compensation.

**File your tinnitus claim. You earned it.**
