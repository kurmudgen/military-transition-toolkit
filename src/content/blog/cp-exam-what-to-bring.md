# What to Bring to Your C&P Exam: Complete Checklist (2025)

Walking into your C&P exam prepared can make the difference between an accurate rating and leaving money on the table. This checklist ensures you don't forget anything important.

## Essential Items (Bring These No Matter What)

### 1. Government-Issued Photo ID
- Driver's license
- State ID
- Military ID (active or retired)
- Passport

**Why it matters:** You won't be seen without valid identification.

### 2. VA Exam Notification Letter
The letter or notification telling you about the appointment. This contains:
- Date, time, and location
- Which condition(s) are being examined
- Contact information for rescheduling

**Tip:** Screenshot or photograph the notification if it's electronic.

### 3. List of Current Medications
Include:
- Medication name
- Dosage
- Frequency
- What it's prescribed for
- Prescribing doctor

**Format example:**
```
1. Omeprazole 40mg - Once daily - GERD - Dr. Smith
2. Sertraline 100mg - Once daily - PTSD - Dr. Jones
3. Naproxen 500mg - Twice daily - Back pain - Dr. Williams
```

**Why it matters:** Medications demonstrate ongoing treatment and can affect your exam (pain meds, muscle relaxants, etc.).

### 4. List of All Healthcare Providers
- Name
- Specialty
- Facility/clinic
- What they treat you for

**Include:**
- VA doctors
- Private doctors
- Specialists
- Mental health providers
- Physical therapists
- Chiropractors

### 5. Written Notes on Your Symptoms
Prepare notes covering:
- All symptoms for the condition being examined
- Frequency (daily, weekly, monthly)
- Severity (1-10 scale)
- Duration of episodes
- What makes it better/worse
- Impact on daily activities

**You can reference these during the exam.** Many veterans forget important details due to stress or pain.

## Condition-Specific Items

### For Musculoskeletal Exams (Back, Knee, Shoulder, etc.)

**Bring:**
- Any braces or supports you use
- Cane, walker, or other mobility aids
- Documentation of physical therapy
- Notes on range of motion limitations
- Flare-up documentation

**Wear:**
- Loose, comfortable clothing
- Shorts if it's a knee/hip exam
- Clothing you can easily move in

### For Mental Health Exams (PTSD, Depression, Anxiety)

**Bring:**
- Treatment records from therapists/psychiatrists
- Notes on specific symptoms and incidents
- Documentation of hospitalizations
- List of triggers
- Notes on social and occupational impact

**Prepare mentally:**
- You may be asked about traumatic events
- You can take breaks if needed
- You don't have to provide graphic details

### For Sleep Apnea Exams

**Bring:**
- Copy of sleep study results
- CPAP prescription
- CPAP compliance data (if available)
- Notes on symptoms before/after CPAP
- For secondary claims: evidence of primary condition

### For Hearing/Tinnitus Exams

**Bring:**
- Any previous audiograms
- Documentation of noise exposure during service
- Notes on when symptoms started
- Description of how tinnitus affects you

### For Skin Condition Exams

**Bring:**
- Photos of skin condition at its worst
- Documentation of treatments tried
- Notes on percentage of body affected
- List of triggers

**Timing tip:** Try to schedule the exam when your condition is active, not during a period of remission.

## Documentation to Support Your Claim

### 6. Records Not Yet in VA System

If you have medical records the VA might not have:
- Recent private doctor visits
- Specialist evaluations
- Urgent care/ER visits
- Outside imaging (MRI, X-rays)
- Private therapy records

**Important:** Bring copies, not originals. You won't get them back.

### 7. Buddy Statements

If you submitted buddy statements, bring copies. These include:
- Statements from spouse/family
- Fellow service members
- Coworkers
- Anyone who has observed your condition

### 8. Service Records (If Relevant)

For direct service connection claims:
- DD-214
- Service treatment records
- Deployment orders
- Line of duty determinations
- Anything showing in-service injury or event

### 9. Private Medical Opinions

If you have:
- Nexus letters from private doctors
- Independent Medical Opinions (IMO)
- Specialist evaluations supporting your claim

**Bring copies.** Even if already submitted, having them available ensures the examiner sees them.

## Helpful Extras

### 10. Flare-Up Documentation

A log or diary showing:
- Dates of flare-ups
- Duration
- Severity
- What triggered them
- What you couldn't do during them

**Example format:**
```
Date: 11/15/24
Pain level: 8/10
Duration: 3 days
Trigger: Cold weather
Impact: Couldn't work, stayed in bed
```

### 11. Work Impact Documentation

Evidence of how your condition affects employment:
- Attendance records showing missed days
- Performance reviews mentioning condition
- Letters from employer
- Notes on job accommodations needed

### 12. Photos/Videos

For visible conditions:
- Skin conditions at their worst
- Swelling
- Scars
- Range of motion limitations (video)

**Tip:** Date-stamp photos by texting them to yourself.

### 13. Questions for the Examiner

You can ask:
- "Are you evaluating my [condition]?"
- "Did you note my flare-ups?"
- "Can I provide additional information about [topic]?"
- "When will I receive results?"

## What NOT to Bring

### Don't Bring:
- **Original documents** (bring copies)
- **An aggressive attitude** (stay calm and cooperative)
- **Someone who will speak for you** (usually not allowed in exam room)
- **Weapons** (leave in car)

### Don't Do:
- **Take extra pain medication** before the exam (skews results)
- **Stretch or warm up** before musculoskeletal exams
- **Exaggerate symptoms** (undermines credibility)
- **Dress to look healthier than usual**

## Day-Before Checklist

**The night before:**

- [ ] Confirm appointment time and location
- [ ] Map the route and parking
- [ ] Gather all items from this checklist
- [ ] Charge your phone (in case you need to check documents)
- [ ] Set multiple alarms
- [ ] Get adequate sleep
- [ ] Don't drink excessive alcohol
- [ ] Take normal medications as prescribed

**Morning of:**

- [ ] Eat normally (unless told otherwise)
- [ ] Take regular medications (not extra pain meds)
- [ ] Wear appropriate clothing
- [ ] Leave early for traffic/parking
- [ ] Bring phone charger (long waits possible)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I bring someone with me for support?
To the waiting room, yes. Into the exam room, generally no—but ask if you have special circumstances (like a hearing interpreter).

### What if I forgot something?
Do your best with what you have. You can submit additional evidence after the exam through your claim file.

### Can I bring my own doctor?
No, but you can bring their written opinion (nexus letter).

### Should I bring a recording device?
Policies vary. Ask before recording. You can take detailed notes immediately after.

### What if I can't find a document?
Focus on what you have. Missing one document isn't claim-ending—the examiner will evaluate based on what's available.

### Can I bring notes to read from?
Yes! You're allowed to reference written notes during the exam.

## Bottom Line

Being prepared for your C&P exam demonstrates organization and strengthens your claim. The examiner will have access to your VA records, but having additional documentation shows you're taking this seriously.

**Key items:**
- Photo ID (required)
- Medication list
- Written symptom notes
- Any records not in VA system
- Assistive devices you actually use

Print this checklist. Use it. Don't wing your C&P exam.
