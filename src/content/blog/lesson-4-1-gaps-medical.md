### PART 1: UNDERSTANDING EMPLOYMENT GAPS (0:30-3:00)

**[ON SCREEN: "What Counts as an Employment Gap?"]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**
First, let's define what we're talking about. An employment gap is any period of 3+ months where you weren't working or in school.

For transitioning veterans, common gaps include:
- Time between military separation and starting civilian job search (transition period)
- Medical treatment or recovery after separation
- Time spent on education/training using GI Bill
- Time caring for family members
- Time dealing with PTSD, TBI, or other service-connected conditions

**[ON SCREEN: "The Good News: Gaps Are More Common Than You Think"]**

Here's what you need to know: employment gaps are increasingly common and accepted, especially post-COVID. The key isn't to hide the gap - it's to explain it confidently and show what you did during that time.

**STRATEGY 1: MINIMIZE THE APPEARANCE OF GAPS ON YOUR RESUME**

You can do this legitimately through formatting:

**Use Years Only (Not Months) for Older Positions:**

❌ Draws attention to gap:
"U.S. Army, Jan 2010 - March 2023"
"ABC Company, September 2023 - Present"
[6-month gap visible]

✅ Minimizes gap:
"U.S. Army, 2010 - 2023"
"ABC Company, 2023 - Present"
[Gap less obvious]

**Group Activities During Gap:**

If you were doing multiple things during a gap (training, volunteering, freelancing), group them:

**PROFESSIONAL DEVELOPMENT & TRANSITION**
2023 - 2024
- Completed PMP certification (Project Management Professional)
- Volunteered as team leader for Veterans of Foreign Wars
- Conducted independent study in supply chain management
- Participated in SkillBridge program with [Company Name]

This shows you were productive, not idle.

### PART 2: EXPLAINING MEDICAL RETIREMENT (3:00-6:30)

**[ON SCREEN: "How to Address Medical Retirement"]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**
Medical retirement is a sensitive topic. You want to be honest, but you don't want to raise concerns about your ability to do the job.

**THE BASIC PRINCIPLE: EMPHASIZE CAPABILITY, NOT DISABILITY**

You need to answer three questions for the employer:
1. Can you do the job?
2. Will you be reliable?
3. Are you moving forward or dwelling on the past?

**[ON SCREEN: How to Mention Medical Retirement on Resume]**

**ON YOUR RESUME:**

You have two options:

**Option 1: Don't mention it explicitly**

Simply list your military service with end date:

"Operations Manager (Platoon Sergeant)"
U.S. Army | 2010 - 2023

That's it. The employer doesn't need to know WHY you left. If asked in an interview, you can explain then.

**Option 2: Mention it briefly and positively**

"Operations Manager (Platoon Sergeant)"
U.S. Army | 2010 - 2023
Honorably discharged after 13 years of service. Transitioned to pursue second career in project management."

Note: "Honorable discharge" and "transitioned" - positive language.

**[ON SCREEN: How to Address in Cover Letter]**

**IN YOUR COVER LETTER (if you choose to mention it):**

One sentence, framed positively:

"After 13 years of distinguished Army service, I am transitioning to civilian life and excited to apply my leadership and operational expertise in the corporate logistics sector."

OR

"Following a successful military career abbreviated by medical retirement, I am pursuing my passion for project management and am fully capable and eager to contribute to your team."

**[ON SCREEN: How to Address in Interview]**

**IN THE INTERVIEW (when asked "Why did you leave the military?"):**

**The Formula: Acknowledge + Capability + Forward Focus**

**Example 1:**
"I was medically retired after sustaining injuries during deployment. I've completed my treatment, I'm fully capable of performing this role, and I'm excited to start the next chapter of my career in [industry]."

**Example 2:**
"I medically retired due to service-connected injuries. I've been cleared by my physicians for this type of work, and I'm ready to bring my 15 years of leadership experience to a new challenge."

**Example 3:**
"I medically separated from the Army, but I'm fully capable and excited about this opportunity. My medical issues don't impact my ability to [perform key job functions], and I'm looking forward to contributing to your team."

**[ON SCREEN: What NOT to say]**

**WHAT NOT TO SAY:**

❌ Don't overshare medical details
"I have degenerative disc disease, PTSD, TBI, and hearing loss in both ears..."

❌ Don't sound bitter or negative
"The Army broke my body and forced me out..."

❌ Don't raise doubts about capability
"I can't do physical labor anymore, so I'm looking for desk jobs..."

✅ DO say:
"I medically retired after 15 years of service. I'm fully capable of this role and excited about the opportunity."

Short, confident, forward-looking.

### PART 3: DISABILITY RATING DISCLOSURE (6:30-10:00)

**[ON SCREEN: "Should You Disclose Your VA Disability Rating?"]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**
This is one of the most common questions I get: "Should I mention my VA disability rating on my resume or in interviews?"

The short answer: It depends on the rating and the job.

**[ON SCREEN: Disability Disclosure Decision Tree]**

**WHEN TO MENTION YOUR DISABILITY RATING:**

✅ Mention it if:
- You're applying for federal jobs (veteran preference points)
- You're applying for positions with companies that have veteran hiring programs
- You have a 30% or higher rating and you're comfortable disclosing
- The rating is for conditions that don't impact job performance (e.g., tinnitus, knee injury for desk job)

**WHEN NOT TO MENTION IT:**

❌ Don't mention if:
- You're applying to private sector companies (not relevant to job qualifications)
- You have mental health conditions you're not comfortable disclosing
- You think it might raise concerns about your ability to do the job
- The employer has no specific veteran hiring initiatives

**[ON SCREEN: How to Disclose (If You Choose To)]**

**HOW TO DISCLOSE (if you choose to):**

**On federal applications:**
Check the "30% or more disabled veteran" box for preference points. This is straightforward and expected.

**On resume (optional):**
Under "Military Service" or "Additional Information" section:

"Service-Connected Disabled Veteran (30%+)"
OR
"VA Disability Rating: [X]% - Service-Connected"

**In interview (if relevant):**
"I have a service-connected disability rating for [general condition, e.g., 'mobility issues,' 'hearing loss'], but it doesn't impact my ability to perform this role."

**[ON SCREEN: Understanding ADA Protections]**

**UNDERSTANDING YOUR LEGAL PROTECTIONS:**

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) protects you:
- Employers cannot discriminate based on disability
- You're entitled to "reasonable accommodations" if needed
- You do NOT have to disclose disabilities unless you need accommodations

**Key point:** You control the narrative. You decide what to share and when.

**[ON SCREEN: Mental Health Disclosure]**

**MENTAL HEALTH DISCLOSURE (PTSD, TBI, Anxiety, Depression):**

This is especially sensitive. Here's my guidance:

**DO NOT disclose mental health conditions on your resume or in initial applications.**

Why? Unfortunately, there's still stigma. You risk being filtered out before you have a chance to demonstrate your capabilities.

**You may choose to disclose later if:**
- You get the job and need accommodations (flexible schedule for therapy appointments)
- You've built trust with your manager
- The company has a strong mental health support culture

**But there's NEVER a requirement to disclose mental health conditions unless they directly prevent you from performing essential job functions - which is rare.**

**[ON SCREEN: Accommodations]**

**REQUESTING ACCOMMODATIONS:**

If you need accommodations due to a service-connected condition:

**AFTER receiving a job offer, you can request accommodations:**

"I have a service-connected condition that requires [specific accommodation]. Under the ADA, I'm requesting [accommodation] as a reasonable adjustment to perform this role."

**Common reasonable accommodations:**
- Flexible schedule for medical appointments
- Ergonomic office setup
- Noise-canceling headphones (for PTSD/TBI)
- Work-from-home flexibility
- Modified break schedule

Employers are required to provide reasonable accommodations unless they create "undue hardship."

### PART 4: SKILLBRIDGE AND TRANSITION PROGRAMS (10:00-12:30)

**[ON SCREEN: "Using SkillBridge to Fill Gaps"]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**
Let me show you how to use transition programs like SkillBridge to your advantage.

**WHAT IS SKILLBRIDGE?**

SkillBridge is a DoD program that allows service members to intern with civilian companies during their last 180 days of service (while still receiving military pay and benefits).

**HOW TO LIST SKILLBRIDGE ON YOUR RESUME:**

**SKILLBRIDGE INTERNSHIP**
[Company Name] | [Location]
[Dates]

- Participated in DoD SkillBridge program transitioning to civilian workforce
- [Accomplishments and projects during internship]
- [Skills gained or applied]

**Example:**

**OPERATIONS INTERN (SKILLBRIDGE PROGRAM)**
Amazon Logistics | Seattle, WA
January 2024 - March 2024

- Participated in DoD SkillBridge transition program preparing for civilian logistics career
- Managed warehouse operations workflow for 50-person shift, optimizing productivity by 15%
- Coordinated inventory management using Amazon WMS, processing 500+ daily transactions
- Completed internal training on Lean Six Sigma principles and warehouse safety protocols

**[ON SCREEN: Other Transition Activities]**

**OTHER TRANSITION ACTIVITIES TO INCLUDE:**

If you took time off to transition, don't leave it blank. Show what you did:

**PROFESSIONAL TRANSITION & DEVELOPMENT**
[Dates]

- Completed Project Management Professional (PMP) certification
- Attended Hiring Our Heroes Career Summit
- Participated in Google Veterans Network mentorship program
- Completed coursework in [relevant field] via LinkedIn Learning/Coursera
- Volunteered as operations coordinator for [veteran organization]

This shows initiative, continued learning, and professional development during your transition period.

### PART 5: WRITING YOUR EXPLANATION (12:30-14:00)

**[ON SCREEN: "Crafting Your Explanation"]**

**INSTRUCTOR:**
Let me give you exact language you can use to explain gaps or medical retirement in interviews.

**[ON SCREEN: Explanation Templates]**

**TEMPLATE 1: MEDICAL RETIREMENT**

"I medically retired from the Army after [X] years of service due to service-connected injuries sustained during [deployment/training]. I completed my treatment and recovery, and I'm fully capable of performing this role. I'm excited to start this next chapter and bring my [X years] of leadership and operational experience to your team."

**TEMPLATE 2: TRANSITION PERIOD GAP**

"After leaving the military in [month/year], I took [X months] to transition, complete my PMP certification, and strategically prepare for my civilian career. During that time, I also [volunteered/completed SkillBridge/took courses], and I'm now ready to fully commit to my new career in [field]."

**TEMPLATE 3: MEDICAL TREATMENT GAP**

"I took time off to address some service-connected medical issues and complete treatment. I'm now fully recovered and cleared for work. The experience actually reinforced my resilience and time management skills, and I'm eager to bring that determination to your team."

**TEMPLATE 4: FAMILY CARE GAP**

"I took time off to care for a family member. That period is now behind me, and I'm fully available and committed to this opportunity. During that time, I also maintained my professional skills by [online courses/volunteer work/professional reading], and I'm ready to contribute."

**[ON SCREEN: Practice Your Delivery]**

**PRACTICE YOUR DELIVERY:**

The key is:
1. **Be brief** - 2-3 sentences maximum
2. **Be confident** - No apologizing or sounding defensive
3. **Redirect forward** - End with enthusiasm about the opportunity
4. **Practice out loud** - Say it until it sounds natural and confident
