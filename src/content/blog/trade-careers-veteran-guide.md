# Trade Careers for Veterans: Complete Apprenticeship Guide (2025)

The skilled trades offer veterans one of the best career paths available: high pay, no college debt, union benefits, and work that uses your military discipline and work ethic. Many trades face severe labor shortages, meaning opportunity for veterans who want to work with their hands.

This guide covers how to enter the trades, union apprenticeships, Helmets to Hardhats, and building a career earning $60,000 to $100,000+.

## Why Trades for Veterans?

### The Opportunity

**Labor Shortage**: The construction industry alone needs 650,000+ workers. Skilled tradespeople are in critical demand across America.

**No College Required**: Earn while you learn through apprenticeships. No student debt.

**High Pay**: Journeyman wages range from $55,000 to $100,000+. Overtime, benefits, and pension add substantial value.

**Your Military Skills Transfer**:
- Physical work? You've done harder.
- Following procedures? That's SOPs.
- Safety consciousness? Already ingrained.
- Working in teams? Second nature.
- Showing up on time and working hard? Your competitive advantage.

### Why Unions?

**Apprenticeship Programs**: Structured training, earn while you learn
**Living Wages**: Union scale typically exceeds non-union
**Benefits**: Health insurance, pension, annuity
**Job Security**: Union hiring halls and contractor relationships
**Veteran Support**: Many unions actively recruit veterans

## Top Trades for Veterans

### Electrical

**IBEW (International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers)**

**Apprenticeship:**
- 5 years (8,000-10,000 hours)
- Classroom + on-the-job training
- Start earning immediately (~50-60% of journeyman rate)

**Journeyman Wages (2025):**
- National average: $55,000-$85,000
- High-cost areas (NYC, SF, Chicago): $80,000-$130,000+

**Career Paths:**
- Inside Wireman (commercial/industrial)
- Residential Electrician
- Lineman (utility work): $75,000-$140,000+
- Low-voltage/Data/Fire Alarm

**Why Veterans Fit:** Technical work, safety-critical, physical demands

### Plumbing and Pipefitting

**UA (United Association of Plumbers and Pipefitters)**

**Apprenticeship:**
- 5 years (typically)
- Plumber, Pipefitter, HVAC, Sprinklerfitter tracks

**Journeyman Wages:**
- Plumber: $55,000-$90,000
- Pipefitter: $60,000-$100,000+
- Sprinklerfitter: $55,000-$85,000

**Career Paths:**
- Commercial plumbing
- Industrial pipefitting
- HVAC service
- Fire protection (sprinklerfitters)

**UA Veterans in Piping (VIP):** Direct entry program for veterans

### HVAC/Refrigeration

**SMART (Sheet Metal Workers) and UA**

**Apprenticeship:**
- 4-5 years
- Commercial and residential tracks

**Journeyman Wages:**
- HVAC Technician: $50,000-$80,000
- Commercial HVAC: $60,000-$90,000
- Refrigeration Tech: $55,000-$85,000

**Why Veterans Fit:** Technical troubleshooting, military HVAC experience transfers

### Ironwork

**Ironworkers Union**

**Apprenticeship:**
- 3-4 years
- Structural, reinforcing, ornamental

**Journeyman Wages:**
- Structural Ironworker: $60,000-$100,000+
- Reinforcing Ironworker: $55,000-$85,000

**Work:** Building skyscrapers, bridges, stadiums

**Why Veterans Fit:** Physical work, heights, rigging (91B/BM/MK experience)

### Carpentry

**UBC (United Brotherhood of Carpenters)**

**Apprenticeship:**
- 4 years

**Journeyman Wages:**
- $50,000-$85,000
- Higher in major metros

**Specialties:**
- Commercial carpentry
- Millwright (industrial machinery)
- Pile driver
- Floor coverer

### Operating Engineers

**IUOE (International Union of Operating Engineers)**

**Apprenticeship:**
- 3-4 years
- Heavy equipment operation

**Journeyman Wages:**
- Crane Operator: $60,000-$100,000+
- Heavy Equipment Operator: $55,000-$90,000

**Equipment:** Cranes, excavators, bulldozers, loaders

**Why Veterans Fit:** Heavy equipment MOS (12N, 91L) direct translation

### Boilermakers

**Boilermakers Union**

**Apprenticeship:**
- 4 years

**Journeyman Wages:**
- $65,000-$100,000+
- Travel work often higher

**Work:** Power plants, refineries, industrial settings

**Why Veterans Fit:** Welding skills, industrial environment, Navy/MK experience

### Elevator Constructors

**IUEC (Elevator Constructors)**

**Highest Paid Trade:**
- Journeyman: $80,000-$130,000+
- Extremely competitive entry

**Apprenticeship:**
- 4 years
- Very limited slots

### Lineman

**IBEW Utility Division**

**Wages:**
- Journeyman Lineman: $75,000-$140,000+
- Storm work: Substantial overtime

**Work:** Power line construction and maintenance

**Why Veterans Fit:** Outdoor work, physical demands, travel

## Helmets to Hardhats

### What It Is

**Helmets to Hardhats (H2H)** is a national program connecting veterans to union apprenticeships.

**Website:** helmetstohardhats.org

### How It Works

1. **Register online** at H2H website
2. **Create profile** with military experience
3. **Search opportunities** by trade and location
4. **Apply to programs** directly through portal
5. **Get matched** with union apprenticeships

### Benefits

- Direct connection to building trades unions
- Streamlined application process
- Veteran priority consideration
- Support through application process
- Covers most major trades

### Who Participates

**Building Trades Unions:**
- IBEW (Electrical)
- UA (Plumbers and Pipefitters)
- Ironworkers
- Carpenters (UBC)
- IUOE (Operating Engineers)
- Sheet Metal Workers (SMART)
- Boilermakers
- Laborers (LIUNA)
- Painters
- Roofers
- And more

## Getting Into an Apprenticeship

### General Process

**1. Research Programs:**
- Visit union local websites
- Use Helmets to Hardhats
- Contact local Joint Apprenticeship Training Committee (JATC)

**2. Meet Requirements:**
- Age: Usually 18+
- Education: High school diploma or GED
- Physical: Ability to perform trade work
- Drug-free: Testing required
- Driver's license: Often required

**3. Apply:**
- Application periods vary (some continuous, some periodic)
- Application fee (often $25-100)
- Submit DD-214 and transcripts

**4. Testing:**
- Aptitude test (math, reading comprehension)
- Some trades require algebra
- Study resources often available

**5. Interview:**
- Panel interview with JATC
- Questions about why you want the trade
- Military experience is a plus

**6. Selection:**
- Ranked based on test, interview, qualifications
- Veterans often receive preference points

### Veteran Advantages

**What helps your application:**
- Military construction/technical MOS
- Honorable discharge
- Physical fitness
- Drug-free history
- Reliability/attendance record
- Related experience

**Specific programs:**
- Many locals have veteran preference
- Some have dedicated veteran slots
- UA VIP offers direct entry for qualifying veterans

## Union vs Non-Union

### Union Advantages

| Factor | Union | Non-Union |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| Wages | Higher (scale rates) | Lower (market rates) |
| Benefits | Full package included | Varies |
| Training | Structured apprenticeship | Often less formal |
| Pension | Defined benefit common | Rare |
| Job Security | Hiring hall, seniority | At-will employment |
| Work Hours | Overtime rules enforced | Varies |

### Non-Union Path

**Can be faster entry:**
- Get hired directly by contractor
- On-the-job training
- May be able to test into union later

**Trade-offs:**
- Lower wages typically
- Benefits responsibility on you
- Less structured training
- Less job security

## Trade School and Certifications

### Pre-Apprenticeship Options

**Trade Schools:**
- Shorter programs (3-12 months)
- Specific trade focus
- Can help get into apprenticeship
- Use GI Bill (some programs)

**Certifications:**
- OSHA 10/30 (construction safety): Required by many
- EPA 608 (refrigerant handling): Required for HVAC
- NCCER certifications: Industry recognized
- Welding certifications (AWS): Required for welding work

### During Apprenticeship

**Classroom training:**
- Technical education
- Code requirements
- Safety training
- Often at union training centers

**Certifications earned:**
- Journeyman card (state license where applicable)
- Specialty certifications
- Continuing education requirements

## Career Progression

### Apprentice to Journeyman

**Apprentice Phase (3-5 years):**
- Earn 50-90% of journeyman rate (increasing each year)
- Learn from journeymen on the job
- Attend classes
- Pass required tests

**Journeyman:**
- Full rate
- Work independently
- Sign on with contractors
- Dispatch from hiring hall

### Beyond Journeyman

**Foreman:**
- Lead small crews
- 10-15% above journeyman rate
- Supervisory responsibility

**General Foreman:**
- Oversee multiple crews
- Higher pay
- Management responsibilities

**Superintendent:**
- Oversee entire project
- $90,000-$150,000+
- Senior management

**Business/Contractor:**
- Start your own company
- Risk and reward
- SBA veteran loans available

## Financial Reality

### Apprenticeship Earnings

**Year 1:** ~50-60% of journeyman ($25,000-$40,000)
**Year 2:** ~60-70% of journeyman ($32,000-$50,000)
**Year 3:** ~70-80% of journeyman ($40,000-$60,000)
**Year 4:** ~80-90% of journeyman ($48,000-$70,000)
**Year 5:** ~90-95% of journeyman ($55,000-$80,000)

**After Journeyman:** Full scale plus raises

### Benefits Package Value

**Typical union benefits add 30-50% to wages:**
- Health insurance (family coverage)
- Pension contribution
- Annuity/401(k) contribution
- Training fund
- Vacation pay

**Example:**
- Journeyman wage: $40/hour
- Total package: $55-60/hour
- Annual value: $115,000-$125,000

### Overtime and Work Availability

**Construction trades:**
- Overtime common (time-and-a-half, double-time)
- Seasonal variations
- May have slow periods
- Travel work available (often higher pay)

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need construction experience?

No. Apprenticeships start from zero. Your military work ethic matters more than specific experience.

### How competitive are apprenticeships?

Varies by trade and location. Some (elevator, IBEW in major cities) are very competitive. Others have more openings. Apply to multiple programs.

### Can I use GI Bill during apprenticeship?

Yes. Many registered apprenticeships accept GI Bill, providing monthly stipend on top of apprenticeship wages.

### What if there's no union in my area?

Non-union contractors also hire and train. Trade schools provide training. You can relocate for union apprenticeship.

### How do I choose which trade?

Consider: What work interests you? Indoor vs outdoor? Heights? Travel? Heavy vs detailed work? Research each trade's actual work.

### What's the age limit?

Most have no upper limit, just minimum (usually 18). Veterans in their 30s and 40s successfully enter trades.

## Bottom Line

The skilled trades offer veterans high-paying careers with no college debt, union benefits, and pension retirement. Your military discipline, work ethic, and physical capability are exactly what the trades need during a severe labor shortage.

**Best Entry Paths:**
1. **Helmets to Hardhats** - Direct connection to union apprenticeships
2. **UA VIP** - Direct entry for qualifying veterans into pipefitting
3. **Local union JATCs** - Apply directly to apprenticeship programs
4. **GI Bill + Apprenticeship** - Get paid while you learn

Your military service prepared you for this. The trades need you.
