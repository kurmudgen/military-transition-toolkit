# Starting a Veteran-Owned Government Contractor (SDVOSB): Complete Guide

The federal government wants to do business with veteran-owned companies. In fact, there's a statutory goal that 3% of all federal contracting dollars go to Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Businesses (SDVOSBs). That represents billions of dollars annually in opportunities specifically set aside for veteran entrepreneurs.

If you're a service-disabled veteran considering government contracting, this guide covers what you need to know to get started.

## Understanding Veteran Business Certifications

### SDVOSB (Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business)

**Definition:**
- Owned 51%+ by service-disabled veteran(s)
- Controlled by service-disabled veteran(s)
- Service-connected disability (any percentage)
- Small business by SBA size standards

**Benefits:**
- Sole-source contracts up to $5 million
- Set-aside competitions
- Evaluation preferences
- Access to specialized opportunities

### VOSB (Veteran-Owned Small Business)

**Definition:**
- Owned 51%+ by veteran(s)
- Controlled by veteran(s)
- No disability requirement
- Small business by size standards

**Benefits:**
- Fewer set-asides than SDVOSB
- Still valuable for some opportunities
- Pathway to other certifications

### VA Verification vs. SBA Certification

**SBA Certification (Veteran Small Business Certification Program):**
- Government-wide certification
- Required for most SDVOSB set-asides
- Rigorous review process
- Valid for 3 years

**VA Verification (VetCert):**
- Specific to VA contracts
- Now administered by SBA
- Required for VA set-asides
- Must meet SBA requirements

## Eligibility Requirements

### Service-Disabled Veteran

**Must have:**
- Honorable discharge
- Service-connected disability rating from VA
- Any percentage qualifies (even 0%)

**Documentation:**
- DD-214
- VA disability rating letter
- Benefits summary letter

### Ownership Requirements

**51% unconditional ownership by:**
- Service-disabled veteran(s)
- Must be direct ownership (not through trusts, etc.)
- Control of business decisions

### Control Requirements

**Service-disabled veteran must:**
- Hold highest officer position
- Control day-to-day operations
- Make long-term decisions
- Have management experience for the work

### Small Business Size

**Must meet SBA size standards:**
- Varies by NAICS code
- Based on annual receipts or employees
- Check specific NAICS codes for your industry

## Getting Certified

### Step 1: Prepare Your Business

Before applying:
- **Business formation:** Establish legal entity (LLC, Corporation)
- **Operating agreement/bylaws:** Document veteran ownership and control
- **Business plan:** Show capability and experience
- **Financial records:** Demonstrate business operations

### Step 2: Register in Government Systems

**Required registrations:**

**SAM.gov (System for Award Management):**
- Required for any federal contracting
- Free registration
- Renew annually
- Get your Unique Entity ID (UEI)

**DSBS (Dynamic Small Business Search):**
- SBA's small business database
- Free profile
- Helps agencies find you

### Step 3: Apply for SBA Certification

**Application through certify.sba.gov:**

**Documentation required:**
- Birth certificate or passport
- DD-214
- VA disability rating letter
- Business formation documents
- Operating agreement/bylaws
- Tax returns
- Bank statements
- Resume showing relevant experience
- Additional documentation as requested

**Timeline:**
- 90-day processing goal
- Can take longer
- Respond promptly to requests

### Step 4: Maintain Certification

**Ongoing requirements:**
- Annual recertification
- Report changes immediately
- Maintain eligibility
- Keep documentation current

## Finding Government Opportunities

### Set-Aside Contracts

**SDVOSB set-asides:**
- Contracts reserved for SDVOSB competition only
- Can be any size (though typically smaller)
- Must have certified SDVOSB status

**Sole-source contracts:**
- Direct award without competition
- Up to $5 million
- Agency must justify
- Requires certified SDVOSB

### Where to Find Opportunities

**SAM.gov:**
- Primary federal opportunity site
- Search by SDVOSB set-aside
- Filter by NAICS code
- Set up alerts

**Agency procurement forecasts:**
- Each agency publishes anticipated needs
- Plan ahead for large opportunities
- Build relationships early

**GovWin/Deltek:**
- Commercial intelligence service
- Paid but valuable
- Earlier visibility into opportunities

**SubNet:**
- SBA's subcontracting database
- Prime contractors seeking subs
- Path to larger contracts

### VA-Specific Opportunities

**Veterans First Contracting Program:**
- VA prioritizes SDVOSB/VOSB
- Largest federal buyer from veteran businesses
- Significant opportunities
- Must be VetCert verified

## Winning Government Contracts

### Capability Statement

**Essential marketing tool:**
- One-page summary of your company
- Core competencies
- Past performance
- Differentiators
- Contact information
- SDVOSB certification status
- NAICS codes

### Past Performance

**Building your track record:**
- Start small
- Subcontracting builds experience
- Commercial work counts
- Document everything
- Collect references

### Pricing Strategy

**Understanding government pricing:**
- Cost-plus vs. fixed-price
- Labor rates must be justified
- Competitive but sustainable
- Know your costs thoroughly

### Proposal Writing

**Winning proposals:**
- Follow instructions exactly
- Answer all requirements
- Demonstrate understanding
- Show capability
- Highlight veteran ownership advantage
- Professional presentation

## Compliance Requirements

### Limitations on Subcontracting

**SDVOSB prime contractors must:**
- Perform certain percentage of work themselves
- Services: 50% of cost of contract performance
- Supplies: 50% of cost of manufacturing
- General construction: 15% of cost with own employees
- Construction (specialty): 25% with own employees

### Joint Ventures

**SDVOSB joint ventures:**
- Can partner with larger companies
- Must meet specific requirements
- SDVOSB must control joint venture
- Approved mentor-protégé arrangements available

### Reporting Requirements

**Ongoing compliance:**
- Report accurately
- Maintain eligibility
- Document veteran control
- Annual certification update

## Building Your Business

### Starting Out

**Recommended approach:**

1. **Subcontracting first:**
   - Work for prime contractors
   - Build past performance
   - Learn government processes
   - Develop relationships

2. **Small contracts:**
   - Micro-purchases (under $10,000)
   - GSA Schedule (see our guide)
   - Set-aside competitions
   - Build steadily

3. **Grow strategically:**
   - Expand capabilities
   - Hire carefully
   - Maintain quality
   - Protect your certification

### Key Success Factors

**What makes SDVOSB contractors successful:**

**Technical capability:**
- Deliver quality work
- Meet deadlines
- Solve problems
- Exceed expectations

**Business acumen:**
- Know your costs
- Price appropriately
- Manage cash flow
- Plan for growth

**Relationship building:**
- Attend industry days
- Meet contracting officers
- Partner strategically
- Join industry associations

**Compliance focus:**
- Follow the rules
- Document everything
- Report accurately
- Maintain eligibility

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Pitfalls for new SDVOSB contractors:**

- **Underbidding:** Winning contracts you can't afford to perform
- **Overcommitting:** Taking more than you can handle
- **Poor documentation:** Not tracking costs and performance
- **Certification issues:** Failing to maintain eligibility
- **Cash flow problems:** Government pays slowly; plan accordingly
- **Ignoring compliance:** Violations can be serious

## The Bottom Line

Starting an SDVOSB can be rewarding:
- **Significant opportunities** in billions of set-aside dollars
- **Competitive advantage** from certification
- **Mission continuation** serving government
- **Wealth building** through business ownership

But it requires:
- **Real capability** to deliver quality work

If you have a service-connected disability, relevant experience, and entrepreneurial drive, SDVOSB certification can open significant doors.
