---# VA Disability Rating Guide: Back Conditions (Spine/Lumbar/Thoracic)

## Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

**Possible Ratings:** 10%, 20%, 30%, 40%, 50%, 60%, 100%

**Evidence Needed:**
- Medical diagnosis of spine condition (arthritis, disc disease, spinal stenosis, etc.)
- Range of motion measurements (flexion, extension, lateral bending, rotation)
- Imaging (X-rays, MRI, CT scan)
- Neurological examination (if radiculopathy present)
- Service connection proof
- Nexus linking current back condition to service
- Functional impact documentation

**Average Monthly Compensation (2025 rates, veteran without dependents):**
- 10%: $171.23
- 20%: $333.01
- 30%: $524.31
- 40%: $755.28
- 50%: $1,075.16
- 60%: $1,361.88
- 100%: $3,831.30

**Lifetime Value at 40%:** $362,534 (over 40 years)

## Understanding Back Condition VA Ratings

Back conditions are rated under 38 CFR § 4.71a with various diagnostic codes. The general formula ratings are based on range of motion (ROM) limitations and pain, with additional ratings for specific conditions like intervertebral disc syndrome (IVDS) and radiculopathy.

### Common Spine Diagnostic Codes

**DC 5235-5243:** Various spine conditions (arthritis, DDD, fractures)
**DC 5237:** Lumbar or lumbosacral spine strain (most common for general back conditions)
**DC 5238:** Spinal stenosis
**DC 5239:** Spondylolisthesis or segmental instability
**DC 5240:** Ankylosis of entire spine
**DC 5241:** Spinal fusion
**DC 5242:** Degenerative arthritis of the spine (DDD)
**DC 5243:** Intervertebral disc syndrome (IVDS)

**DC 8520:** Radiculopathy (rated separately when nerve involvement)

### General Rating Formula Based on Range of Motion

Most back conditions are rated using the general formula based on thoracolumbar spine ROM:

#### 10% Rating
**Criteria:**
- Forward flexion of thoracolumbar spine **greater than 60 degrees but not greater than 85 degrees**; OR
- Combined ROM not greater than 120 degrees; OR
- Muscle spasm or guarding on exam; OR
- Localized tenderness not resulting in abnormal gait

**What This Looks Like:**
- Moderate limitation in bending forward
- Can bend to touch knees but not floor
- Some stiffness and pain
- Can walk normally
- Can work most jobs with some difficulty
- Pain with prolonged sitting/standing

**Monthly:** $171.23

**Most Common Initial Rating** for service-connected back pain.

#### 20% Rating
**Criteria:**
- Forward flexion **greater than 30 degrees but not greater than 60 degrees**; OR
- Combined ROM not greater than 120 degrees; OR
- Muscle spasm, guarding severe enough to result in abnormal gait or spinal contour

**What This Looks Like:**
- Significant limitation bending forward
- Cannot bend to knees
- Abnormal posture or gait from pain/spasm
- Significant daily pain
- Difficulty with many work tasks
- Frequent medication use

**Monthly:** $333.01

#### 30% Rating (Rare for ROM Alone)
**Criteria:**
- Forward flexion **30 degrees or less**; OR
- Favorable ankylosis of entire thoracolumbar spine

**What This Looks Like:**
- Severe limitation - can barely bend forward
- Trunk essentially rigid
- Major functional impairment
- Significant difficulty with most activities

**Monthly:** $524.31

**Note:** 30% rare without additional factors like IVDS or radiculopathy.

#### 40% Rating
**Criteria:**
- Unfavorable ankylosis of entire thoracolumbar spine

**What This Looks Like:**
- Spine essentially fused in poor position
- Severe functional disability

**Monthly:** $755.28

**Very Rare** as standalone rating.

#### 50% Rating
**Criteria:**
- Unfavorable ankylosis of entire spine (cervical, thoracic, and lumbar)

**What This Looks Like:**
- Entire spine immobile
- Extreme functional impairment

**Monthly:** $1,075.16

**Extremely Rare** without additional complications.

#### 100% Rating
**Criteria:**
- Complete loss of spine ROM with severe disability

**What This Looks Like:**
- Unable to function independently
- Total disability from spine condition

**Monthly:** $3,831.30

**Very Rare** - reserved for most catastrophic spine injuries.

## Intervertebral Disc Syndrome (IVDS) - DC 5243

**Common Separate Rating for Disc Conditions**

IVDS can be rated at 10%, 20%, 40%, or 60% based on incapacitating episodes.

#### 10% - IVDS
**Criteria:** With or without symptoms such as pain, stiffness, or aching in the area of the spine affected by residuals of injury or disease.

#### 20% - IVDS
**Criteria:** With recurring symptoms with or without incapacitating episodes over the past 12 months.

#### 40% - IVDS
**Criteria:** With incapacitating episodes having a total duration of at least 2 weeks but less than 4 weeks during the past 12 months.

**Incapacitating Episode Defined:** Period of acute signs and symptoms severe enough to require bed rest prescribed by a physician and treatment by a physician.

#### 60% - IVDS
**Criteria:** With incapacitating episodes having a total duration of at least 4 weeks but less than 6 weeks during the past 12 months.

**Key:** Document incapacitating episodes - bed rest prescribed by physician, inability to work, acute flare-ups requiring treatment.

## Radiculopathy - DC 8520

**Separate Rating for Nerve Involvement**

When back conditions cause nerve compression with symptoms down the legs, radiculopathy can be rated separately.

**Ratings:**
- **Mild (10%):** Numbness, tingling, mild pain in leg
- **Moderate (20%):** Weakness, moderate sensory loss
- **Moderately Severe (40%):** Significant weakness, sensory loss, reflex changes
- **Severe (60%):** Marked weakness, severe sensory loss, muscle atrophy
- **Complete (80-100%):** Total loss of function, paralysis

**Common:** Sciatica (lumbar radiculopathy affecting sciatic nerve)

**Important:** Radiculopathy can be rated separately from the spine condition causing it, significantly increasing combined rating.

## Common Back Conditions and Service Connection

### 1. **Degenerative Disc Disease (DDD) / Arthritis**
**Most Common**

**Causes:**
- Heavy lifting, load carrying
- Airborne operations (repeated impact)
- Vehicle operations (vibration)
- Injury during service
- Natural aging accelerated by service

**Service Connection:**
- Direct: Onset during/after service
- Secondary: Worsening of pre-existing condition
- Aggravation: Existing arthritis made worse

**Evidence:** X-rays or MRI showing disc degeneration, facet arthritis, bone spurs

### 2. **Herniated/Bulging Disc**

**Causes:**
- Lifting injury
- Fall or accident
- Combat injury
- Cumulative microtrauma

**Symptoms:**
- Back pain
- Leg pain (sciatica)
- Numbness/tingling
- Weakness

**Evidence:** MRI showing disc herniation/bulge

**Rating:** Usually under IVDS (DC 5243) and/or radiculopathy if nerve involvement

### 3. **Spinal Stenosis - DC 5238**

**Causes:**
- Arthritis narrowing spinal canal
- Disc bulges
- Ligament hypertrophy

**Symptoms:**
- Back and leg pain
- Neurogenic claudication (leg pain with walking, relieved by sitting)
- Numbness
- Weakness

**Evidence:** MRI or CT showing canal narrowing

### 4. **Spondylolisthesis - DC 5239**

**Causes:**
- Vertebra slips forward on vertebra below
- Stress fracture (spondylolysis)
- Degenerative changes
- Traumatic injury

**Ratings:** Based on ROM and symptoms

### 5. **Compression Fracture**

**Causes:**
- Trauma (fall, accident, combat)
- Osteoporosis (can be secondary)

**Symptoms:**
- Acute pain at time of fracture
- Chronic pain and stiffness
- Height loss
- Kyphosis (hunched posture)

**Evidence:** X-ray, CT, or MRI showing fracture

### 6. **Spinal Fusion (Post-Surgical)**

**Rating:**
- Based on residual symptoms and ROM
- Often higher ratings post-fusion due to limited ROM
- IVDS rating if flare-ups continue

### 7. **Muscle Strain/Sprain**

**Causes:**
- Lifting injury
- Overuse
- Training accidents

**Chronic Effects:**
- Ongoing pain
- Muscle spasm
- ROM limitations

## Evidence Requirements

### 1. Medical Diagnosis and Imaging

**Required:**
- Medical records documenting back condition
- Imaging studies:
  - **X-rays:** Show arthritis, bone spurs, fractures, alignment
  - **MRI:** Best for disc herniations, nerve compression, soft tissue
  - **CT scan:** Bone detail, stenosis
- Orthopedic or neurosurgical evaluation

**Diagnosis Should Include:**
- Specific condition (DDD, herniated disc, stenosis, etc.)
- Level affected (L4-L5, L5-S1, etc.)
- Severity
- Nerve involvement (if any)

### 2. Range of Motion Measurements - CRITICAL

**Thoracolumbar Spine ROM:**
- **Forward flexion (bending forward):** Normal 90 degrees
- **Extension (bending backward):** Normal 30 degrees
- **Right lateral flexion:** Normal 30 degrees
- **Left lateral flexion:** Normal 30 degrees
- **Right rotation:** Normal 30 degrees
- **Left rotation:** Normal 30 degrees
- **Combined ROM:** Normal 240 degrees

**How ROM Measured:**
- Inclinometer (two devices on spine)
- Goniometer
- Measured by physician, PA, chiropractor, or physical therapist

**Pain on Motion:**
- ROM limited by pain is functional ROM
- Report pain during ROM testing

**Repeated Motion:**
- ROM may worsen with repeated use
- VA should consider this

**Flare-Ups:**
- Document ROM during flare-ups/worse periods

### 3. Neurological Examination (If Nerve Involvement)

**Tests:**
- **Straight Leg Raise (SLR):** Tests for nerve root compression
- **Sensory exam:** Pin prick, light touch in legs
- **Motor exam:** Strength testing (dorsiflexion, plantarflexion, etc.)
- **Reflexes:** Knee jerk (L4), ankle jerk (S1)
- **Special tests:** Lasegue, Bowstring sign

**Positive Findings Support Radiculopathy Rating**

### 4. Functional Impact Documentation

**Employment:**
- Lifting restrictions
- Standing/sitting limitations
- Job modifications or loss
- Reduced hours
- Disability accommodations

**Daily Life:**
- Difficulty bending (tying shoes, picking things up)
- Sitting tolerance
- Standing tolerance
- Walking limitations
- Sleep disturbance from pain
- Activities given up

**Incapacitating Episodes (for IVDS):**
- Dates of acute flare-ups
- Bed rest required
- Doctor visits during flare-ups
- Missed work
- ER visits

### 5. Service Connection Proof

**Direct Service Connection:**
- Service medical records documenting back injury or pain
- Injury during training, combat, duty
- Continuity of treatment from service to present

**Common Service Activities Causing Back Conditions:**
- Heavy load carrying (ruck marches)
- Airborne operations
- Vehicle operations
- Lifting/moving equipment
- Combat injuries
- Training accidents
- Falls

**Secondary Service Connection:**
- Back condition secondary to other service-connected conditions:
  - **Leg length discrepancy** → altered gait → back stress
  - **Foot/ankle condition** → altered gait → back problems
  - **Hip condition** → biomechanical compensation
  - **Knee condition** → gait alteration
  - **Obesity** (if secondary) → increased back stress

**Aggravation:**
- Pre-existing back condition worsened by service
- Evidence of baseline and worsening

### 6. Nexus Opinion

Medical opinion linking back condition to service or service-connected condition.

**Strong Nexus Includes:**
- Review of service records and MOS
- Review of imaging and diagnosis
- Medical explanation of connection
- "At least as likely as not" statement
- Timeline analysis

**Example Nexus:**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's lumbar degenerative disc disease is related to military service. Service records document the veteran served as an infantryman (MOS 11B) involving frequent heavy load carrying, airborne operations, and a documented back injury in 2005. The veteran's MRI shows multi-level disc degeneration at L3-L4, L4-L5, and L5-S1 consistent with repetitive microtrauma and overuse. The temporal relationship and mechanism support service connection."

## C&P Examination Preparation

### Before the Exam

**1. Gather All Medical Records:**
- Imaging reports (X-rays, MRI, CT)
- Orthopedic/neurosurgery notes
- Surgical reports (if applicable)
- Physical therapy records
- Pain management records
- Treatment history

**2. Document Functional Limitations:**
Detailed examples:
- Bending limitations (can't tie shoes, pick up items)
- Sitting tolerance (how long before pain)
- Standing tolerance
- Walking distance before pain
- Lifting restrictions
- Sleep disturbance
- Activities avoided

**3. Track Incapacitating Episodes (if applicable):**
- Dates of severe flare-ups
- Bed rest periods
- Doctor visits for acute episodes
- Work missed
- ER visits

**4. Prepare Symptom Description:**
- Location of pain (lower back, mid-back)
- Radiation (does it go down legs?)
- Quality (sharp, dull, aching, burning)
- Severity (1-10 scale)
- Frequency (constant, intermittent)
- What makes it worse/better
- Medications and effectiveness

### During the Exam

**Physical Examination:**
Examiner will:
- Observe posture and gait
- Palpate spine (feel for tenderness, muscle spasm)
- Check for muscle spasm/guarding
- Measure ROM with inclinometer
- Neurological exam (if nerve symptoms)
- Straight leg raise test
- Check reflexes, sensation, strength in legs

**DO:**
- Wear easily removable clothing
- Move naturally - don't force ROM beyond pain
- Report pain during ROM testing
- Demonstrate spasm or guarding if present
- Explain if having a good or bad day
- Describe functional limitations clearly
- Mention all symptoms (back pain AND leg symptoms)
- Bring imaging reports

**DON'T:**
- Try to "tough it out"
- Exaggerate limitations (examiner can assess)
- Minimize symptoms
- Forget to mention radiculopathy symptoms
- Forget to discuss incapacitating episodes

**Critical Information to Provide:**

1. **History:** When back problems started, connection to service
2. **Pain:** Location, severity, frequency, triggers
3. **Radiculopathy:** Leg pain, numbness, tingling, weakness
4. **ROM Limitations:** What you can't do (bend, twist, lift)
5. **Functional Impact:** Work limitations, daily life difficulties
6. **Treatment:** Surgeries, injections, medications, PT
7. **Incapacitating Episodes:** Acute flare-ups requiring bed rest/doctor care
8. **Work Impact:** How back affects job
9. **Service Connection:** How back condition relates to service

**Range of Motion Testing:**
- Examiner places inclinometers on your spine
- You bend forward, backward, side to side, rotate
- Report pain during movement
- If you can't complete movement due to pain, that's your functional ROM

## Claim Strategy

### Initial Claim Strategy

**Step 1: Get Comprehensive Evaluation**
- Orthopedic or neurosurgery examination
- Recent imaging (MRI best, X-ray minimum)
- ROM measurements
- Neurological exam if leg symptoms
- Document all symptoms

**Step 2: Determine Service Connection Path**
- **Direct:** Back injury/pain during service
- **Secondary:** Back problem from another service-connected condition
- **Aggravation:** Pre-existing condition worsened by service

**Step 3: Gather Service Evidence**
- Service medical records documenting back injury/treatment
- If no service records:
  - MOS involving back stress (infantry, airborne, combat engineer, etc.)
  - Buddy statements about injury or chronic pain
  - Lay statements about observed limitations since service

**Step 4: Obtain Nexus**
- Orthopedist, neurosurgeon, physiatrist, or primary care
- Provide service records and imaging
- Independent Medical Opinion (IMO) if needed

**Step 5: File Comprehensively**
Submit:
- VA Form 21-526EZ
- Medical records with diagnosis
- Imaging reports
- Service records documenting injury/MOS
- Nexus letter
- Personal statement with functional impact
- Lay statements
- Employment records if relevant
- Incapacitating episode log (if applicable for IVDS)

### Radiculopathy Secondary Claim

**If you have leg symptoms from back condition:**

File for both spine condition AND radiculopathy separately.

**Example:**
- Lumbar spine condition (DC 5237): 20%
- Radiculopathy left leg (DC 8520): 20%
- **Combined: 36%**

**Evidence:**
- Positive straight leg raise
- MRI showing nerve compression
- Numbness, tingling, or weakness in leg
- Abnormal reflexes or sensation
- EMG/nerve conduction study (if performed)

**This Can Significantly Increase Your Rating**

### IVDS Rating Strategy

**If you have disc herniations with flare-ups:**

Consider filing under DC 5243 (IVDS) instead of or in addition to general formula.

**Document Incapacitating Episodes:**
- Each acute flare-up
- Bed rest prescribed by doctor
- Doctor visits during episodes
- Work missed
- ER visits

**40% IVDS Requires:** 2-4 weeks total of incapacitating episodes in 12 months

**60% IVDS Requires:** 4-6 weeks total of incapacitating episodes in 12 months

**Example:**
- 4 flare-ups in year, each requiring 1 week bed rest = 4 weeks total = 60% IVDS

### Increase Rating Strategy

**When to File for Increase:**
- ROM has decreased
- Pain has worsened
- New radiculopathy developed
- Imaging shows progression (new disc herniation, worsening stenosis)
- Surgery performed (even if "successful")
- Increased incapacitating episodes
- Functional impairment increased

**Evidence for Increase:**
- New ROM measurements showing worse limitation
- Updated imaging
- Surgical records
- Increased medications
- Pain management records (injections, etc.)
- Work impact increased (job loss, reduced hours)
- Updated personal and lay statements

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: Lumbar DDD with Bilateral Radiculopathy - 60% Combined
**Background:** Army infantry veteran, 6 years active duty, chronic back and leg pain.

**Strategy:**
- MRI showed multi-level disc degeneration and disc bulges at L4-L5, L5-S1
- ROM testing: forward flexion 55 degrees (20% under general formula)
- Neurological exam: positive straight leg raise both legs, decreased sensation
- Filed for:
  - Lumbar spine: 20%
  - Radiculopathy left leg: 20%
  - Radiculopathy right leg: 20%
- Orthopedist nexus: DDD and radiculopathy from infantry service (heavy loads, marching, airborne operations)

**Result:**
- Lumbar spine: 20%
- Left radiculopathy: 20%
- Right radiculopathy: 20%
- **Combined: 49% → paid at 50%** ($1,075.16/month)

**Key Factors:** Claimed all separate rateable conditions, imaging evidence, ROM measurements, neurological findings, strong nexus.

### Case Study 2: IVDS with Incapacitating Episodes - 40%
**Background:** Marine Corps veteran, herniated disc L5-S1, frequent severe flare-ups.

**Strategy:**
- Service medical records documented back injury during training
- MRI showed large disc herniation at L5-S1
- Documented 5 incapacitating episodes over 12 months:
  - Episode 1: 5 days bed rest, doctor visit
  - Episode 2: 1 week bed rest, ER visit
  - Episode 3: 4 days bed rest, doctor visit
  - Episode 4: 3 days bed rest, doctor visit
  - Episode 5: 1 week bed rest, pain management appointment
  - **Total: 28 days = 4 weeks**
- Filed under DC 5243 (IVDS) based on incapacitating episodes
- Medical records and personal log documented each episode

**Result:** Granted 40% under IVDS ($755.28/month).

**Key Factors:** Documented incapacitating episodes totaling 4 weeks, medical records of doctor visits during flare-ups, personal log corroborated by medical records.

### Case Study 3: Post-Fusion with Continued Symptoms - 30% Increased to 50%
**Background:** Navy veteran, initially rated 20% for lumbar DDD, underwent spinal fusion, continued pain.

**Strategy:**
- Post-fusion ROM significantly limited
- ROM testing 1 year post-op: forward flexion 50 degrees
- Continued pain despite "successful" fusion
- Developed new radiculopathy symptoms
- Filed for increase with:
  - Post-op ROM measurements
  - Surgical records
  - Updated imaging
  - Neurological exam showing new radiculopathy
  - Pain management records

**Result:**
- Lumbar spine increased to 30%
- New radiculopathy rating: 20%
- **Combined: 44% → paid at 50%** ($1,075.16/month)

**Key Factors:** Don't assume surgery will reduce rating - often increases it. Documented post-surgical limitations, new radiculopathy symptoms.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I get VA disability for back pain without an injury documented in service?
Yes. If you have a current back condition and can show it's related to your service duties (heavy lifting, airborne ops, vehicle operations, etc.), you can establish service connection even without specific injury records. Nexus letter explaining the connection is key.

### What if my back condition has gotten worse since service?
This is common. Degenerative spine conditions progress over time. File for an increase showing worsening ROM, new disc herniations, radiculopathy, etc.

### Can I get separate ratings for back problems and leg problems from my back?
Yes! This is important. The spine condition (based on ROM) is rated separately from radiculopathy (nerve pain/symptoms in legs). File for both.

### What ROM measurement qualifies for 20%?
Forward flexion greater than 30 degrees but not greater than 60 degrees, OR combined ROM not greater than 120 degrees, OR muscle spasm/guarding severe enough to cause abnormal gait/spinal contour.

### Do I need an MRI to get a back rating?
Not always required, but very helpful. X-rays can show arthritis. MRI shows discs and nerve compression. Better imaging = better documentation.

### Will VA reduce my back rating if I get surgery?
Not automatically. Often surgery reveals worse damage than expected, or leaves residual symptoms, resulting in higher ratings. Don't avoid needed surgery for fear of rating reduction.

### Can back problems be secondary to other conditions?
Yes. Foot, ankle, knee, or hip problems can alter gait and biomechanics, causing back stress and arthritis. File secondary claim with medical nexus.

### What's the difference between the general formula and IVDS rating?
General formula is based on ROM limitations. IVDS is based on frequency and duration of incapacitating episodes. You may qualify for one or both. VA assigns whichever is higher.

### Can I get more than 20% for back pain alone?
Yes, but requires either very limited ROM (30 degrees or less), or IVDS with documented incapacitating episodes. Most common path to higher rating is adding radiculopathy rating separately.

### What if I have pain but good ROM?
Difficult to get high rating based only on ROM. However, if you have frequent incapacitating episodes, consider IVDS rating. Also, pain during ROM testing can limit functional ROM.

### How do I prove "incapacitating episodes" for IVDS?
Medical records of doctor visits during flare-ups, ER visits, prescriptions for acute pain/muscle relaxers, work excuses, personal log of episodes (corroborated by medical records).

### Can I get TDIU for back conditions?
Possibly. If back problems prevent substantially gainful employment, file for TDIU. Requires one condition at 60% or combined 70% with one at 40%. Back conditions with radiculopathy often qualify.

### Should I mention pain from standing, sitting, lying down?
Yes. Document all functional limitations. Standing tolerance, sitting tolerance, sleep disturbance all matter.

### What if my back pain is from multiple areas (lower and mid-back)?
Each spinal region can potentially be rated separately: cervical (neck), thoracolumbar (mid/low back), lumbar (low back). If you have distinct conditions in different regions, file for each.

## Final Thoughts

Back conditions are among the most common and disabling conditions affecting veterans. The physical demands of military service - heavy load carrying, airborne operations, vehicle operations, combat injuries - take a significant toll on the spine.

**Keys to a Successful Back Claim:**

1. **Get comprehensive evaluation** - MRI, ROM measurements, neurological exam
2. **Document all aspects** - Back pain AND leg symptoms
3. **File for everything** - Spine condition, radiculopathy, IVDS if applicable
4. **Establish service connection** - Injury or cumulative trauma from military duties
5. **Track incapacitating episodes** - If you have severe flare-ups, document them
6. **Don't delay surgery** - If needed, get it - won't hurt your rating
7. **File for increase** - As condition worsens, file for increase

Back pain is debilitating and significantly impacts quality of life and ability to work. If your back condition is related to your military service, you deserve compensation.

**File your claim. You earned it through your service.**
