## CHAPTER 1: Understanding VA PTSD Claims (The 4 Required Elements)

### Overview

The VA will NOT approve your PTSD claim unless you provide evidence for ALL FOUR of these elements:

1. **Stressor Event** (the traumatic event occurred in service)
2. **Current Diagnosis** (you have PTSD right now)
3. **Nexus** (your current PTSD is connected to the service stressor)
4. **Symptoms** (evidence of how PTSD affects you)

**Miss even ONE element = Claim Denied**

Let's break down each requirement.

---

### ELEMENT #1: STRESSOR EVENT

**What the VA Wants to Know:**
"Did a traumatic event actually occur during your military service?"

**Why This Matters:**
The VA must verify that you experienced a qualifying traumatic stressor while in service. They don't just take your word for it.

**Types of Qualifying Stressors:**

**Combat-Related Stressors** (Easiest to Prove)
- Direct combat engagement
- IED attacks or explosions
- Being fired upon
- Witnessing casualties
- Handling human remains
- Experiencing mortar/rocket attacks

**Non-Combat Stressors** (Require More Evidence)
- Military Sexual Trauma (MST)
- Physical assault
- Accidents or disasters
- Threat of injury or death
- Witnessing violence or death

**Evidence Required:**
- **Combat stressors:** Often your DD-214 showing deployment to combat zone is sufficient
- **Non-combat stressors:** Require incident reports, medical records, or buddy statements

ðŸ’¡ **Pro Tip:** If you served in a combat zone, the VA presumes you experienced combat stressors. This makes approval much easier.

---

### ELEMENT #2: CURRENT DIAGNOSIS

**What the VA Wants to Know:**
"Do you currently have PTSD diagnosed by a qualified professional?"

**Why This Matters:**
You must have an actual PTSD diagnosis from a psychiatrist, psychologist, or licensed clinical social worker. Self-diagnosis doesn't count.

**What Qualifies:**

âœ… **Diagnosis from:**
- VA psychiatrist or psychologist
- Private psychiatrist or psychologist
- Licensed clinical social worker (LCSW)
- Military mental health provider

âœ… **Diagnosis must:**
- Be based on DSM-5 criteria for PTSD
- Be current (within last 2 years preferred)
- Include diagnostic codes
- List specific symptoms

âŒ **Does NOT Qualify:**
- Self-diagnosis
- Diagnosis from primary care doctor only (not mental health specialist)
- Diagnosis of "anxiety" or "depression" without PTSD
- Decades-old diagnosis with no current treatment

ðŸ’¡ **Pro Tip:** If you haven't been formally diagnosed, schedule an appointment with a psychiatrist or psychologist NOW. Get the diagnosis before filing your claim.

---

### ELEMENT #3: NEXUS

**What the VA Wants to Know:**
"Is your current PTSD connected to the service stressor?"

**Why This Matters:**
You could have PTSD from a car accident last year. The VA needs evidence that your PTSD is **caused by or related to** your military service, not other life events.

**How to Prove Nexus:**

**Best Evidence:**
- **Nexus letter** from treating psychiatrist/psychologist stating PTSD is "at least as likely as not" related to service stressor

**Supporting Evidence:**
- Timeline showing symptoms began during or shortly after service
- No other traumatic events that could explain PTSD
- Treatment records linking symptoms to service events

**Nexus Statement Language:**
The magic words the VA wants to see:

> "It is at least as likely as not that [Veteran's Name]'s current diagnosis of PTSD is related to the in-service stressor of [specific event]."

This meets the VA's burden of proof standard (50% or greater probability).

ðŸ’¡ **Pro Tip:** A strong nexus letter is often the difference between approval and denial. We'll cover exactly how to get one in Chapter 4.

---

### ELEMENT #4: SYMPTOMS (For Rating Purposes)

**What the VA Wants to Know:**
"How severely does PTSD affect your daily life and functioning?"

**Why This Matters:**
This determines your disability rating (0%, 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, or 100%) and therefore your monthly compensation.

**Evidence of Symptoms:**
- Your own testimony (DBQ form, personal statement)
- Lay statements from spouse, family, friends
- Treatment records showing symptoms
- Work performance issues
- Social/relationship problems
- Daily living difficulties

We'll cover how to document symptoms for maximum rating in Chapter 5.

---

### The VA Rating Scale for PTSD

| Rating | Monthly Compensation (2025) | Symptom Severity |
|--------|----------------------------|------------------|
| 0% | $0 | Diagnosed but no functional impairment |
| 10% | $171 | Mild symptoms, minimal impairment |
| 30% | $524 | Occasional symptoms, some impairment |
| 50% | $1,075 | Moderate symptoms, significant impairment |
| 70% | $1,716 | Severe symptoms, major impairment |
| 100% | $3,737 | Total occupational and social impairment |

**The difference between 50% and 70% is $641/month = $7,692/year.**

Proper symptom documentation is worth tens of thousands of dollars.

## CHAPTER 2: Stressor Evidence - Proving Your Trauma

### Overview

The VA must verify your stressor actually occurred. The evidence you need depends on whether it was combat-related or non-combat.

### COMBAT STRESSORS (Easiest to Prove)

#### What Qualifies as Combat

âœ… Serving in combat zone during wartime
âœ… Direct enemy engagement
âœ… Indirect fire (mortars, rockets, IEDs)
âœ… Witnessing casualties or death
âœ… Handling remains
âœ… Convoys or patrols in hostile areas

#### Evidence That Proves Combat Service

**Tier 1 Evidence (Strongest):**

âœ… **DD-214 showing:**
- Deployment to combat zone
- Combat service medals (Combat Infantryman Badge, Combat Action Badge, Combat Action Ribbon)
- Campaign medals (Iraq Campaign Medal, Afghanistan Campaign Medal)

âœ… **Unit Records showing:**
- Deployment orders

**This is often enough.** If your DD-214 shows combat deployment, the VA generally accepts you experienced combat stressors.

**Tier 2 Evidence (Supporting):**

âœ… Orders assigning you to combat unit
âœ… Awards for valor or combat service
âœ… Photos from deployment (with context)
âœ… News articles about unit operations
âœ… Official unit histories

**You don't need to prove every specific incident.** The VA presumes combat veterans experienced stressors.

#### Personal Statement for Combat PTSD

Even with strong DD-214 evidence, write a personal statement describing:
- Where you deployed and when
- General nature of combat exposure (firefights, IEDs, casualties)
- Specific incidents that affected you most (if you're comfortable sharing)
- When symptoms began

**Example:**

> "I deployed to Afghanistan from June 2012 to March 2013 with 3rd Battalion, 5th Marines. Our unit conducted daily patrols in Sangin District, one of the most hostile areas in Helmand Province. During my deployment, I experienced approximately 15-20 IED explosions, multiple firefights, and witnessed three members of my platoon killed in action. One incident on November 3, 2012, particularly affected me when..."

You don't need to write a novel. 1-2 pages is sufficient.

ðŸ’¡ **Pro Tip:** Be specific about locations and timeframes, but you don't have to relive every traumatic detail in your personal statement. The C&P examiner will ask for details.

### HOW TO OBTAIN STRESSOR EVIDENCE

#### 1. Request Your Military Personnel Records

**What to Request:**
- Complete military personnel file (OMPF)
- Medical records
- Unit assignment records
- Deployment records

**How to Request:**
- **eVetRecs:** https://www.archives.gov/veterans/military-service-records
- **National Personnel Records Center**
- **Turnaround:** 2-4 weeks (up to 6 months for older records)

ðŸ’¡ **Start this process EARLY.** It takes time.

#### 2. Request Unit Records

If your incident involved your entire unit (combat, accident, etc.):
- Contact unit historian
- Request deployment records
- Request unit after-action reports

#### 3. Search News Archives

For significant events:
- Google news archives
- Military Times archives
- Local news from deployment area
- Unit Facebook pages or veteran groups

#### 4. FOIA Requests (If Needed)

If records aren't in your personnel file:
- File Freedom of Information Act request
- Specify: incident reports, safety investigations, law enforcement reports
- **Timeline:** 60-90 days

### What If You Were Diagnosed Years Ago?

**If you have old diagnosis but no recent treatment:**
- Get **current evaluation** confirming PTSD still present
- VA wants to know you have PTSD **now**, not just that you had it years ago

**If you've been in ongoing treatment:**
- Request records from last 2 years
- Most recent diagnosis is acceptable
- Ongoing treatment records actually strengthen claim

---

### Treatment Records as Supporting Evidence

**Why Treatment Records Help:**

1. **Confirm ongoing PTSD** (not just old diagnosis)
2. **Document symptoms** over time
3. **Show functional impairment** (work issues, relationship problems mentioned in therapy notes)
4. **Demonstrate attempts to manage condition**

**What to Request from Your Provider:**
âœ… Diagnosis letter
âœ… Treatment summaries (last 6-12 months)
âœ… Therapy notes mentioning symptoms (if provider willing to share)
âœ… Medication records

ðŸ’¡ **Pro Tip:** You don't need years of treatment to file a claim. Even 2-3 months of treatment provides valuable evidence.

### What If You've Never Sought Treatment?

**Many veterans haven't sought treatment due to:**
- Stigma
- Toughness mentality
- Not realizing symptoms were PTSD
- Lack of access to mental health care

**This doesn't disqualify you.** But you need to:

1. **Get diagnosed NOW** before filing
2. **Explain the delay** in your personal statement

**Example Statement:**

> "For 10 years after my deployment, I did not seek mental health treatment. I believed seeking help was a sign of weakness, and I tried to manage my symptoms on my own through alcohol and isolation. It wasn't until my wife threatened to leave due to my anger and withdrawal that I finally sought help in 2023. I was diagnosed with PTSD in March 2024."

The VA understands delayed treatment for PTSD is common among veterans.
