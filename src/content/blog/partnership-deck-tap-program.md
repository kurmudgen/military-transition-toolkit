### Slide 2: TAP Program Performance Gaps

**Headline:** "Current TAP Outcomes Show Room for Improvement"

**Content:**

**GAO Findings (2023-2024):**
- **25%** of servicemembers required to attend TAP don't show up
- **70%** don't start TAP >1 year in advance as required
- **DOD not using** available performance data to improve outcomes
- **Limited evidence** that federal transition programs are effective

**Servicemember Feedback:**
- **Only 15%** felt TAP adequately prepared them for civilian employment
- **40-75%** describe military transition as difficult or very difficult
- **47%** of post-9/11 veterans say transition was somewhat to very difficult

**Employment Outcomes:**
- **43%** leave first civilian job within one year
- **60%+** are underemployed relative to their skills and experience
- **12.3%** unemployment for veterans ages 18-24 (vs 9.3% non-veterans)

**Program Scope Limitations:**
- Narrow focus primarily on employment/education/benefits
- Little attention to family needs, financial management, housing
- One-size-fits-all approach doesn't account for individual backgrounds
- Limited follow-up post-separation

**Visual Suggestion:**
Split screen showing GAO statistics on left, servicemember outcomes on right, with highlighted numbers in red/amber to show areas needing improvement

**Presenter Notes:**
"Let's start with the data. The GAO has documented significant challenges with current TAP programs. Nearly a quarter of servicemembers who are required to attend don't show up—particularly those who need the most support. Seventy percent don't start TAP a year in advance as required. And critically, DOD isn't effectively using performance data to understand why and take corrective action.

From the servicemember perspective, only 15% feel TAP adequately prepared them for civilian employment. Almost half of post-9/11 veterans describe their transition as difficult or very difficult.

The employment outcomes reflect this. Forty-three percent leave their first civilian job within a year. Over 60% are underemployed. Young veterans face unemployment rates significantly higher than their non-veteran peers.

The program itself has scope limitations. It focuses narrowly on employment, education, and benefits, with little attention to the broader challenges of transition—family adjustment, financial management beyond budgeting basics, housing in expensive civilian markets, and dealing with trauma responses.

Your installation is likely doing excellent work within these constraints. But the constraints themselves are the problem. This is where supplemental digital tools can make a significant difference."

- TAP focuses on first job, not career trajectory

- No tools for ongoing career development

- Benefits vary dramatically by state
- TAP can't cover all 50 states comprehensively
- Servicemembers often don't know destination state during TAP

**What Servicemembers Tell Us:**
> "TAP gave me a resume template and overview of benefits, but I left still not knowing what civilian jobs I was qualified for or how to find them."

But let's talk honestly about what's missing.

First, personalized career guidance. TAP delivers the same content to an Aviation Electrician's Mate and an Infantry Marine. It doesn't account for the dramatically different civilian opportunities and credentialing pathways these backgrounds create.

Fourth, family support. We know military spouse unemployment is 21%—three times the national average. TAP doesn't adequately address family transition challenges.

Fifth, long-term career planning. TAP focuses on getting the first job, but doesn't help servicemembers understand career trajectories, advancement pathways, or how education aligns with career goals over time.

And sixth, state-specific information. Benefits vary dramatically by state, but TAP can't cover all 50 states comprehensively, and servicemembers often don't know their destination state during TAP.

Listen to what transitioning servicemembers tell us. [Read quotes]

This isn't a criticism of your TAP program. It's recognition that the program, as currently structured and resourced, cannot possibly address all these needs. That's where digital supplemental tools come in."
