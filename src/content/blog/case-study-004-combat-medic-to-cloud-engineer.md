---# Combat Medic to $110K Cloud Security Engineer in 10 Months

## Quick Facts

**Background:**
- Branch: Army
- Rank at Separation: E5 (Sergeant)
- MOS/AFSC/Rating: 68W (Combat Medic Specialist)
- Years of Service: 8 years
- Separation Type: ETS (End of Term of Service)
- Location: Fort Bragg, NC → Remote (Austin-based company)

**Challenge:**
Transitioning from healthcare to technology felt like an impossible leap with no obvious connection between medicine and cloud computing.

**Outcome:**
Landed Cloud Security Engineer role earning $110,000 at a healthcare tech company, combining medical knowledge with cloud architecture expertise.

**Timeline:**
10 months from certification study start to job offer

## Background Story

### Military Career

Andre spent 8 years as an Army combat medic, completing three combat deployments where he provided emergency medical care under fire. He saved lives, managed medical supplies, trained junior medics, and worked in field hospitals treating everything from minor injuries to life-threatening trauma.

After his third deployment, Andre was assigned to a clinic at Fort Bragg where he managed patient records, coordinated care between providers, and became the "tech guy" troubleshooting electronic health record (EHR) systems when they malfunctioned. He taught himself basic IT skills by watching YouTube videos and reading forums.

During this time, Andre became frustrated with how vulnerable healthcare systems were to cyber attacks. He read news stories about hospitals shut down by ransomware and patient data breaches. He wondered: "Someone needs to secure these systems. Could that be me?"

As his ETS approached, Andre faced a crossroads: pursue civilian nursing (the obvious path) or take a risk on cybersecurity and cloud computing, fields he was passionate about but had no formal credentials in.

### The Challenge

Andre's primary challenge was the perception gap. He was a medic, not an IT professional. He had no degree in computer science, no professional tech experience, and no certifications. How could he convince tech employers to take him seriously?

His second challenge was choosing the right path. Cybersecurity? Cloud computing? DevOps? Networking? The tech industry had dozens of specializations, and he didn't know which fit his interests and background best.

The third challenge was financial. Cloud certifications weren't cheap, and with a family to support, he couldn't afford to spend thousands on training that might not lead to employment. He needed a strategic, cost-effective approach.

Finally, Andre worried about the job search. Even if he got certified, would companies hire someone transitioning from healthcare? Would they see his medic background as irrelevant?

## Key Tactics That Worked

### Tactic 1: The Healthcare-Tech Intersection

**What They Did:**
Andre positioned himself at the intersection of healthcare and technology. Instead of competing as a generic cloud engineer against CS graduates, he became the cloud engineer who deeply understood healthcare workflows, patient privacy, and clinical requirements. He targeted only healthcare tech companies where his medical background was an asset, not ignored.

**Why It Worked:**
Healthcare tech companies struggle to find engineers who understand both technology and healthcare. Most engineers know AWS but have never read a patient chart or understood HIPAA from a clinical perspective. Andre's unique combination made him irreplaceable. His medical knowledge informed better architectural decisions for healthcare applications.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Identify the intersection of your military specialty and tech. Combat arms + cybersecurity = threat assessment and defense. Logistics + cloud = scalable distributed systems. Intelligence + data science = analysis and pattern recognition. Don't abandon your domain expertise—leverage it. Target companies in your domain that need tech talent who understand the business context.

### Tactic 2: AWS Certification Speed Run

**What They Did:**
Andre achieved two AWS certifications (Solutions Architect Associate and Security Specialty) in 7 months while working full-time. He studied 25-30 hours/week, used high-quality paid courses ($40-50 total), built hands-on labs, and scheduled exams strategically to maintain momentum.

**Why It Worked:**
AWS certifications are highly respected in the industry and don't require years of experience to earn. They provided immediate credibility that Andre knew cloud computing, bypassing the "need experience to get experience" catch-22. The Security Specialty certification differentiated him from candidates with only foundational certs. Together, they made him interview-ready in under a year.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Start with AWS Certified Solutions Architect Associate (most foundational and valuable). Use Adrian Cantrill's course ($40) and Tutorials Dojo practice exams ($12). Study 20-25 hours/week and pass within 2-3 months. Immediately pursue a specialty certification matching your target role (Security, DevOps, Data Analytics). Two certs within 6-9 months positions you competitively. Don't over-certify before job searching—two strong certs are enough to start.

### Tactic 3: Building in Public

**What They Did:**
Andre documented his learning journey on Medium, publishing articles about cloud security, healthcare tech, and veteran transitions. He shared portfolio projects on GitHub with detailed README files explaining architectural decisions. He engaged on LinkedIn and Twitter, commenting on cloud security posts and connecting with professionals.

**Why It Worked:**
Building in public established credibility and visibility. Recruiters found Andre through his Medium articles. Hiring managers reviewed his GitHub projects during interviews. His public presence demonstrated communication skills and thought leadership—rare for junior engineers. He was no longer just another applicant; he was a recognized voice in healthcare cloud security.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Start a blog (Medium, Dev.to, Hashnode—all free). Write about what you're learning, even as a beginner. "What I Learned Building My First AWS VPC" is valuable content. Share portfolio projects on GitHub with clear documentation. Post your learning wins on LinkedIn. Engage authentically—comment on others' posts, share interesting articles, ask questions. Consistency matters more than perfection. Building in public differentiates you from silent job seekers.

### Tactic 4: Portfolio Over Résumé

**What They Did:**
Andre built a portfolio project demonstrating real-world skills: a secure, HIPAA-compliant telemedicine application architecture on AWS. The project included: VPC design, EC2/ECS compute, RDS database, S3 storage encryption, IAM access controls, CloudTrail logging, AWS KMS key management, and VPN connectivity. He documented everything on GitHub with architecture diagrams and wrote a blog post explaining design decisions.

**Why It Worked:**
Hiring managers could see exactly what Andre could build. During technical interviews, he walked through his portfolio project, demonstrating hands-on expertise rather than just theoretical knowledge. The project answered the question: "Can you actually do this job?" The answer was clearly yes. Portfolio projects outweigh years of experience for demonstrating capability.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Build 2-3 substantial portfolio projects demonstrating skills for your target role. Cloud engineer: Deploy multi-tier application with security best practices. DevOps: Set up CI/CD pipeline with automated testing. Data engineer: Build ETL pipeline processing real datasets. Document thoroughly on GitHub. Write blog posts explaining your approach. In interviews, reference specific projects. Show, don't just tell.

### Tactic 5: Strategic Company Targeting

**What They Did:**
Andre didn't apply to FAANG companies or generic tech firms. He specifically targeted healthcare tech companies: EHR providers, telemedicine platforms, medical device software, health insurance tech. He researched each company's products, identified security challenges in their domain, and tailored applications to demonstrate understanding of their specific needs.

**Why It Worked:**
Healthcare tech companies valued Andre's medical background and healthcare knowledge. Generic tech companies would have seen him as "just another bootcamp grad with AWS certs." By targeting healthcare tech specifically, he turned his medic experience into a competitive advantage. He wasn't competing against all cloud engineers—he was competing in a narrower niche where he had unique value.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Don't apply to all tech jobs generically. Identify companies and sectors where your military domain expertise adds value. Logistics background? Target supply chain tech companies. Aviation experience? Target aerospace software firms. Intelligence background? Target cybersecurity or data analytics companies. Research the company's challenges and explain in your application how your unique background helps solve them.

## Obstacles Overcome

### Obstacle 1: Imposter Syndrome as Career Changer

**The Problem:**
Andre constantly felt like a fraud. He was surrounded by people with CS degrees and years of professional tech experience. When he joined cloud security forums, others discussed concepts he'd never heard of. He hesitated to apply to jobs, convinced he wasn't "technical enough."

**How They Overcame It:**
Andre's breakthrough came from reframing: "I'm not lacking CS knowledge—I have complementary healthcare knowledge that most engineers don't have. I'm not behind; I'm different, and different is valuable." He also kept an "evidence file" documenting every lab he completed, every certification he earned, and every positive interaction. When doubt crept in, he reviewed the evidence.

He also connected with other career changers in tech who shared similar feelings. Realizing imposter syndrome was universal—not unique to him—helped normalize the feeling.

**Lesson Learned:**
Imposter syndrome is a sign of growth, not inadequacy. Career changers bring unique perspectives that traditional paths don't provide. Your "non-traditional" background is your competitive advantage, not a weakness. Document your progress to combat self-doubt. Connect with others who've made similar transitions. The feeling doesn't disappear; you just learn to act despite it.

### Obstacle 2: First Technical Interview Failure

**The Problem:**
Andre's first technical interview asked him to design a secure three-tier web application on AWS in 45 minutes on a whiteboard (virtual). He froze, forgot basic concepts, and couldn't articulate his thought process. The interviewer ended early. Andre was devastated and questioned whether he'd made a huge mistake.

**How They Overcame It:**
Andre's mentor normalized the experience: "Everyone bombs their first technical interview. It's a rite of passage. You learn what to expect." Andre scheduled mock interviews with three peers from his AWS study group. He practiced thinking out loud while designing architectures. He learned to start with requirements gathering ("What's the expected traffic? What's the data sensitivity? What's the budget?") before jumping to solutions.

By his fifth technical interview, Andre felt conversational. He still got nervous, but he had frameworks for structuring his responses.

**Lesson Learned:**
Technical interviews are a distinct skill from technical competence. Failure is feedback, not judgment. Schedule mock interviews early to desensitize yourself to pressure. Practice verbalizing your thought process while solving problems. Ask clarifying questions before architecting solutions. First interviews are learning experiences; each one improves performance.

### Obstacle 3: Choosing Between Security and General Cloud Engineering

**The Problem:**
After earning his Solutions Architect Associate certification, Andre debated whether to specialize in security or remain a generalist. Security roles paid more but were more competitive. Generalist roles offered broader opportunities but potentially slower career growth.

**How They Overcame It:**
Andre reached out to five cloud professionals on LinkedIn asking for their perspectives. The consensus: "Specialize early, especially if you have a unique angle. Generalists are commodity; specialists are valuable." Given his healthcare background and interest in protecting patient data, security was the obvious specialization. He invested in the AWS Security Specialty certification and never looked back.

**Lesson Learned:**
Specialization beats generalization, especially early in a tech career. Choose a specialization aligned with your interests and background. The market rewards depth in one area more than shallow knowledge across many. You can always broaden later, but starting specialized accelerates growth and increases earnings potential.

- Have basic computer literacy and comfort with technology

## Key Takeaways

### For You to Remember

1. **Leverage Domain Expertise with Tech**: Don't abandon your military specialty—combine it with tech skills. Healthcare + cloud, logistics + data, intelligence + security. Intersection positioning creates unique value.

2. **AWS Certifications Are Fast Track**: Two AWS certifications (Solutions Architect Associate + one specialty) in 6-9 months provides entry-level credibility. Faster and cheaper than degree programs.

3. **Building in Public Establishes Credibility**: Blog about your learning, share projects on GitHub, engage on LinkedIn. Public presence differentiates you from silent candidates and attracts opportunities.

4. **Portfolio Projects Beat Work History**: Employers hire based on what you can build. Portfolio projects demonstrating real-world skills outweigh lack of professional experience for entry-level roles.

5. **Target Companies Where Your Background Matters**: Apply to companies in your domain (healthcare, defense, logistics) where military expertise is asset. You're not "just another" cloud engineer—you're the cloud engineer who understands their world.

- **Healthcare-tech positioning**: Unique intersection made him valuable rather than commodity
