---# VA Disability Rating Guide: Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

## Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

**Possible Ratings:** 0%, 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, 100%

**Evidence Needed:**
- Current PTSD diagnosis from qualified mental health professional
- In-service stressor event (combat, MST, personal assault, etc.)
- Nexus linking current PTSD to service
- Detailed treatment records showing symptoms and functional impact
- Lay statements describing daily life impairment
- DBQ (Disability Benefits Questionnaire) for PTSD

**Average Monthly Compensation (2025 rates, veteran without dependents):**
- 30%: $524.31
- 50%: $1,075.16
- 70%: $1,759.19
- 100%: $3,831.30

**Lifetime Value at 100%:** $1,835,824 (over 40 years)

## Understanding PTSD VA Ratings

PTSD is rated under 38 CFR ยง 4.130, Diagnostic Code 9411. The VA evaluates PTSD based on the frequency, severity, and duration of symptoms and their impact on occupational and social functioning.

### Rating Criteria Breakdown

#### 0% Rating
**Criteria:** A mental condition has been formally diagnosed, but symptoms are not severe enough to interfere with occupational and social functioning or to require continuous medication.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Diagnosed PTSD but minimal symptoms
- No medication needed or only occasional use
- Full-time employment maintained
- Social relationships intact
- No significant functional impairment

#### 10% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment due to mild or transient symptoms which decrease work efficiency and ability to perform occupational tasks only during periods of significant stress, or symptoms controlled by continuous medication.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Mild anxiety or depression
- Occasional difficulty concentrating
- Minor sleep disturbance
- Symptoms worsen under stress but generally manageable
- Medication helps control symptoms
- Can maintain employment with some difficulty during stressful periods

#### 30% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment with occasional decrease in work efficiency and intermittent periods of inability to perform occupational tasks (although generally functioning satisfactorily, with routine behavior, self-care, and conversation normal), due to such symptoms as: depressed mood, anxiety, suspiciousness, panic attacks (weekly or less often), chronic sleep impairment, mild memory loss (such as forgetting names, directions, recent events).

**What This Looks Like:**
- Noticeable symptoms affecting work performance
- Depressed mood or anxiety present regularly
- Panic attacks occur weekly or less
- Sleep problems are chronic
- Mild memory issues
- Can work but with decreased efficiency
- Social life somewhat affected
- Self-care generally maintained

#### 50% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment with reduced reliability and productivity due to such symptoms as: flattened affect; circumstantial, circumlocutory, or stereotyped speech; panic attacks more than once a week; difficulty in understanding complex commands; impairment of short- and long-term memory (e.g., retention of only highly learned material, forgetting to complete tasks); impaired judgment; impaired abstract thinking; disturbances of motivation and mood; difficulty in establishing and maintaining effective work and social relationships.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Significant work performance issues
- Frequent panic attacks (more than weekly)
- Memory problems affecting daily tasks
- Difficulty maintaining relationships
- Reduced reliability at work
- May miss work frequently
- Social isolation beginning
- Emotional numbness or inappropriate affect
- Difficulty following complex instructions

#### 70% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment, with deficiencies in most areas, such as work, school, family relations, judgment, thinking, or mood, due to such symptoms as: suicidal ideation; obsessional rituals which interfere with routine activities; speech intermittently illogical, obscure, or irrelevant; near-continuous panic or depression affecting the ability to function independently, appropriately and effectively; impaired impulse control (such as unprovoked irritability with periods of violence); spatial disorientation; neglect of personal appearance and hygiene; difficulty in adapting to stressful circumstances (including work or a worklike setting); inability to establish and maintain effective relationships.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Unable to maintain employment or only able to work with significant accommodations
- Deficiencies in most areas of life
- Severe depression or near-constant panic
- Suicidal thoughts (with or without plan)
- Cannot maintain relationships effectively
- May have violent outbursts
- Personal hygiene declining
- Cannot handle stress
- Severe memory and concentration problems
- Social isolation is severe

#### 100% Rating
**Criteria:** Total occupational and social impairment, due to such symptoms as: gross impairment in thought processes or communication; persistent delusions or hallucinations; grossly inappropriate behavior; persistent danger of hurting self or others; intermittent inability to perform activities of daily living (including maintenance of minimal personal hygiene); disorientation to time or place; memory loss for names of close relatives, own occupation, or own name.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Cannot work at all
- Cannot care for self consistently
- Severe symptoms dominating daily life
- May be actively suicidal or homicidal
- Possible hallucinations or delusions
- Cannot maintain personal hygiene regularly
- Disoriented to time/place
- Extreme memory loss
- Completely isolated socially
- Requires supervision or assistance for daily living

## Evidence Requirements

### 1. Current Diagnosis
You need a formal PTSD diagnosis from a qualified mental health professional (psychiatrist, psychologist, or licensed clinical social worker). The diagnosis must be documented in medical records.

**Best Practices:**
- Seek treatment from VA mental health services or private providers
- Ensure diagnosis is clearly stated in records
- Get ongoing treatment to document symptom progression
- Request DBQ (Disability Benefits Questionnaire) completion

### 2. In-Service Stressor
You must establish that a stressful event occurred during service. The type of stressor affects evidence requirements:

**Combat Stressor:**
- If you engaged in combat, your testimony alone may be sufficient
- Combat can be verified through service records, medals, or unit history
- Easier standard of proof than non-combat stressors

**Military Sexual Trauma (MST):**
- Your testimony is crucial
- Markers of MST in service records help (medical visits, behavior changes, requests for transfer)
- Secondary evidence (friend/family statements, contemporaneous journals)
- VA provides special consideration for MST claims

**Personal Assault:**
- Similar to MST in evidence requirements
- Behavioral markers in service records
- Lay statements from people who knew you during/after service

**Non-Combat Stressor:**
- Must provide more detailed corroboration
- Service records, buddy statements, unit histories
- Morning reports, duty logs, etc.

**Fear of Hostile Military or Terrorist Activity:**
- Must be consistent with circumstances of service
- Does not need corroboration if consistent with places, types, and circumstances of service

### 3. Nexus (Link) to Service
A medical professional must provide an opinion that your PTSD is "at least as likely as not" (50% or greater probability) related to your military service.

**Best Practices:**
- Ask your treating provider for a nexus letter
- Provide your provider with service records and stressor information
- Independent Medical Opinion (IMO) if VA denies
- Nexus letters should be detailed and reference specific events and symptoms

### 4. Functional Impact Documentation
This is critical for higher ratings. Document how PTSD affects your daily life:

**Employment Records:**
- Performance reviews showing decline
- Attendance records
- Disciplinary actions
- Termination notices
- Inability to maintain employment

**Medical Treatment Records:**
- Frequency of appointments
- Medications prescribed
- Hospitalizations or ER visits
- Crisis interventions
- Therapy notes showing symptom severity

**Personal Statements:**
- Daily routine description
- Specific examples of how PTSD affects you
- Work difficulties
- Relationship problems
- Social isolation
- Self-care issues

**Lay Statements from Family/Friends:**
- Before and after service observations
- Specific behavioral changes
- Impact on relationships
- Daily life limitations they've witnessed

## Secondary Conditions Linked to PTSD

PTSD rarely exists in isolation. File for these commonly associated conditions:

### 1. **Major Depressive Disorder**
- Often co-occurs with PTSD
- Can be rated separately if symptoms are distinct
- Look for: persistent sadness, loss of interest, suicidal thoughts

### 2. **Generalized Anxiety Disorder**
- Excessive worry beyond PTSD-specific triggers
- Can be rated separately with distinct symptoms
- Look for: constant worry, restlessness, fatigue

### 3. **Sleep Apnea**
- Studies show PTSD significantly increases sleep apnea risk
- File as secondary to PTSD
- Requires sleep study for diagnosis
- Can be rated up to 50% with CPAP use

### 4. **Insomnia (Sleep Disturbance)**
- Usually considered part of PTSD symptoms
- Can be rated separately if particularly severe
- Document chronic sleep problems

### 5. **Migraine Headaches**
- Common in PTSD sufferers
- Can be secondary to PTSD stress/anxiety
- Rate up to 50% (30% most common)
- Requires specific frequency documentation

### 6. **Temporomandibular Joint Disorder (TMJ)**
- From teeth grinding/jaw clenching related to PTSD anxiety
- Document nightguard use
- Dental records showing tooth damage
- Can be rated 10-40%

### 7. **Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)**
- Strong gut-brain connection to anxiety/PTSD
- Document GI symptoms and their frequency
- Can be rated up to 30%

### 8. **Erectile Dysfunction**
- PTSD and antidepressants can cause ED
- Common but underreported
- Can be rated up to 20%
- Urology evaluation helpful

### 9. **Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)**
- Chronic stress from PTSD can cause/worsen hypertension
- Requires blood pressure monitoring records
- Can be rated up to 60%

### 10. **Obesity**
- PTSD medications and decreased activity can lead to weight gain
- Secondary to PTSD and/or medications
- Document weight gain timeline
- Can qualify for SMC if severe

### 11. **Substance Abuse Disorders**
- Self-medication for PTSD symptoms
- Can be rated separately
- Must be in recovery for rating
- Be honest about substance use history

### 12. **Chronic Pain Syndrome**
- PTSD alters pain perception
- Widespread unexplained pain
- Can be rated separately if distinct

## C&P Examination Preparation

The Compensation & Pension (C&P) exam is critical. The examiner will assess your symptoms and functional limitations.

### Before the Exam

**1. Prepare a Symptom List**
Write down all symptoms you experience, including frequency and severity:
- Intrusive thoughts/memories
- Nightmares
- Flashbacks
- Avoidance behaviors
- Emotional numbness
- Hypervigilance
- Exaggerated startle response
- Concentration problems
- Memory issues
- Sleep disturbances
- Mood changes
- Relationship difficulties
- Anger/irritability
- Panic attacks
- Suicidal thoughts

**2. Document Functional Impairment**
Prepare specific examples of how PTSD affects:
- Work (missed days, performance issues, job loss)
- Relationships (divorce, isolation, conflict)
- Self-care (hygiene, eating, medical care)
- Social activities (avoidance, withdrawal)
- Daily tasks (shopping, errands, appointments)

**3. Bring Supporting Documents**
- List of medications
- Recent treatment records
- Lay statements from family/friends
- Symptom journal
- Employment records

**4. Be Honest and Detailed**
- Don't minimize symptoms
- Don't exaggerate
- Describe your worst days, not your best
- Give specific examples
- Answer all questions fully

### During the Exam

**DO:**
- Arrive on time
- Be respectful and cooperative
- Describe symptoms honestly
- Give specific examples
- Explain functional impact
- Mention all symptoms, even if minor
- Discuss secondary conditions
- Let the examiner know if you're having a "better day"

**DON'T:**
- Minimize your symptoms to appear strong
- Exaggerate (will hurt credibility)
- Refuse to answer questions
- Be hostile or confrontational
- Rush through the exam
- Forget to mention important symptoms
- Hide medication or treatment history

### What the Examiner Assesses

The C&P examiner will evaluate:
1. **Criterion A: Stressor Event** - Trauma exposure
2. **Criterion B: Intrusion Symptoms** - Flashbacks, nightmares
3. **Criterion C: Avoidance** - Avoiding triggers
4. **Criterion D: Negative Changes in Mood/Cognition** - Negative beliefs, blame, decreased interest
5. **Criterion E: Arousal/Reactivity** - Hypervigilance, irritability
6. **Functional Impairment** - Work, social, self-care impact
7. **Symptom Frequency and Severity**

## Claim Strategy

### Initial Claim Strategy

**1. Establish Service Connection First**
Focus on proving:
- Current PTSD diagnosis (get treatment NOW)
- In-service stressor (gather evidence)
- Nexus (get medical opinion)

**2. Build Medical Evidence**
- Begin treatment immediately if not already in care
- Attend appointments consistently
- Be honest with providers about symptom severity
- Request DBQ completion by your provider

**3. Gather Lay Evidence**
- Write detailed personal statement
- Get statements from spouse, family, friends, coworkers
- Include buddy statements if stressor needs corroboration

**4. File Thoroughly**
Submit:
- VA Form 21-526EZ
- Medical records showing diagnosis
- Service records documenting stressor
- Nexus letter from provider
- Personal statement
- Lay statements
- Any other supporting evidence

### Increase Rating Strategy

If your rating is too low:

**1. File for Increase When:**
- Symptoms have worsened
- Functional impairment has increased
- You have at least 6 months of new medical evidence showing worsening

**2. Document Worsening:**
- Increased treatment frequency
- Medication changes/increases
- Hospitalizations
- Job loss or reduced hours
- Relationship breakdowns
- Self-care decline

**3. New Medical Evidence:**
- Updated treatment records
- New DBQ showing increased severity
- Hospitalization records
- Crisis intervention records
- Therapist letters documenting decline

**4. Update Personal and Lay Statements:**
- How symptoms have worsened
- Increased functional limitations
- New impacts on life
- Specific examples of decline

### Appeal Strategy

If denied or rated too low:

**1. Supplemental Claim (New Evidence)**
- Submit new medical evidence
- Get Independent Medical Opinion (IMO)
- Add missing lay statements
- Provide stressor corroboration if that was issue

**2. Higher-Level Review (No New Evidence)**
- VA reviews same evidence with fresh eyes
- Point out errors in original decision
- No new evidence allowed

**3. Board Appeal**
- Three options: Direct Review, Evidence Submission, Hearing
- Evidence Submission allows new evidence
- Hearing allows you to testify
- Takes longer but allows full presentation

### Special Consideration: Unemployability (TDIU)

If PTSD prevents you from working but you're rated less than 100%, you may qualify for TDIU (Total Disability Individual Unemployability), which pays at the 100% rate.

**TDIU Requirements:**
- At least one condition rated 60%, OR
- Multiple conditions totaling 70% with one at 40%
- Unable to maintain substantially gainful employment due to service-connected disabilities

**TDIU Evidence Needed:**
- Employment history showing inability to work
- Termination notices citing performance/attendance
- Medical opinions stating you cannot work
- Vocational expert opinion (helpful but not required)

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: Combat PTSD - 70% to 100%
**Background:** Marine Corps veteran, Iraq War, initially rated 30% for PTSD.

**Strategy:**
- Continued consistent mental health treatment
- Documented job loss and inability to maintain employment
- Spouse provided detailed lay statement about behavioral changes
- Filed for increase with 2 years of new treatment records
- Obtained DBQ from private psychiatrist showing severe symptoms

**Result:** Increased to 70%, then 100% after additional evidence of daily living impairment. Currently receiving $3,831.30/month.

**Key Factors:** Consistent treatment documentation, detailed lay statements, clear evidence of occupational impairment.

### Case Study 2: MST-Related PTSD - Initial 70% Rating
**Background:** Army veteran, experienced MST during basic training, no service medical records of assault.

**Strategy:**
- Filed with personal statement detailing MST
- Buddy statement from fellow trainee corroborating behavioral changes
- Documented markers in service record (request for early discharge, behavioral issues)
- 5 years of consistent mental health treatment post-service
- Lay statements from mother and husband describing life impact

**Result:** Service-connected at 70% on initial claim. Now receiving $1,759.19/month plus unemployability benefits.

**Key Factors:** Detailed personal testimony, behavioral markers, consistent treatment, strong lay evidence.

### Case Study 3: PTSD with Secondary Conditions - 94% Combined
**Background:** Navy veteran, Gulf War, initially rated 50% for PTSD.

**Strategy:**
- Filed increase to 70% with updated treatment records
- Simultaneously filed secondary claims for:
  - Sleep apnea secondary to PTSD (50%)
  - IBS secondary to PTSD (10%)
  - Migraine headaches secondary to PTSD (30%)
- All secondary claims granted

**Result:** PTSD increased to 70%, secondary conditions added, combined rating 94% (rounded to 100% for pay purposes). Receiving $3,831.30/month.

**Key Factors:** Identifying secondary conditions, medical nexus letters linking conditions to PTSD, comprehensive claim strategy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I get VA disability for PTSD if I never saw combat?
Yes. PTSD can result from any traumatic event during service, including MST, personal assault, accidents, training incidents, witnessing death/injury, or even fear of hostile military activity. The key is establishing that a traumatic stressor occurred during service.

### Do I need to have filed a report of MST or assault to get PTSD benefits?
No. The VA recognizes that many assaults go unreported. You can establish MST or assault through behavioral markers in your service records, lay statements, and your own credible testimony.

### How long does a PTSD claim take?
Average processing time is 130-180 days for initial claims, but complex cases can take longer. Appeals can take 1-3 years depending on the appeal path chosen.

### Can I work and still receive PTSD disability?
Yes. You can work and receive VA disability unless you're claiming unemployability (TDIU). However, your ability to work may affect your rating level, as severe ratings require significant occupational impairment.

### Will my PTSD rating be reduced?
Ratings can be reduced if VA determines your condition has improved. However, PTSD ratings are often protected after 20 years or if the veteran is over 55. Continue treatment and document ongoing symptoms.

### Can I get 100% for PTSD alone?
Yes. If your PTSD symptoms meet the 100% criteria (total occupational and social impairment), you can receive a 100% rating for PTSD alone.

### What if my PTSD has gotten worse?
File for an increase. Wait until you have at least 6 months of medical evidence showing worsening symptoms, then file VA Form 21-526EZ requesting an increase.

### Do I need a VA doctor or can I use private doctors?
Both are acceptable. Private medical evidence is given equal weight to VA medical evidence. Many veterans successfully claim using only private doctor records.

### What if my stressor is classified?
You can still file. Provide as much detail as possible without revealing classified information. The VA has access to classified records and can verify your stressor through other means.

### Can I get PTSD disability if I was discharged dishonorably?
Possibly. Dishonorable discharges generally bar VA benefits, but if you can prove your misconduct was caused by undiagnosed PTSD, you may qualify for a discharge upgrade or character of discharge determination allowing benefits.

### Should I hire a VA attorney or claims agent?
For initial claims, many veterans successfully file on their own or with help from VSOs (free). For appeals, especially complicated cases, an accredited attorney may be worthwhile. They work on contingency (typically 20-33% of back pay) and are only paid if you win.

### Will my PTSD affect my security clearance?
Getting treatment and filing for VA disability should not automatically affect a security clearance. Mental health treatment is increasingly viewed as responsible behavior. However, severe untreated symptoms could be a security concern.

### Can I get PTSD benefits if I'm Guard/Reserve?
Yes. If your PTSD resulted from active duty, active duty for training, or inactive duty training, you may be eligible. Deployment-related PTSD is common among Guard/Reserve members.

### What if VA says my stressor didn't happen?
Appeal with additional evidence. Get buddy statements, unit histories, morning reports, or other documentation. Consider hiring a private researcher who specializes in military records. For combat and MST claims, remember the burden of proof is lower.

### Does marijuana use affect my PTSD claim?
Be honest about marijuana use with your providers. Some veterans use it to manage PTSD symptoms. While the VA won't pay for medical marijuana, they won't deny claims solely based on use. However, substance abuse issues must be addressed.

## Final Thoughts

PTSD is one of the most common and significant service-connected disabilities. If you're suffering from PTSD related to your military service, you deserve compensation and treatment. The key to a successful claim is:

1. **Get treatment** - Both for your health and to document your condition
2. **Be thorough** - Gather all evidence before filing
3. **Be honest** - Don't minimize or exaggerate symptoms
4. **Be persistent** - Don't give up if initially denied
5. **Get help** - Use VSOs, attorneys, or accredited agents when needed

Remember: You earned these benefits through your service. There is no shame in claiming PTSD disability. Take care of yourself, document your condition, and file your claim.

**You are not alone. Help is available. You deserve support.**
