# Veteran-Owned Small Business (VOSB/SDVOSB) Certification: Complete Guide

## Bottom Line Up Front
- **What it is:** Official certification that your business is owned/controlled by a veteran or service-disabled veteran
- **Two types:** VOSB (Veteran-Owned) and SDVOSB (Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned)
- **Key benefit:** Access to federal set-aside contracts worth billions annually
- **Who certifies:** SBA (Small Business Administration) since January 2023
- **Insider tip:** The 3% government-wide SDVOSB contracting goal means billions in opportunities

## What Is VOSB/SDVOSB Certification?

### VOSB (Veteran-Owned Small Business)

**Definition:**
- Business owned 51%+ by one or more veterans
- Controlled in daily operations by veterans
- Management decisions made by veterans

### SDVOSB (Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business)

**Definition:**
- Business owned 51%+ by one or more service-disabled veterans
- Controlled in daily operations by service-disabled veterans
- Service-disabled veteran(s) hold highest officer position

**Service-disabled means:**
- VA-rated disability from military service (any percentage)
- Or presumed service-connected disability

## Benefits of Certification

### Federal Contracting Access

**SDVOSB Set-Aside Contracts:**
- 3% government-wide goal for SDVOSB
- Billions in contracts annually
- Less competition than open contracts

**Sole-Source Contracts:**
- SDVOSB can receive sole-source up to:
  - $4 million for services
  - $4 million for manufacturing

**VOSB Benefits:**
- Price evaluation preference
- Priority in some competitions
- Networking opportunities

### VA-Specific Contracting

**Veterans First Contracting Program:**
- VA prioritizes SDVOSB/VOSB for contracts
- Tiered approach: SDVOSBs first, then VOSBs
- Significant VA procurement opportunities

### State and Local Opportunities

**Many states offer:**
- State contract set-asides
- Preferential bidding
- Tax incentives
- Certification reciprocity

### Private Sector Benefits

**Large corporations often:**
- Have supplier diversity programs
- Seek certified VOSB/SDVOSBs
- Track veteran business spending
- Offer mentorship programs

## Eligibility Requirements

### VOSB Requirements

**Ownership:**
- 51%+ owned by U.S. citizen veterans
- Unconditional ownership (no restrictions)
- Direct ownership (not through another business entity generally)

**Control:**
- Veterans control management and daily operations
- Veterans make long-term decisions
- Highest officer is a veteran

**Business:**
- Small business (SBA size standards for your industry)
- Organized for profit
- Principal place of business in U.S.

### SDVOSB Additional Requirements

**All VOSB requirements PLUS:**
- Qualifying owner(s) are service-disabled
- Service-connected disability rated by VA (any %)
- Or permanent disability discharge

**Service-disabled veteran must:**
- Own at least 51% of business
- Control management and daily operations
- Hold highest officer position
- Make long-term strategic decisions

### Who Is a "Veteran"?

**For VOSB/SDVOSB purposes:**
- Served on active duty in U.S. Armed Forces
- Discharged other than dishonorably
- Includes Guard/Reserve if called to active duty

### Who Is "Service-Disabled"?

**VA-rated disability:**
- Any percentage (even 0%)
- From military service
- Verified by VA records

**Or:**
- Disability discharge from military
- Permanent disability retirement

## How to Get Certified

### Step 1: Prepare Your Business

**Before applying:**
- Ensure ownership meets requirements
- Update business documents
- Verify small business status
- Get veteran/disability documentation

### Step 2: Register on SAM.gov

**System for Award Management:**
- Required for federal contracting
- Free registration
- Renew annually
- Must complete before SBA certification

### Step 3: Apply Through SBA

**VetCert Application:**
- Go to sba.gov/vetcert
- Create account
- Complete application
- Upload documents

### Step 4: Submit Required Documents

**Ownership documents:**
- Articles of incorporation/organization
- Bylaws or operating agreement
- Stock certificates or membership interests
- Meeting minutes (if applicable)

**Control documents:**
- Resumes of owners/officers
- Organizational chart
- Employment agreements
- Salary information

**Veteran status:**
- DD-214 (Member 4 copy)
- VA disability letter (for SDVOSB)
- Service records

**Business documents:**
- Tax returns (3 years)
- Financial statements
- Licenses and certifications
- Contracts and agreements

### Step 5: SBA Review

**Review process:**
- SBA verifies all information
- May request additional documentation
- May conduct site visit
- Makes eligibility determination

### Step 6: Receive Determination

**If approved:**
- Receive certification letter
- Listed in SBA's database
- Valid for 3 years

**If denied:**
- Receive explanation
- Can appeal or reapply
- Address deficiencies

## Maintaining Certification

### Annual Review

**You must:**
- Update information in VetCert annually
- Report any ownership changes
- Report any control changes
- Maintain SAM.gov registration

### Recertification

**Every 3 years:**
- Full recertification required
- Submit updated documents
- SBA reviews again

### Changes to Report

**Must report within 30 days:**
- Change in ownership percentage
- New owners or investors
- Change in management/control
- Change in business structure
- Veteran status changes

### Maintaining Eligibility

**To keep certification:**
- Remain a small business
- Veterans maintain 51%+ ownership
- Veterans maintain control
- Keep all documents current

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Ownership Structure Problems

**Issues:**
- Veteran owns less than 51%
- Ownership through trust without proper structure
- Stock options count against ownership

**Fix:** Review with attorney before applying

### 2. Control Problems

**Issues:**
- Non-veteran in highest position
- Board controlled by non-veterans
- Day-to-day decisions by non-veterans

**Fix:** Ensure veteran has actual authority

### 3. Missing Documents

**Issues:**
- Missing formation documents
- Outdated agreements
- Missing signatures

**Fix:** Gather all documents before applying

### 4. Size Standard Issues

**Issues:**
- Business exceeds size standard
- Affiliates push over limit
- Wrong NAICS code used

**Fix:** Verify size before applying

### 5. Not Reporting Changes

**Issues:**
- Ownership changes not reported
- Management changes not disclosed
- Can lose certification

**Fix:** Report changes within 30 days

## If Application Is Denied

### Common Denial Reasons

1. **Ownership not 51%+** - Structure doesn't meet requirements
2. **Control issues** - Non-veteran actually running business
3. **Documentation gaps** - Missing or inconsistent documents
4. **Size standard** - Not a small business
5. **Veteran status** - Discharge or disability issues

### Appeal Process

**Request reconsideration:**
1. File within 60 days of denial
2. Provide additional evidence
3. Address specific denial reasons
4. SBA reviews appeal

### Reapplication

**After denial:**
- Address all issues identified
- Submit new application
- No waiting period

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How much does certification cost?**
A: Free through SBA. Beware of third parties charging fees.

**Q: Do I need certification to call myself veteran-owned?**
A: No, but you need certification for federal set-asides.

**Q: Can a franchise be certified?**
A: Possibly, but complex. SBA reviews franchise agreements.

**Q: What if I'm 50/50 with a non-veteran?**
A: Doesn't qualify. Must be 51%+ veteran ownership.

**Q: Can an LLC be certified?**
A: Yes. Any business structure can qualify if requirements met.

**Q: Do Reserve/Guard members qualify?**
A: Yes, if called to active duty. Generally yes for most Guard/Reserve.

**Q: What disability rating is needed for SDVOSB?**
A: Any rating—even 0% service-connected qualifies.

**Q: Can I be certified while on active duty?**
A: Generally no for veteran status. May qualify as military spouse business.

**Q: How long is certification valid?**
A: 3 years, with annual updates required.

**Q: Can multiple veteran-owned businesses joint venture?**
A: Yes, under specific SBA rules for joint ventures.
