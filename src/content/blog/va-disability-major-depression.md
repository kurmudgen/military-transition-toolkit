---# VA Disability Rating Guide: Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

## Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

**Possible Ratings:** 0%, 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, 100%

**Evidence Needed:**
- Current diagnosis of Major Depressive Disorder from qualified mental health professional
- Medical evidence showing onset during or aggravation by military service
- Nexus linking depression to service or service-connected condition
- Treatment records documenting symptoms, medications, and functional impact
- Personal and lay statements describing daily life impairment
- Mental Health DBQ completion

**Average Monthly Compensation (2025 rates, veteran without dependents):**
- 30%: $524.31
- 50%: $1,075.16
- 70%: $1,759.19
- 100%: $3,831.30

**Lifetime Value at 100%:** $1,839,024 (over 40 years)

## Understanding Major Depression VA Ratings

Major Depressive Disorder is rated under 38 CFR ยง 4.130, Diagnostic Code 9434 (Major Depressive Disorder) or can be rated under the general mental health rating formula. The VA evaluates depression based on symptom severity and functional impairment across occupational, social, and self-care domains.

### Rating Criteria Breakdown

#### 0% Rating
**Criteria:** A mental condition has been formally diagnosed, but symptoms are not severe enough to interfere with occupational and social functioning or to require continuous medication.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Diagnosed depression but symptoms are minimal or in remission
- No medication required or only as-needed use
- Full-time employment without issues
- Normal social functioning
- No impairment in daily activities
- Past history but currently asymptomatic

#### 10% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment due to mild or transient symptoms which decrease work efficiency and ability to perform occupational tasks only during periods of significant stress, or symptoms controlled by continuous medication.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Mild depressive symptoms managed with medication
- Occasional low mood or lack of motivation
- Minor concentration difficulties during stress
- Can maintain employment with minimal difficulty
- Social life generally intact
- Daily medication keeps symptoms controlled
- Minimal impact on daily functioning

#### 30% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment with occasional decrease in work efficiency and intermittent periods of inability to perform occupational tasks due to such symptoms as: depressed mood, anxiety, suspiciousness, panic attacks (weekly or less often), chronic sleep impairment, mild memory loss.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Regular depressive symptoms affecting work performance
- Persistent low mood and anhedonia (inability to feel pleasure)
- Chronic insomnia or hypersomnia
- Decreased motivation and energy
- Occasional inability to complete work tasks
- Some social withdrawal
- Mild memory and concentration problems
- Can work but with noticeable difficulty

#### 50% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment with reduced reliability and productivity due to such symptoms as: flattened affect; circumstantial, circumlocutory, or stereotyped speech; panic attacks more than once a week; difficulty in understanding complex commands; impairment of short- and long-term memory; impaired judgment; impaired abstract thinking; disturbances of motivation and mood; difficulty in establishing and maintaining effective work and social relationships.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Significant depressive symptoms impairing work
- Frequent absences from work or poor performance reviews
- Flat or blunted emotional expression
- Severe lack of motivation
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- Significant memory impairment
- Social isolation beginning
- Relationships suffering
- Reduced reliability and productivity
- May be on verge of job loss or unable to work consistently

#### 70% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment, with deficiencies in most areas, such as work, school, family relations, judgment, thinking, or mood, due to such symptoms as: suicidal ideation; obsessional rituals which interfere with routine activities; speech intermittently illogical, obscure, or irrelevant; near-continuous panic or depression affecting the ability to function independently, appropriately and effectively; impaired impulse control; spatial disorientation; neglect of personal appearance and hygiene; difficulty in adapting to stressful circumstances; inability to establish and maintain effective relationships.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Unable to maintain employment or only with extreme accommodation
- Near-constant depressed mood
- Suicidal thoughts with or without plan
- Severe anhedonia (no enjoyment in anything)
- Significant impairment in most life areas
- Neglecting personal hygiene and self-care
- Social isolation is severe
- Cannot handle any stress
- Relationship breakdowns (divorce, estrangement)
- May have suicide attempts in history
- Spending most of day in bed

#### 100% Rating
**Criteria:** Total occupational and social impairment, due to such symptoms as: gross impairment in thought processes or communication; persistent delusions or hallucinations; grossly inappropriate behavior; persistent danger of hurting self or others; intermittent inability to perform activities of daily living (including maintenance of minimal personal hygiene); disorientation to time or place; memory loss for names of close relatives, own occupation, or own name.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Cannot work at all - total disability
- Cannot consistently care for self
- Frequent psychiatric hospitalizations
- Active and persistent suicidal ideation
- May have psychotic features (delusions, hallucinations)
- Cannot perform basic activities of daily living
- Requires supervision or caregiver assistance
- Complete social isolation
- Unable to leave home regularly
- Extreme functional impairment in all domains

## Evidence Requirements

### 1. Current Diagnosis
You need a formal diagnosis of Major Depressive Disorder from a qualified mental health professional. The diagnosis must meet DSM-5 criteria and be documented in medical records.

**DSM-5 Criteria for Major Depression:**
Five or more of the following symptoms present during the same 2-week period (must include depressed mood or loss of interest):
- Depressed mood most of the day
- Markedly diminished interest or pleasure in activities
- Significant weight loss or gain
- Insomnia or hypersomnia
- Psychomotor agitation or retardation
- Fatigue or loss of energy
- Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
- Diminished ability to think or concentrate
- Recurrent thoughts of death or suicide

**Best Practices:**
- Seek treatment from psychiatrist or psychologist
- Document symptoms comprehensively with provider
- Get ongoing treatment to show symptom progression
- Request Mental Health DBQ completion

### 2. Service Connection Proof

**Direct Service Connection:**
- Depression began during military service
- Service medical records documenting depressive symptoms
- Stressful events during service (combat, MST, injuries, loss)
- Nexus letter from provider linking depression to service

**Secondary Service Connection:**
- Depression secondary to another service-connected condition (very common)
- Most common: secondary to PTSD, TBI, chronic pain, sleep disorders
- Medical opinion stating depression is caused or aggravated by primary condition

**Aggravation:**
- Pre-existing depression worsened by military service
- Evidence of mild depression before service, severe depression after
- Medical opinion on aggravation

### 3. Nexus (Medical Link)
A medical professional must provide an opinion that your depression is "at least as likely as not" (50% or greater probability) caused or aggravated by military service or a service-connected condition.

**Strong Nexus Letter Includes:**
- Provider's qualifications
- Review of service records and medical history
- Analysis of timeline (when depression began)
- Medical rationale connecting depression to service/condition
- Statement: "It is at least as likely as not that..."
- Supporting medical literature if applicable

### 4. Functional Impact Documentation

**Employment Evidence:**
- Performance reviews showing decline
- Disciplinary actions
- Attendance records (sick days, tardiness)
- Reduction in hours or job loss
- Inability to maintain employment
- Vocational rehabilitation attempts

**Medical Treatment Records:**
- Frequency and duration of treatment
- Medications prescribed (especially changes/increases)
- Hospitalizations (psychiatric)
- Emergency room visits for suicidal ideation
- Therapy notes documenting severity
- Mental status examinations

**Personal Statement:**
- Daily routine (or lack thereof)
- How depression affects work, relationships, self-care
- Specific symptoms and their frequency
- Suicidal thoughts history
- Activities you can no longer do

**Lay Statements:**
- Spouse, family, friends describing changes they've observed
- Before and after service comparisons
- Specific examples of functional limitations
- Impact on relationships

## Secondary Conditions Linked to Major Depression

### 1. **Anxiety Disorders**
- Depression and anxiety commonly co-occur
- Can be rated separately if symptoms are distinct
- Generalized Anxiety Disorder most common

### 2. **Sleep Disorders (Insomnia)**
- Nearly universal in depression
- Usually rated as part of depression
- Can be rated separately if particularly severe
- Document chronic sleep disturbance

### 3. **Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)**
- Gut-brain axis affected by depression
- Common secondary condition
- Rate up to 30%
- Document GI symptoms and frequency

### 4. **Fibromyalgia/Chronic Pain**
- Depression alters pain perception
- Central sensitization syndrome
- Widespread pain without clear cause
- Rate up to 40%

### 5. **Hypertension**
- Chronic stress and depression affect cardiovascular system
- Document blood pressure readings over time
- Rate up to 60%

### 6. **Weight Gain/Obesity**
- Medication side effects and decreased activity
- Can be secondary to depression and medications
- Document weight gain timeline
- May qualify for additional compensation if severe

### 7. **Erectile Dysfunction**
- Depression and antidepressants commonly cause ED
- Often underreported
- Rate up to 20%
- Urology evaluation helpful

### 8. **Migraines**
- Depression and migraines often co-occur
- Neurotransmitter dysfunction
- Rate up to 50%
- Requires frequency documentation

### 9. **Cognitive Impairment**
- "Brain fog" from depression
- Memory and concentration problems
- May improve with treatment or be permanent
- Document with neuropsychological testing

### 10. **Substance Abuse Disorders**
- Self-medication for depression
- Must be in recovery for separate rating
- Can complicate depression treatment
- Be honest with providers about use

## C&P Examination Preparation

### Before the Exam

**1. Create Comprehensive Symptom List:**
- Depressed mood (how often, how severe)
- Anhedonia (loss of pleasure/interest)
- Sleep changes (insomnia, hypersomnia, nightmares)
- Appetite/weight changes
- Fatigue and low energy
- Worthlessness or guilt feelings
- Concentration problems
- Memory issues
- Decision-making difficulties
- Suicidal thoughts (frequency, intensity, plans, attempts)
- Social withdrawal
- Crying spells
- Irritability
- Physical symptoms (aches, pains, GI issues)

**2. Document Functional Impairment:**
Prepare specific examples for:
- **Work:** Missed days, performance issues, conflicts, job loss
- **Relationships:** Divorce, family conflict, isolation from friends
- **Self-care:** Hygiene, grooming, eating, medical appointments
- **Social:** Inability to go out, lost hobbies, avoiding people
- **Daily tasks:** Difficulty with shopping, errands, household chores

**3. Gather Evidence:**
- Medication list with doses and frequency
- Recent treatment records
- Hospitalization records
- Suicide attempt records
- Lay statements from family
- Employment records

**4. Be Prepared to Discuss:**
- When depression started
- Connection to military service or service-connected condition
- Treatment history
- What you've tried (medications, therapy types)
- What works and what doesn't
- Worst depressive episodes
- Suicide attempts or hospitalizations

### During the Exam

**DO:**
- Arrive on time
- Describe symptoms accurately without minimizing
- Talk about your worst days, not best days
- Give specific examples of functional impairment
- Mention all symptoms, even if you think they're minor
- Discuss suicidal thoughts honestly (critical for accurate rating)
- Explain how medications help but don't fully resolve symptoms
- Describe impact on all life areas

**DON'T:**
- Minimize symptoms to appear strong
- Exaggerate (will damage credibility)
- Answer with just "yes" or "no" - explain fully
- Forget to mention important symptoms
- Hide suicide attempts or hospitalizations
- Say you're "fine" or "doing great"
- Forget about secondary conditions

### What the Examiner Will Assess

**Mental Status Examination:**
- Appearance and grooming
- Behavior and attitude
- Speech (rate, tone, volume)
- Mood and affect
- Thought process and content
- Cognition (memory, concentration, orientation)
- Insight and judgment
- Suicidal/homicidal ideation

**Depression-Specific Assessment:**
- Depressive symptom severity
- Frequency and duration of symptoms
- Impact on occupational functioning
- Impact on social functioning
- Impact on self-care
- Treatment compliance and response
- Suicide risk assessment

**Functional Capacity:**
- Ability to maintain employment
- Ability to maintain relationships
- Self-care capabilities
- Social functioning
- Adaptation to stressful circumstances

## Claim Strategy

### Initial Claim Strategy

**1. Establish Diagnosis First**
- Begin treatment if not already in care
- Get formal Major Depressive Disorder diagnosis
- Attend appointments regularly
- Be honest about symptom severity with providers

**2. Determine Connection to Service**
- **Direct:** Review service medical records for depression symptoms during service
- **Secondary:** Identify primary service-connected condition causing depression (PTSD, chronic pain, TBI, etc.)
- **Aggravation:** Show pre-service depression was worsened by service

**3. Obtain Nexus Opinion**
- Ask treating psychiatrist/psychologist for nexus letter
- Provide provider with service records and timeline
- If VA denies, consider Independent Medical Opinion (IMO)

**4. Document Functional Impact**
- Personal statement with specific examples
- Lay statements from family/friends
- Employment records
- Treatment records showing severity

**5. File Comprehensively**
Submit:
- VA Form 21-526EZ
- Medical records with MDD diagnosis
- Service records (if direct service connection)
- Nexus letter
- Personal statement
- Lay statements
- Employment records
- Mental Health DBQ (if available)

### Secondary Claim Strategy

Depression secondary to another condition is extremely common and often easier to prove than direct service connection.

**Common Primary Conditions Causing Secondary Depression:**
- PTSD (very common)
- Chronic pain conditions
- TBI
- Sleep apnea
- Tinnitus (with life impact)
- Paralysis or amputation
- Chronic illness (diabetes, heart disease)

**Secondary Claim Requirements:**
1. Primary condition must be service-connected
2. Current MDD diagnosis
3. Medical opinion that depression is caused or aggravated by primary condition
4. Temporal relationship (depression onset after primary condition)

**Example Nexus Statement:**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's Major Depressive Disorder is caused by their service-connected PTSD [or chronic pain, TBI, etc.]. The chronic stress, sleep disturbance, and functional limitations from [primary condition] are well-established risk factors for depression. The temporal relationship supports causation, as the veteran's depression began after the onset of [primary condition]."

### Increase Rating Strategy

**When to File for Increase:**
- Symptoms have worsened significantly
- Functional impairment has increased
- You have 6+ months of new evidence showing deterioration

**Evidence for Increase:**
- Updated treatment records
- Medication changes/increases
- New or increased hospitalizations
- Job loss or inability to work
- Relationship breakdowns
- Decline in self-care
- Updated personal and lay statements
- New Mental Health DBQ

### Total Disability Individual Unemployability (TDIU)

If depression prevents you from working but you're rated less than 100%, you may qualify for TDIU, which pays at 100% rate.

**TDIU Requirements:**
- One condition at 60%, OR
- Multiple conditions totaling 70% with one at 40%
- Unable to maintain substantially gainful employment due to service-connected disabilities

**TDIU Evidence:**
- Employment history showing inability to maintain work
- Termination records citing performance/attendance
- Medical opinions stating inability to work
- Vocational rehabilitation records showing failed attempts

**File:** VA Form 21-8940 (Application for TDIU)

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: Depression Secondary to PTSD - 70% Rating
**Background:** Army veteran with 70% PTSD rating, untreated depression for years.

**Strategy:**
- Finally sought treatment for depression
- Psychiatrist diagnosed Major Depressive Disorder
- Nexus letter: "Depression is clearly secondary to service-connected PTSD"
- Personal statement describing suicidal thoughts and inability to work
- Spouse statement about social isolation and hygiene decline
- Recent job termination due to absences

**Result:** Granted 70% for depression secondary to PTSD on initial claim. Combined rating increased to 91%, which rounds to 100% for pay purposes ($3,831.30/month).

**Key Factors:** Clear nexus to existing service-connected condition, documented severe functional impairment, evidence of suicidal ideation.

### Case Study 2: Direct Service Connection - 50% Rating
**Background:** Navy veteran, depression began during deployment following combat exposure.

**Strategy:**
- Service medical records showing treatment for depression during active duty
- Continued treatment post-service
- Treatment records documenting chronic symptoms
- Job performance reviews showing decline
- Filed for direct service connection

**Result:** Granted 50% for MDD with direct service connection. Receiving $1,075.16/month.

**Key Factors:** Service medical records documented onset during service, continuity of treatment, clear functional impairment at work.

### Case Study 3: Depression with TDIU - Paid at 100%
**Background:** Marine Corps veteran, 70% PTSD, 50% depression (combined 84%), unable to maintain employment.

**Strategy:**
- Multiple job terminations documented
- Medical opinion stating unable to work due to combined mental health conditions
- Detailed employment history showing pattern of failure
- Filed TDIU application with increase request

**Result:** Granted TDIU, paid at 100% rate despite combined rating of 84%. Now receiving $3,831.30/month plus healthcare benefits.

**Key Factors:** Clear employment history demonstrating inability to work, medical support for unemployability, thorough documentation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I get VA disability for depression if I already have PTSD?
Yes. Depression can be rated separately from PTSD if the symptoms are distinct. However, it's more commonly filed as a secondary condition to PTSD since they often occur together.

### How is depression rated differently from PTSD?
They use the same general rating criteria under the mental health rating schedule. The key is documenting which symptoms come from which condition. Often they overlap significantly.

### Can I get 100% for depression alone?
Yes, if your depression symptoms meet the 100% criteria for total occupational and social impairment. This typically requires severe symptoms like persistent suicidal ideation, inability to care for self, and possible psychiatric hospitalizations.

### Will VA reduce my depression rating if I improve?
Ratings can be reduced if your condition improves. However, continue treatment and document ongoing symptoms. Mental health ratings are often protected after 20 years or if you're over 55.

### How long does a depression claim take?
Initial claims average 130-180 days. Secondary claims may be faster. Complex cases or appeals can take 1-3 years.

### Do I need to be suicidal to get a high rating?
No, but suicidal ideation is mentioned specifically in the 70% rating criteria. Other severe symptoms can also warrant high ratings: inability to work, severe social impairment, neglect of hygiene, etc.

### Can I work and still get VA disability for depression?
Yes, unless you're claiming unemployability (TDIU). However, your ability to work affects your rating since severe ratings require significant occupational impairment.

### What if my depression is from medication side effects?
Depression caused by medication for a service-connected condition can be rated as secondary to that condition.

### Should I see a psychiatrist or psychologist?
Either can diagnose and treat depression. Psychiatrists can prescribe medication. Both can provide nexus opinions. Use whomever you're comfortable with and can access.

### What if VA says my depression is not service-connected?
Appeal with additional evidence. Get an Independent Medical Opinion (IMO) from a private expert. Consider secondary service connection to another rated condition. Use a VSO or attorney for help.

### Does depression count toward getting TDIU?
Yes. Mental health conditions are one of the most common reasons veterans qualify for TDIU. Depression preventing employment is strong evidence for TDIU.

### Will antidepressants help or hurt my claim?
Taking medications shows you're seeking treatment and your condition requires continuous medication (relevant to ratings). Never stop taking prescribed medications to try to worsen your rating - this is dangerous and unethical.

### Can I get VA disability for depression from military sexual trauma?
Yes. Depression is a common response to MST. File with personal statement, behavioral markers, and current treatment records. VA gives special consideration to MST-related claims.

### What if my private doctor says I can't work but VA says I can?
Private medical opinions are given equal weight to VA opinions. Submit your private doctor's opinion. If denied, appeal and consider getting an Independent Medical Opinion (IMO) from a specialist.

## Final Thoughts

Major Depressive Disorder is a serious, often debilitating condition that affects many veterans. If your depression is related to your military service or a service-connected condition, you deserve compensation and comprehensive treatment.

**Keys to a Successful Depression Claim:**

1. **Seek treatment immediately** - Both for your health and to document your condition
2. **Be honest about severity** - Don't minimize symptoms to appear strong
3. **Document functional impact** - Show specifically how depression affects work, relationships, and daily life
4. **Consider secondary connection** - Often easier to prove than direct service connection
5. **Don't give up** - Many successful claims are approved on appeal
6. **Get help** - Use VSOs, attorneys, or accredited agents when needed

**Remember:** Depression is not a weakness. It's a medical condition that can be treated. Seeking help and filing for benefits you earned is a sign of strength, not weakness.

**If you're having suicidal thoughts, please reach out:**
- Veterans Crisis Line: 988 then press 1
- Crisis Text Line: Text 838255
- Call 911 or go to nearest emergency room

You are not alone. Help is available. Recovery is possible.
