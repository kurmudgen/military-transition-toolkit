---# VA Disability Rating Guide: Sleep Apnea

## Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

**Possible Ratings:** 0%, 30%, 50%, 100%

**Evidence Needed:**
- Sleep study (polysomnography) showing Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI)
- Diagnosis of Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) or Central Sleep Apnea
- Service connection proof (direct or secondary)
- CPAP prescription and compliance records
- Nexus linking sleep apnea to service or service-connected condition
- Documentation of symptoms and functional impact

**Average Monthly Compensation (2025 rates, veteran without dependents):**
- 30%: $524.31
- 50%: $1,075.16
- 100%: $3,831.30

**Lifetime Value at 50%:** $516,077 (over 40 years)

**Key Fact:** Most veterans with diagnosed sleep apnea using CPAP receive 50% rating ($1,075.16/month).

## Understanding Sleep Apnea VA Ratings

Sleep apnea is rated under 38 CFR § 4.97, Diagnostic Code 6847. Ratings are based on the severity of respiratory dysfunction and whether treatment with breathing assistance device (CPAP, BiPAP) is required.

### Rating Criteria Breakdown

#### 0% Rating
**Criteria:** Asymptomatic sleep apnea with documented sleep disorder breathing, but not requiring treatment.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Sleep study confirms sleep apnea
- Mild apnea that doesn't require CPAP
- No significant symptoms
- No breathing assistance device prescribed
- Minimal functional impairment

**Rare Rating:** Most veterans with diagnosed sleep apnea requiring any treatment receive at least 30%.

#### 30% Rating
**Criteria:** Persistent day-time hypersomnolence (excessive sleepiness).

**What This Looks Like:**
- Diagnosed sleep apnea
- Excessive daytime sleepiness
- May or may not use CPAP
- Sleep apnea symptoms present but not using breathing device
- Or: using CPAP but it doesn't fully resolve symptoms

**Note:** This rating is less common than 50%. Most veterans using CPAP get 50%.

#### 50% Rating
**Criteria:** Requires use of breathing assistance device such as Continuous Airway Pressure (CPAP) machine.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Diagnosed sleep apnea
- CPAP or BiPAP prescribed
- Using breathing assistance device
- Medical records document CPAP prescription

**Most Common Rating:** If you have diagnosed sleep apnea and use CPAP, you typically receive 50%.

**Monthly Payment:** $1,075.16 (veteran without dependents)

#### 100% Rating
**Criteria:** Chronic respiratory failure with carbon dioxide retention or cor pulmonale (right-sided heart failure), or requires tracheostomy.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Severe, life-threatening sleep apnea
- Carbon dioxide retention in blood
- Heart failure from sleep apnea
- Tracheostomy performed
- Extremely rare for sleep apnea alone

**Very Rare:** Reserved for most severe cases.

## Evidence Requirements

### 1. Sleep Study (Polysomnography)

**Required for Diagnosis:**
- In-lab sleep study (gold standard) or home sleep study
- Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI) score
  - **Normal:** AHI < 5
  - **Mild:** AHI 5-14.9
  - **Moderate:** AHI 15-29.9
  - **Severe:** AHI ≥ 30

**What Sleep Study Measures:**
- Number of apneas (breathing stops)
- Number of hypopneas (shallow breathing)
- Oxygen desaturation events
- Sleep architecture disruption
- Respiratory event-related arousals

**Where to Get Sleep Study:**
- VA sleep clinic
- Private sleep center
- Home sleep study (if approved)

**Important:** You must have a sleep study showing sleep apnea to receive VA rating.

### 2. Medical Diagnosis

**Required Documentation:**
- Official diagnosis of Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) or Central Sleep Apnea
- Sleep medicine physician evaluation
- Sleep study results in medical records
- CPAP prescription (for 50% rating)

### 3. Service Connection Proof

Sleep apnea can be service-connected in three ways:

**A. Direct Service Connection (Rare):**
- Sleep apnea diagnosed during service
- Service medical records documenting sleep apnea or symptoms
- Continuity of treatment from service to present

**Why Rare:** Sleep apnea was rarely diagnosed during service; most veterans develop symptoms after service or diagnosis is delayed.

**B. Secondary Service Connection (Most Common):**
Sleep apnea secondary to another service-connected condition:

**Common Primary Conditions:**
- **PTSD:** Hyperarousal, nightmares, sleep disturbance → sleep apnea
- **Rhinitis/Sinusitis:** Nasal obstruction → sleep apnea
- **Asthma:** Respiratory condition → sleep apnea
- **GERD:** Acid reflux → airway inflammation → sleep apnea
- **Hypothyroidism:** Thyroid condition → weight gain/tissue changes → sleep apnea
- **TBI:** Neurological changes affecting breathing regulation
- **Obesity secondary to service-connected condition**

**C. Aggravation:**
- Pre-existing mild sleep apnea worsened by service
- Must show baseline severity and post-service worsening

### 4. Nexus Opinion

Medical opinion linking sleep apnea to service or service-connected condition.

**Strong Nexus Letter Includes:**
- Review of service records
- Review of sleep study results
- Medical explanation of how primary condition causes sleep apnea
- "At least as likely as not" statement
- Supporting medical literature

**Example Nexus (Secondary to PTSD):**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's obstructive sleep apnea is secondary to service-connected PTSD. Medical literature demonstrates that PTSD significantly increases sleep apnea risk through multiple mechanisms: chronic hyperarousal leading to sleep fragmentation, nightmares causing sleep disruption, and PTSD-related weight gain. The temporal relationship supports this connection, as the veteran's sleep apnea was diagnosed after PTSD onset."

### 5. CPAP Compliance Documentation

For 50% rating, you need:
- CPAP prescription in medical records
- Evidence that CPAP was prescribed for use
- **Note:** VA cannot check CPAP compliance data per 2016 changes, but having CPAP prescribed is required

**CPAP Documentation Sources:**
- Sleep medicine follow-up notes
- CPAP prescription
- DME (Durable Medical Equipment) records
- VA CPAP issuance records

## Secondary Service Connection Strategies

### Sleep Apnea Secondary to PTSD (Most Common)

**Medical Rationale:**
- PTSD causes chronic hyperarousal and sleep disruption
- Nightmares fragment sleep architecture
- PTSD medications (antidepressants) can worsen sleep apnea
- PTSD-related weight gain increases sleep apnea risk
- Studies show PTSD significantly increases sleep apnea prevalence

**Evidence Needed:**
- Service-connected PTSD rating
- Sleep study confirming sleep apnea
- Medical nexus linking sleep apnea to PTSD
- Timeline showing sleep apnea developed after PTSD
- Treatment records for both conditions

**Success Rate:** High success rate with proper nexus opinion.

### Sleep Apnea Secondary to Rhinitis/Sinusitis

**Medical Rationale:**
- Chronic nasal obstruction from rhinitis/sinusitis
- Forces mouth breathing during sleep
- Nasal inflammation affects airway
- Deviated septum (from service injury) causes obstruction

**Evidence Needed:**
- Service-connected rhinitis or sinusitis
- ENT records documenting nasal obstruction
- Sleep study confirming OSA
- Nexus linking nasal obstruction to sleep apnea

### Sleep Apnea Secondary to GERD

**Medical Rationale:**
- Acid reflux causes airway inflammation
- Microaspirations during sleep
- Throat tissue swelling
- Bidirectional relationship (GERD causes apnea, apnea worsens GERD)

**Evidence Needed:**
- Service-connected GERD
- Sleep study
- Nexus explaining GERD-sleep apnea connection

### Sleep Apnea Secondary to Obesity (Secondary to Service-Connected Condition)

**Medical Rationale:**
- Weight gain from service-connected condition
- Common: weight gain from PTSD medications, immobility from orthopedic injuries, metabolic changes from hypothyroidism

**Evidence Needed:**
- Service-connected condition causing weight gain
- Documentation of weight progression
- Sleep study
- Nexus linking weight gain (from primary condition) to sleep apnea

## C&P Examination Preparation

### Before the Exam

**1. Bring Sleep Study Results:**
- Official sleep study report
- AHI score clearly visible
- Date of study

**2. Bring CPAP Documentation:**
- CPAP prescription
- CPAP machine information
- Mask type
- Settings
- Follow-up appointment notes

**3. Document Symptoms:**
Prepare to discuss:
- Loud snoring
- Witnessed apneas (breathing stops)
- Gasping or choking during sleep
- Excessive daytime sleepiness
- Morning headaches
- Fatigue
- Difficulty concentrating
- Mood changes
- How symptoms affect daily life and work

**4. Timeline:**
- When symptoms started
- When diagnosed
- When CPAP prescribed
- Connection to service or service-connected condition

### During the Exam

**DO:**
- Bring all sleep study and CPAP documentation
- Clearly state you use CPAP (if you do)
- Describe symptoms before and after CPAP use
- Explain how sleep apnea affects your daily life
- Mention any difficulties with CPAP compliance
- Discuss connection to service or service-connected condition

**DON'T:**
- Say you don't use CPAP if it's prescribed (critical for 50% rating)
- Forget to bring sleep study report
- Minimize symptoms
- Forget to mention primary service-connected condition (if secondary claim)

**Critical Information:**
1. **Sleep study results:** AHI score, severity
2. **CPAP use:** Prescribed and using it (for 50%)
3. **Symptoms:** Daytime sleepiness, snoring, fatigue
4. **Functional impact:** How it affects work, driving, daily activities
5. **Connection to service:** Direct or secondary link

## Claim Strategy

### Initial Claim Strategy

**Step 1: Get Diagnosed**
- Schedule sleep study (VA or private)
- Get official sleep apnea diagnosis
- Get CPAP prescribed if indicated

**Step 2: Determine Service Connection Path**
- **Direct:** Sleep apnea during service (rare) or onset immediately after
- **Secondary:** Identify primary service-connected condition causing sleep apnea (PTSD, rhinitis, GERD, etc.)

**Step 3: Obtain Nexus Opinion**
- Ask sleep medicine doctor or primary care provider
- Provide them with information on primary service-connected condition
- Medical literature supporting connection helpful
- Or obtain Independent Medical Opinion (IMO)

**Step 4: File Comprehensively**
Submit:
- VA Form 21-526EZ
- Sleep study report
- Medical diagnosis of sleep apnea
- CPAP prescription
- Nexus letter linking to service/service-connected condition
- Treatment records
- Personal statement

### Secondary Claim Checklist

Most sleep apnea claims are secondary. Ensure you have:

1. **Primary service-connected condition established**
2. **Sleep study confirming sleep apnea diagnosis**
3. **CPAP prescription (for 50% rating)**
4. **Nexus letter** with:
   - Medical rationale for connection
   - Temporal relationship
   - "At least as likely as not" statement
5. **Timeline** showing sleep apnea developed after primary condition

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Filing without sleep study:**
- Must have polysomnography results
- Cannot be diagnosed with sleep apnea without sleep study
- File after you have study results

**2. Not getting CPAP prescribed:**
- 50% requires CPAP prescription
- If sleep study shows apnea, ask doctor for CPAP prescription
- Don't wait for VA to prescribe - get it from private doctor if needed

**3. Weak or missing nexus:**
- Need medical opinion linking sleep apnea to service/service-connected condition
- General statements not sufficient
- Must explain medical mechanism of connection

**4. Not identifying secondary connection:**
- Most sleep apnea claims should be secondary
- Identify which service-connected condition causes sleep apnea
- PTSD is most common, but consider all options

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: Sleep Apnea Secondary to PTSD - 50% Rating
**Background:** Army veteran, 70% PTSD rating, chronic sleep problems, excessive daytime sleepiness.

**Strategy:**
- Discussed sleep problems with VA primary care
- Referred to sleep clinic, completed sleep study
- AHI: 32 (severe sleep apnea)
- CPAP prescribed
- Psychiatrist provided nexus letter linking sleep apnea to PTSD
- Filed secondary claim

**Result:** Granted 50% for sleep apnea secondary to PTSD ($1,075.16/month). Combined rating increased from 70% to 80%.

**Key Factors:** Clear nexus from treating provider, sleep study results, CPAP prescription, documented PTSD-related sleep disruption.

### Case Study 2: Sleep Apnea Secondary to Rhinitis - 50% Rating
**Background:** Navy veteran, 10% service-connected chronic rhinitis, loud snoring, fatigue.

**Strategy:**
- ENT evaluation documented chronic nasal obstruction
- Sleep study ordered: AHI 22 (moderate sleep apnea)
- CPAP prescribed and used
- ENT provided nexus: "Chronic rhinitis causes nasal obstruction leading to mouth breathing and sleep apnea"
- Filed secondary claim

**Result:** Granted 50% for sleep apnea secondary to rhinitis. Combined rating increased to 50% total.

**Key Factors:** ENT expertise in nexus opinion, documented nasal obstruction, clear medical mechanism explained.

### Case Study 3: Initially Denied, Won on Supplemental Claim - 50%
**Background:** Marine Corps veteran, filed sleep apnea claim, initially denied for lack of nexus.

**Strategy:**
- Initial claim filed with sleep study but weak nexus
- Denied for insufficient evidence of service connection
- Filed Supplemental Claim with new evidence:
  - Identified service-connected GERD as primary condition
  - Obtained Independent Medical Opinion (IMO) from sleep specialist
  - IMO explained GERD-sleep apnea connection with medical literature
  - Documented weight gain timeline

**Result:** Granted 50% on supplemental claim.

**Key Factors:** Identified correct primary condition, obtained expert IMO, medical literature support, persistence.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What rating do I get if I use a CPAP?
50% in most cases. If you have diagnosed sleep apnea and a CPAP machine is prescribed for use, you typically receive 50% ($1,075.16/month).

### Do I have to actually use the CPAP to get 50%?
The requirement is that a CPAP machine is "required" (prescribed). After a 2016 change, VA generally cannot check CPAP compliance data. However, having it prescribed for use is required.

### Can I get sleep apnea disability if it wasn't diagnosed during service?
Yes. Most sleep apnea claims are for conditions diagnosed after service. You need to establish secondary service connection to another rated condition (like PTSD, rhinitis, GERD) or show direct service connection.

### What's the easiest way to get sleep apnea service-connected?
Secondary service connection to PTSD or another service-connected condition. This is much more common and easier to prove than direct service connection for sleep apnea.

### How long does a sleep apnea claim take?
Typical processing time is 130-180 days for initial claims. If you have a sleep study and nexus opinion ready, it may be faster.

### Will VA order a sleep study for me?
If you have symptoms, VA should order a sleep study. However, you can also get a private sleep study to speed up the process. Both are acceptable evidence.

### Can I file for sleep apnea if I don't have PTSD?
Yes. Sleep apnea can be secondary to many conditions: rhinitis, sinusitis, asthma, GERD, hypothyroidism, obesity (if secondary), TBI, etc. Identify which service-connected condition causes your sleep apnea.

### What if my sleep apnea is from weight gain?
If the weight gain is caused by a service-connected condition (e.g., PTSD medications, immobility from injuries, metabolic condition), you may file for sleep apnea secondary to that condition, with obesity as an intermediate condition.

### Do I need a certain AHI score to get a rating?
No specific AHI requirement for ratings. Ratings are based on symptoms and treatment required, not AHI score. However, you must have sleep apnea diagnosed via sleep study.

### Can I get more than 50% for sleep apnea?
100% is possible but extremely rare, requiring chronic respiratory failure, cor pulmonale, or tracheostomy. Most veterans receive 50%.

### What if I can't tolerate CPAP?
Document your attempts and difficulties. If CPAP was prescribed but you can't tolerate it despite trying different masks/settings, you may still receive 30% for persistent daytime hypersomnolence. Consider dental appliance or surgical options.

### How do I prove sleep apnea is connected to PTSD?
Nexus letter from a medical professional explaining the connection. Key points: PTSD causes sleep disruption, hyperarousal, nightmares, and sometimes weight gain - all of which contribute to sleep apnea. Temporal relationship (sleep apnea after PTSD onset) supports connection.

### Can sleep apnea cause other conditions I can claim?
Yes. Sleep apnea can cause or contribute to: hypertension, heart disease, depression, cognitive impairment, erectile dysfunction, and others. Consider filing these as secondary to sleep apnea.

### What if VA says my sleep apnea is just from obesity?
Obesity alone is not service-connected. However, if obesity is caused by a service-connected condition (medications, immobility, metabolic condition), then sleep apnea can be secondary to that condition through the intermediate step of obesity. Get a thorough nexus opinion explaining the chain of causation.

## Final Thoughts

Sleep apnea is one of the most commonly granted service-connected conditions, and the 50% rating provides significant compensation ($1,075.16/month, over $500,000 in 40 years).

**Keys to a Successful Sleep Apnea Claim:**

1. **Get a sleep study** - Cannot be diagnosed without it
2. **Get CPAP prescribed** - Required for 50% rating
3. **Identify secondary connection** - Most claims are secondary to PTSD, rhinitis, GERD, etc.
4. **Get strong nexus opinion** - Medical professional must explain the connection
5. **Document symptoms** - How sleep apnea affects your life
6. **File comprehensively** - Include all evidence with initial claim

**Most Common Path:**
1. Service-connected for PTSD (or other condition)
2. Develop sleep problems
3. Get sleep study → diagnosed with sleep apnea
4. CPAP prescribed
5. Get nexus letter linking sleep apnea to PTSD
6. File secondary claim
7. Granted 50% rating

Sleep apnea is a serious medical condition that significantly impacts health and quality of life. If you have sleep apnea and a connection to service, you deserve compensation.
