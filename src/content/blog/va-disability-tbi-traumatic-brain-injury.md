---# VA Disability Rating Guide: Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

## Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

**Possible Ratings:** 0%, 10%, 40%, 70%, 100% (for residuals)

**Evidence Needed:**
- Documentation of traumatic brain injury event during service
- Medical diagnosis of TBI (acute and residual effects)
- Neurological examination and cognitive testing
- Documentation of residual symptoms and their severity
- Nexus linking current symptoms to service-connected TBI
- Treatment records showing ongoing symptoms
- Personal and lay statements describing functional impact

**Average Monthly Compensation (2025 rates, veteran without dependents):**
- 10%: $171.23
- 40%: $755.28
- 70%: $1,759.19
- 100%: $3,831.30

**Important Note:** TBI itself is rated based on residual symptoms. Common residuals include cognitive impairment, memory problems, headaches, sleep disorders, mood disorders, and more. Each residual can be rated separately.

**Lifetime Value at 100%:** $1,839,024 (over 40 years)

## Understanding TBI VA Ratings

TBI is unique in VA rating. The initial TBI is rated temporarily after injury, but long-term ratings are based on residual conditions. Under 38 CFR ยง 4.124a, TBI residuals are evaluated across ten categories of impairment.

### How TBI Ratings Work

**Immediate Post-Injury Rating:**
- 100% for 3 months following hospital discharge
- Then re-evaluation based on ongoing symptoms

**Long-Term Rating - Based on Residual Symptoms:**
TBI residuals are rated from 0% to 100% based on severity across 10 categories:
1. Memory, attention, concentration, executive functions
2. Judgment
3. Social interaction
4. Orientation
5. Motor activity (control of movement)
6. Visual spatial orientation
7. Subjective symptoms (headaches, dizziness, fatigue, etc.)
8. Neurobehavioral effects
9. Communication
10. Consciousness

### Rating Criteria for TBI Residuals

#### 0% Rating
**Criteria:** Normal functioning in all 10 categories, or mild symptoms that do not interfere with work or daily activities.

**What This Looks Like:**
- TBI history but no current symptoms
- Fully recovered
- No functional impairment
- No ongoing treatment needed

#### 10% Rating
**Criteria:** One or two symptoms in one functional category rated as mild.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Mild memory problems
- Occasional headaches
- Minor concentration difficulties
- Can work full-time
- Minimal impact on daily life
- Symptoms noticeable but manageable

#### 40% Rating
**Criteria:**
- One or two symptoms in one category rated as severe, OR
- Three or more symptoms in one category rated as moderate, OR
- Three or more symptoms in two categories rated as mild

**What This Looks Like:**
- Moderate cognitive impairment affecting work
- Frequent headaches or migraines
- Significant memory/concentration problems
- Mood changes affecting relationships
- Vertigo or balance issues
- Reduced work productivity
- Need for work accommodations

#### 70% Rating
**Criteria:**
- One or two symptoms in one category rated as total, OR
- Three or more symptoms in two categories rated as severe, OR
- One symptom in three categories rated as severe

**What This Looks Like:**
- Severe cognitive impairment
- Unable to maintain employment
- Significant functional limitations in multiple areas
- Severe persistent headaches
- Major personality changes
- Significant social impairment
- Difficulty with daily activities
- May require assistance

#### 100% Rating
**Criteria:**
- Three or more symptoms in three or more categories rated as severe or total
- Total impairment in multiple functional areas

**What This Looks Like:**
- Cannot work at all
- Severe cognitive impairment in multiple domains
- Cannot live independently
- Requires caregiver or supervision
- Significant personality/behavioral changes
- Severe communication problems
- May have persistent vegetative state or near-vegetative state
- Total functional impairment

### Common TBI Residual Conditions (Rated Separately)

The following conditions can be rated as secondary to TBI:

**1. Cognitive Impairment (Diagnostic Code 8045):**
- Memory loss
- Attention deficit
- Executive function problems
- Rated 0-100% based on severity

**2. Headaches/Migraines (DC 8100):**
- Post-traumatic headaches very common
- Rated based on prostrating attacks frequency
- 0% to 50%

**3. Mental Health Conditions:**
- Depression (very common)
- Anxiety disorders
- Personality changes
- Emotional lability
- Rated 0-100% under mental health criteria

**4. Sleep Disorders:**
- Insomnia
- Hypersomnia
- Sleep apnea (TBI increases risk)
- Usually rated as part of TBI or mental health

**5. Vertigo/Dizziness (DC 6204):**
- Balance problems
- Vestibular dysfunction
- Rated 10-30%

**6. Tinnitus (DC 6260):**
- Ringing in ears post-TBI
- Rated 10% (flat rate)

**7. Vision Problems:**
- Blurred vision
- Double vision
- Visual field defects
- Rated separately by severity

**8. Olfactory/Taste Dysfunction:**
- Loss of smell
- Loss of taste
- Rated separately

## Evidence Requirements

### 1. Proof of TBI Event During Service

**In-Service Event Documentation:**
- Service medical records documenting head injury
- Combat records (IED blast, RPG, vehicle accident)
- Training accident reports
- Hospitalization records
- Loss of consciousness documentation
- Alteration of consciousness or mental state
- Memory loss around time of event (amnesia)

**Evidence Needed for TBI Diagnosis:**
- Documented head trauma
- Alteration of consciousness (confusion, disorientation)
- Loss of consciousness (any duration, even seconds)
- Post-traumatic amnesia
- Or: neurological symptoms immediately after event

**Note:** Even brief loss of consciousness or "getting your bell rung" can constitute TBI.

### 2. Medical Diagnosis of TBI

**Acute Phase Documentation:**
- Emergency room records
- Hospitalization records
- CT or MRI scans
- Neurological examination
- Glasgow Coma Scale score

**Chronic Phase Documentation:**
- Ongoing symptoms documented in medical records
- Neuropsychological testing showing deficits
- Neurological examination
- Brain imaging (may show abnormalities or rule out other causes)

**Neuropsychological Testing Important:**
- Tests cognitive domains affected by TBI
- Provides objective evidence of impairment
- Key for higher ratings
- Should test: memory, attention, executive function, processing speed

### 3. Current Residual Symptoms

Document ongoing symptoms across functional categories:

**Cognitive Symptoms:**
- Memory problems (short-term, long-term)
- Concentration difficulties
- Slow processing speed
- Executive function deficits (planning, organizing, problem-solving)

**Physical Symptoms:**
- Headaches/migraines
- Dizziness/vertigo
- Balance problems
- Fatigue
- Sleep disturbances
- Sensitivity to light/noise
- Vision problems

**Emotional/Behavioral Symptoms:**
- Mood swings
- Irritability/anger
- Depression
- Anxiety
- Personality changes
- Impulsivity
- Social inappropriateness

**Communication Symptoms:**
- Difficulty finding words
- Slurred speech
- Understanding problems
- Reading/writing difficulties

### 4. Functional Impact Documentation

**Employment Evidence:**
- Job loss due to TBI symptoms
- Performance issues
- Inability to return to pre-injury occupation
- Need for accommodations
- Vocational rehabilitation records

**Daily Life Impact:**
- Personal statement with specific examples
- Lay statements from family describing changes
- Examples of cognitive failures (getting lost, forgetting appointments, etc.)
- Social relationship problems
- Safety concerns (car accidents, getting lost, leaving stove on)

**Medical Treatment:**
- Ongoing treatment records
- Medications
- Therapy (cognitive rehabilitation, speech therapy, etc.)
- Hospitalizations
- Specialist evaluations (neurologist, neuropsychologist)

### 5. Nexus

Medical opinion linking current residual symptoms to service-connected TBI event.

**Strong Nexus Includes:**
- Review of service records documenting TBI
- Review of medical records showing symptom continuity
- Medical explanation of how TBI causes current symptoms
- "At least as likely as not" statement

## Secondary Conditions Linked to TBI

### 1. **Post-Traumatic Headaches/Migraines**
- Extremely common after TBI
- Can be rated up to 50%
- Requires frequency documentation
- File as secondary to TBI

### 2. **Major Depressive Disorder**
- Very common after TBI (neurological and psychological causes)
- Can be rated 0-100%
- Often severe in TBI patients
- File as secondary to TBI

### 3. **Anxiety Disorders**
- GAD, panic disorder common post-TBI
- Neurological changes affect anxiety regulation
- Rate 0-100%
- File as secondary to TBI

### 4. **Sleep Disorders**
- Insomnia extremely common
- Hypersomnia possible
- Sleep apnea risk increased
- Usually rated as part of TBI or mental health

### 5. **Tinnitus**
- Ringing in ears after head injury
- Flat 10% rating
- Very common secondary condition
- File separately

### 6. **Vertigo/Balance Disorders**
- Vestibular system damage from TBI
- Rate 10-30%
- Document frequency and severity of episodes

### 7. **Olfactory/Taste Dysfunction**
- Loss of smell common in TBI
- Loss of taste often accompanies
- Rate separately
- Impacts quality of life significantly

### 8. **Vision Problems**
- Blurred vision
- Double vision
- Visual field cuts
- Light sensitivity
- Rate separately by type and severity

### 9. **Cognitive Disorder**
- Memory, attention, executive function deficits
- Rate separately from TBI under DC 8045
- Can be rated 0-100%
- Neuropsychological testing critical

### 10. **Seizure Disorder**
- TBI increases seizure risk
- Post-traumatic epilepsy
- Rate based on frequency
- Can be rated up to 100%

## C&P Examination Preparation

### Before the Exam

**1. Gather Documentation:**
- Service records documenting TBI event
- All medical records from injury to present
- Neuropsychological testing results
- Medication list
- Symptom journal
- Lay statements

**2. Document Residual Symptoms:**
Create detailed list covering all 10 functional categories:
- **Memory:** What you forget, how often
- **Attention:** Difficulty concentrating, distractibility
- **Executive function:** Planning, organizing, problem-solving difficulties
- **Judgment:** Poor decisions, impulsivity
- **Social interaction:** Relationship problems, inappropriate behavior
- **Orientation:** Getting lost, confusion about time/place
- **Motor:** Coordination problems, tremors, weakness
- **Visual spatial:** Depth perception issues, clumsiness
- **Subjective symptoms:** Headaches, dizziness, fatigue (frequency and severity)
- **Communication:** Word-finding, understanding difficulties

**3. Prepare Functional Impact Examples:**
- Specific instances where TBI symptoms caused problems at work
- Examples of cognitive failures (missed appointments, got lost, forgot important information)
- Relationship conflicts due to personality changes
- Activities you can no longer do
- Safety incidents (car accidents, falls, kitchen accidents)

**4. Bring Supporting Materials:**
- Symptom diary
- Cognitive test results
- Examples of memory aids you use (lists, reminders, apps)
- Photos of injury site or scars
- Lay statements from people who knew you before and after injury

### During the Exam

**DO:**
- Describe all ongoing symptoms honestly
- Give specific examples of functional limitations
- Explain cognitive difficulties clearly
- Mention all secondary conditions (headaches, depression, sleep problems)
- Discuss how symptoms have impacted work and relationships
- Bring up safety concerns
- Mention compensatory strategies you use

**DON'T:**
- Try to "perform well" on cognitive tests to prove you're okay
- Minimize symptoms
- Forget to mention important residuals
- Rush through the exam
- Leave without ensuring all symptoms were discussed

**Critical Information for Examiner:**

1. **History of TBI event:** When, how it happened, loss of consciousness, immediate symptoms
2. **Acute treatment:** Hospitalization, scans, initial diagnosis
3. **Ongoing symptoms:** All residuals across 10 categories
4. **Treatment:** Current medications, therapies, what helps and what doesn't
5. **Functional impact:** Specific examples of work, social, daily life impairment
6. **Cognitive changes:** Memory, attention, processing speed problems
7. **Personality/mood changes:** How you're different from before injury
8. **Secondary conditions:** All related conditions

### Neuropsychological Testing

If your C&P exam includes neuropsych testing:
- Get adequate sleep the night before
- Take regular medications
- Do your best but don't hide difficulties
- Report if you don't understand instructions
- Mention if fatigue affects performance (TBI patients fatigue quickly during testing)
- Tests will assess: memory, attention, processing speed, executive functions, language, visual-spatial skills

## Claim Strategy

### Initial TBI Claim Strategy

**1. Document the TBI Event:**
- Gather service medical records showing head injury
- Combat action reports if applicable
- Buddy statements about incident
- Any contemporary documentation of event

**2. Establish TBI Diagnosis:**
- Ensure medical records state TBI diagnosis
- Get neurological evaluation
- Obtain neuropsychological testing if possible
- Document all acute symptoms

**3. Document Residual Symptoms:**
- Ongoing treatment records
- Personal statement describing all residuals
- Lay statements from family/friends
- Employment records showing impact

**4. File Comprehensively:**
Submit:
- VA Form 21-526EZ
- Service medical records documenting TBI event
- All medical treatment records
- Neuropsychological testing
- Nexus letter linking residuals to service-connected TBI
- Personal statement
- Lay statements
- Employment records

**5. Claim All Residuals:**
File for TBI AND all secondary conditions:
- Post-traumatic headaches
- Depression
- Anxiety
- Sleep disorder
- Tinnitus
- Vertigo
- Cognitive impairment
- Any other residuals

### Secondary Condition Strategy

Many conditions can be secondary to TBI:

**Requirements:**
1. Service-connected TBI diagnosis
2. Current diagnosis of secondary condition
3. Medical opinion linking secondary condition to TBI
4. Temporal relationship (condition developed after TBI)

**Common Secondary Claims:**
- Headaches secondary to TBI
- Depression secondary to TBI
- Anxiety secondary to TBI
- Sleep disorders secondary to TBI
- Cognitive impairment secondary to TBI

### Increase Rating Strategy

**When to File:**
- TBI residual symptoms have worsened
- New residual conditions have emerged
- Cognitive decline documented on testing
- Increased functional impairment
- At least 6 months of new evidence

**Evidence Needed:**
- Updated neuropsychological testing showing decline
- New or worsened symptoms documented
- Increased treatment (medications, therapy)
- Job loss or reduced work capacity
- Updated personal and lay statements
- Medical opinion on worsening

### TDIU Strategy for TBI

TBI often prevents employment even with ratings below 100%.

**TDIU Requirements:**
- One condition at 60%, OR
- Multiple conditions totaling 70% with one at 40%
- Unable to maintain substantially gainful employment

**TDIU Evidence for TBI:**
- Cognitive testing showing impairment
- Vocational expert opinion (helpful)
- Employment history showing inability to work
- Job loss due to TBI symptoms
- Medical opinion stating cannot work
- Specific examples of how TBI prevents employment

**File:** VA Form 21-8940

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: IED Blast TBI - 100% Rating
**Background:** Army veteran, IED blast in Iraq, brief loss of consciousness, ongoing cognitive and physical symptoms.

**Strategy:**
- Service records documented blast exposure and immediate medical treatment
- Neuropsychological testing showed significant cognitive impairment
- Filed for TBI with multiple residual conditions:
  - TBI residuals (cognitive, headaches, vertigo): 70%
  - Major depression secondary to TBI: 50%
  - Post-traumatic headaches: 30%
  - Tinnitus: 10%
- Personal statement detailed functional limitations
- Spouse statement described personality changes
- Unable to maintain employment post-service

**Result:** Combined rating 94%, rounded to 100% for pay ($3,831.30/month). Also granted TDIU.

**Key Factors:** Comprehensive claim for all residuals, neuropsych testing, clear functional impairment documentation, strong lay evidence.

### Case Study 2: Motor Vehicle Accident TBI - 70% Rating
**Background:** Navy veteran, training accident with head injury, ongoing cognitive and mood problems.

**Strategy:**
- Service medical records documented accident and hospitalization
- CT scan showed skull fracture
- Years of treatment records showing persistent symptoms
- Neuropsychological testing demonstrated memory and executive function deficits
- Job loss due to cognitive problems
- Filed for TBI residuals and secondary conditions

**Result:** TBI residuals rated 70% ($1,759.19/month), plus separate ratings for depression (50%) and headaches (30%). Combined rating 91%, rounds to 100% for pay.

**Key Factors:** Objective evidence (imaging, neuropsych testing), employment impact, comprehensive secondary claims.

### Case Study 3: Initially Denied, Won on Appeal - 40% Rating
**Background:** Marine Corps veteran, "got his bell rung" in blast, no documented loss of consciousness, ongoing symptoms denied initially.

**Strategy:**
- Initially denied due to lack of documented LOC
- Appealed with buddy statements confirming he was "out of it" after blast
- Private neuropsychologist evaluation showed TBI pattern of deficits
- Independent Medical Opinion (IMO) explaining TBI can occur without documented LOC
- Detailed personal statement about immediate and ongoing symptoms

**Result:** Granted 40% for TBI residuals on appeal ($755.28/month).

**Key Factors:** Persistence, private medical evidence, IMO, buddy statements, understanding that LOC documentation isn't always required.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I get VA disability for TBI if I don't remember the injury?
Yes. Memory loss around the time of injury (post-traumatic amnesia) is actually a symptom of TBI. Service medical records, buddy statements, and circumstantial evidence can establish the injury occurred.

### Do I need to have lost consciousness to have a TBI?
No. While loss of consciousness is common, TBI can be diagnosed with alteration of mental state (confusion, disorientation, "seeing stars") without full loss of consciousness.

### What if my TBI wasn't diagnosed until years after service?
You can still file. If you can establish that a head injury occurred during service (even if not diagnosed as TBI at the time) and a medical professional now diagnoses TBI with residuals, you may be service-connected.

### Can I get separate ratings for TBI and its residual symptoms?
Yes. TBI residuals are rated as one evaluation, but specific conditions (headaches, depression, tinnitus, etc.) can be rated separately as secondary to TBI.

### How important is neuropsychological testing?
Very important, especially for cognitive residuals. Neuropsych testing provides objective evidence of impairment that supports your subjective complaints.

### What if my TBI is from a blast but I wasn't diagnosed at the time?
Many blast TBI cases were not diagnosed during service, especially in earlier conflicts. Current diagnosis by a qualified professional, combined with evidence of blast exposure, can establish service connection.

### Will my TBI rating be reduced if I improve?
Ratings can be reduced if your condition improves. However, many TBI residuals are permanent. Continue treatment and document ongoing symptoms.

### Can I get 100% for TBI alone?
Yes, if residual symptoms are severe enough across multiple functional categories. However, most 100% TBI cases involve the TBI rating combined with secondary conditions.

### What if VA says my symptoms are from PTSD, not TBI?
This is common. PTSD and TBI have overlapping symptoms. You may have both. Get evaluations from both mental health and neurology specialists. Both conditions can be service-connected.

### How do I prove a TBI event if there's no documentation?
Buddy statements, circumstantial evidence (combat records showing you were in blast area), indirect evidence (service medical records showing headaches, dizziness after suspected injury), and your own statement. Lack of documentation doesn't mean it didn't happen.

### Can I work and still get TBI disability?
Yes, unless claiming TDIU. However, your work capacity affects your rating. Document any accommodations you need or work limitations.

### What if I have multiple head injuries from service?
All can contribute to your current TBI condition. Document all incidents. Cumulative effects may be considered.

### Should I claim TBI or post-concussion syndrome?
VA generally rates persistent symptoms from head injury as TBI residuals, regardless of whether it's called TBI, post-concussion syndrome, or another term. Focus on documenting symptoms and functional impact.

### Can TBI cause personality changes?
Yes, and this is ratable. Personality changes from TBI can be rated under neurobehavioral effects or as a separate mental health condition secondary to TBI.

## Final Thoughts

Traumatic Brain Injury can have profound, lifelong effects on veterans. If you experienced a head injury during service and have ongoing symptoms, you deserve evaluation and compensation.

**Keys to a Successful TBI Claim:**

1. **Document the injury event** - Even without perfect records
2. **Get comprehensive evaluation** - Neuropsychological testing is critical
3. **Document all residual symptoms** - Across all 10 functional categories
4. **Claim all secondary conditions** - Headaches, depression, anxiety, etc.
5. **Show functional impact** - Specific examples of work and life limitations
6. **Don't give up** - TBI claims are complex but winnable
7. **Get expert help** - VSO, attorney, private medical opinions

**Remember:** Many TBI cases were not properly diagnosed during service, especially blast TBI. Current symptoms and proper evaluation can establish service connection even without perfect service documentation.

TBI is not always visible, but its effects are real. You earned these benefits through your service and sacrifice.
