---# VA Disability Rating Guide: Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

## Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

**Possible Ratings:** 0%, 10%, 30%, 50%, 70%, 100%

**Evidence Needed:**
- Current diagnosis of Generalized Anxiety Disorder from qualified mental health professional
- Medical evidence linking anxiety to military service or service-connected condition
- Nexus opinion connecting anxiety to service
- Treatment records showing symptoms, medications, therapy
- Documentation of functional impairment in work, social, and daily life
- Personal and lay statements with specific examples
- Mental Health DBQ

**Average Monthly Compensation (2025 rates, veteran without dependents):**
- 30%: $524.31
- 50%: $1,075.16
- 70%: $1,759.19
- 100%: $3,831.30

**Lifetime Value at 100%:** $1,839,024 (over 40 years)

## Understanding Generalized Anxiety Disorder VA Ratings

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) is rated under 38 CFR ยง 4.130, Diagnostic Code 9400 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder) using the general formula for mental disorders. The VA evaluates GAD based on the frequency, severity, and duration of anxiety symptoms and their impact on occupational and social functioning.

### Rating Criteria Breakdown

#### 0% Rating
**Criteria:** A mental condition has been formally diagnosed, but symptoms are not severe enough to interfere with occupational and social functioning or to require continuous medication.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Diagnosed GAD but symptoms minimal or in remission
- No medication required or only as-needed use
- Can work full-time without issues
- Social functioning normal
- No significant impairment in daily activities
- May have had anxiety in past but currently well-controlled

#### 10% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment due to mild or transient symptoms which decrease work efficiency and ability to perform occupational tasks only during periods of significant stress, or symptoms controlled by continuous medication.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Mild anxiety symptoms managed with daily medication
- Occasional worry or nervousness
- Concentration difficulties during stressful periods
- Can maintain employment but with some difficulty during high-stress times
- Social life generally intact
- Symptoms controlled by medication
- Minimal functional impairment overall

#### 30% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment with occasional decrease in work efficiency and intermittent periods of inability to perform occupational tasks due to such symptoms as: depressed mood, anxiety, suspiciousness, panic attacks (weekly or less often), chronic sleep impairment, mild memory loss.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Regular anxiety symptoms affecting work
- Excessive worry most days
- Chronic tension and restlessness
- Panic attacks weekly or less
- Sleep problems from worry
- Occasional inability to complete work tasks
- Some social avoidance
- Mild concentration and memory problems
- Can work but with noticeable difficulty

#### 50% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment with reduced reliability and productivity due to such symptoms as: flattened affect; circumstantial, circumlocutory, or stereotyped speech; panic attacks more than once a week; difficulty in understanding complex commands; impairment of short- and long-term memory; impaired judgment; impaired abstract thinking; disturbances of motivation and mood; difficulty in establishing and maintaining effective work and social relationships.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Significant anxiety interfering with work performance
- Panic attacks more than once per week
- Constant worry affecting concentration and decision-making
- Frequent work absences
- Significant memory problems
- Difficulty maintaining relationships
- Social isolation beginning
- Reduced reliability at work
- May be on verge of job loss
- Physical symptoms: muscle tension, headaches, GI issues

#### 70% Rating
**Criteria:** Occupational and social impairment, with deficiencies in most areas, such as work, school, family relations, judgment, thinking, or mood, due to such symptoms as: suicidal ideation; obsessional rituals which interfere with routine activities; speech intermittently illogical, obscure, or irrelevant; near-continuous panic or depression affecting the ability to function independently, appropriately and effectively; impaired impulse control; spatial disorientation; neglect of personal appearance and hygiene; difficulty in adapting to stressful circumstances; inability to establish and maintain effective relationships.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Unable to maintain employment without significant accommodation
- Near-constant anxiety and worry
- Frequent panic attacks severely limiting activities
- Avoidance behaviors significantly restricting life
- Cannot handle stress at all
- Deficiencies in most life areas
- Relationship breakdowns
- May have suicidal thoughts from overwhelming anxiety
- Personal hygiene declining
- Severe social isolation
- Cannot leave home for many activities

#### 100% Rating
**Criteria:** Total occupational and social impairment, due to such symptoms as: gross impairment in thought processes or communication; persistent delusions or hallucinations; grossly inappropriate behavior; persistent danger of hurting self or others; intermittent inability to perform activities of daily living (including maintenance of minimal personal hygiene); disorientation to time or place; memory loss for names of close relatives, own occupation, or own name.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Cannot work at all
- Cannot consistently care for self
- Extreme anxiety preventing most activities
- May be housebound
- Possible panic disorder with agoraphobia
- Cannot perform basic daily activities reliably
- May have psychotic features from severe anxiety
- Requires supervision or caregiver assistance
- Complete social isolation
- Total functional impairment

## Evidence Requirements

### 1. Current Diagnosis

You need a formal diagnosis of Generalized Anxiety Disorder from a qualified mental health professional (psychiatrist, psychologist, or LCSW).

**DSM-5 Criteria for GAD:**
- Excessive anxiety and worry about various events or activities
- Occurs more days than not for at least 6 months
- Difficult to control the worry
- Associated with three or more of:
  - Restlessness or feeling keyed up/on edge
  - Being easily fatigued
  - Difficulty concentrating or mind going blank
  - Irritability
  - Muscle tension
  - Sleep disturbance
- Causes significant distress or impairment
- Not due to substances or medical condition

**Best Practices:**
- Seek treatment from mental health specialist
- Document all anxiety symptoms with provider
- Get ongoing treatment showing symptom course
- Request Mental Health DBQ completion
- Track panic attacks, physical symptoms, avoidance behaviors

### 2. Service Connection Proof

**Direct Service Connection:**
- Anxiety began during military service
- Service medical records showing anxiety symptoms
- Stressful service events (combat, deployment, training accidents, MST)
- Nexus linking current GAD to service experiences

**Secondary Service Connection (Very Common):**
- GAD secondary to another service-connected condition
- Most common primaries: PTSD, TBI, chronic pain, tinnitus, sleep apnea
- Medical opinion stating GAD caused or aggravated by primary condition
- Timeline showing anxiety developed after primary condition

**Aggravation:**
- Pre-existing mild anxiety significantly worsened by military service
- Evidence of anxiety before service vs. severe anxiety after
- Medical opinion on aggravation

### 3. Nexus Opinion

A medical professional must opine that your GAD is "at least as likely as not" (50% or greater probability) related to military service or a service-connected condition.

**Strong Nexus Letter Includes:**
- Provider credentials and expertise
- Review of service and medical records
- Timeline analysis (when anxiety began/worsened)
- Medical rationale for connection
- Clear "at least as likely as not" statement
- References to medical literature if applicable

**Example for Secondary GAD:**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's Generalized Anxiety Disorder is secondary to their service-connected PTSD. Chronic hyperarousal, hypervigilance, and stress from PTSD commonly lead to generalized anxiety. The temporal relationship supports this, as GAD symptoms emerged after PTSD diagnosis."

### 4. Functional Impact Documentation

**Employment Evidence:**
- Performance reviews showing anxiety-related issues
- Attendance problems (sick days, early departures)
- Disciplinary actions for concentration problems
- Job loss or inability to maintain employment
- Reduction in work hours
- Accommodations needed

**Medical Records:**
- Treatment frequency and type
- Medications (especially increases/changes)
- Emergency room visits for panic attacks
- Hospitalizations
- Therapy notes documenting severity
- Failed medication trials

**Personal Statement:**
- Daily life description with anxiety impact
- Specific symptoms and frequency
- How anxiety limits work, relationships, activities
- Panic attack descriptions
- Avoidance behaviors
- Physical symptoms

**Lay Statements:**
- Family/friends describing observable changes
- Before and after service comparisons
- Specific examples of anxiety limiting veteran
- Impact on relationships and family life

## Secondary Conditions Linked to GAD

### 1. **Panic Disorder**
- Often co-occurs with GAD
- Recurrent unexpected panic attacks
- Can be rated separately if distinct
- Rate up to 100% if severe

### 2. **Depression (Major Depressive Disorder)**
- Extremely common with anxiety
- Can be rated separately
- Look for: persistent sadness, anhedonia, hopelessness
- Rate 0-100%

### 3. **Insomnia/Sleep Disorders**
- Worry prevents sleep
- Usually rated as part of GAD symptoms
- Can be separate if particularly severe
- Document chronic sleep problems

### 4. **Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)**
- Gut-brain axis heavily affected by anxiety
- Very common in GAD sufferers
- Rate up to 30%
- Document frequency and severity of GI symptoms

### 5. **Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)**
- Stress/anxiety worsens acid reflux
- Common secondary condition
- Rate up to 30%
- Document chronic heartburn, medication use

### 6. **Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)**
- Chronic anxiety affects cardiovascular system
- Document elevated blood pressure readings
- Rate up to 60%
- Requires consistent BP monitoring

### 7. **Migraine Headaches**
- Tension and anxiety trigger migraines
- Common secondary condition
- Rate up to 50% (30% most common)
- Requires prostrating headache frequency documentation

### 8. **Temporomandibular Joint Disorder (TMJ)**
- Jaw clenching from anxiety
- Teeth grinding (bruxism)
- Rate 10-40%
- Dental records and nightguard use helpful

### 9. **Chronic Pain Conditions**
- Anxiety increases pain perception
- Muscle tension causes widespread pain
- Fibromyalgia possible
- Document pain patterns

### 10. **Erectile Dysfunction**
- Anxiety and anti-anxiety medications cause ED
- Often underreported
- Rate up to 20%
- Urology evaluation helpful

## C&P Examination Preparation

### Before the Exam

**1. Comprehensive Symptom Documentation:**
Create a detailed list of your anxiety symptoms:
- **Psychological:** Excessive worry, fear, dread, sense of impending doom
- **Cognitive:** Concentration problems, memory issues, racing thoughts, mind going blank
- **Physical:** Heart palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath, chest pain, nausea, dizziness
- **Behavioral:** Avoidance, restlessness, irritability, difficulty sleeping
- **Panic attacks:** Frequency, triggers, duration, symptoms
- **Functional impact:** What you can't do because of anxiety

**2. Track Anxiety Patterns:**
Keep a log for 2-4 weeks before exam:
- Daily anxiety levels (1-10 scale)
- Panic attack frequency
- What triggers anxiety
- How anxiety affects daily activities
- Medications taken and effectiveness
- Sleep quality

**3. Prepare Functional Impact Examples:**
- **Work:** Specific instances where anxiety affected performance, absences, conflicts
- **Social:** Events/activities avoided, relationships affected
- **Self-care:** Medical appointments missed, hygiene affected
- **Daily life:** Errands avoided, difficulty with phone calls, avoiding stores/crowds

**4. Gather Documentation:**
- Current medication list with doses
- Recent treatment records
- Panic attack/ER visit records
- Lay statements from family
- Employment records
- Anxiety symptom journal

### During the Exam

**DO:**
- Describe your worst days, not your best
- Be specific about physical anxiety symptoms (often overlooked)
- Mention panic attack frequency accurately
- Explain avoidance behaviors in detail
- Discuss how anxiety affects all life areas
- Mention secondary symptoms (depression, GI issues, headaches)
- Be honest about severity
- Explain medication side effects and efficacy

**DON'T:**
- Minimize symptoms to avoid appearing weak
- Exaggerate (hurts credibility)
- Give one-word answers - explain fully
- Forget physical symptoms (heart racing, sweating, GI issues)
- Downplay avoidance behaviors
- Say medication "fixes everything"
- Rush through the exam

**Critical Points to Make:**
- Anxiety is constant, not just occasional
- Worry is excessive and hard to control
- Physical symptoms are frequent and distressing
- Panic attacks (if present) - frequency and severity
- Significant functional limitations in multiple areas
- Treatment helps but doesn't eliminate symptoms

### What the Examiner Assesses

**Mental Status Examination:**
- Appearance (grooming, signs of anxiety)
- Behavior (restlessness, fidgeting)
- Speech (rate, pressure)
- Mood and affect (anxious, worried, flat)
- Thought process (racing thoughts)
- Thought content (worry themes)
- Cognition (concentration, memory)
- Insight and judgment

**GAD-Specific Assessment:**
- Nature and extent of worry
- Physical symptoms of anxiety
- Frequency of panic attacks
- Avoidance behaviors
- Impact on functioning
- Sleep disturbance
- Treatment history and response

**Functional Assessment:**
- Occupational functioning
- Social relationships
- Self-care abilities
- Stress tolerance
- Decision-making capacity

## Claim Strategy

### Initial Claim Strategy

**1. Get Diagnosed and Treated:**
- Seek mental health care immediately
- Get formal GAD diagnosis
- Attend appointments regularly
- Try prescribed treatments
- Document symptom severity honestly

**2. Determine Service Connection Path:**
- **Direct:** Anxiety began during or shortly after service
- **Secondary:** Anxiety developed after another service-connected condition (PTSD, chronic pain, TBI, etc.)
- **Aggravation:** Pre-existing anxiety worsened by service

**3. Obtain Nexus Opinion:**
- Request nexus letter from treating provider
- Provide service records and timeline
- If VA denies, consider Independent Medical Opinion (IMO)

**4. Document Thoroughly:**
- Personal statement with specific examples
- Lay statements from family/friends
- Employment records showing impact
- Daily symptom journal

**5. File Complete Claim:**
Submit:
- VA Form 21-526EZ
- Medical records with GAD diagnosis
- Service records (if direct connection)
- Nexus letter
- Personal statement with functional impact examples
- Lay statements
- Employment/school records
- Mental Health DBQ (if available)

### Secondary Claim Strategy

GAD as a secondary condition is very common and often easier to prove than direct service connection.

**Common Primary Conditions Causing Secondary GAD:**
- PTSD (hypervigilance leads to generalized anxiety)
- Chronic pain (worry about pain, disability)
- TBI (neurological changes)
- Tinnitus (constant noise causes anxiety)
- Sleep apnea (poor sleep, health worries)
- Any chronic service-connected condition

**Requirements for Secondary Claim:**
1. Primary condition must be service-connected
2. Current GAD diagnosis
3. Medical opinion linking GAD to primary condition
4. Temporal relationship (GAD developed after primary condition)

### Increase Rating Strategy

**When to File:**
- Anxiety has significantly worsened
- Panic attacks more frequent
- Increased functional impairment
- Job loss or reduced work capacity
- Increased avoidance behaviors
- At least 6 months of new evidence

**Evidence Needed:**
- Updated treatment records
- Increased medications or new medications
- ER visits for panic attacks
- Job loss/performance documentation
- Updated personal and lay statements
- New Mental Health DBQ showing worsening

### TDIU (Total Disability Individual Unemployability)

If anxiety prevents you from working but you're rated less than 100%, you may qualify for TDIU (pays at 100% rate).

**TDIU Requirements:**
- One condition at 60%, OR
- Multiple conditions totaling 70% with one at 40%
- Unable to maintain substantially gainful employment

**TDIU Evidence:**
- Employment history showing pattern of job loss
- Termination notices citing anxiety-related issues
- Medical opinions stating inability to work
- Vocational rehabilitation failure
- Detailed explanation of how anxiety prevents employment

**File:** VA Form 21-8940

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: GAD Secondary to Chronic Pain - 50% Rating
**Background:** Army veteran with 40% service-connected back pain, developed severe anxiety worrying about pain and disability.

**Strategy:**
- Sought mental health treatment, diagnosed with GAD
- Psychiatrist provided nexus letter linking anxiety to chronic pain
- Personal statement describing constant worry about pain, ability to work, finances
- Documented panic attacks when pain flares
- Spouse statement about personality change and social withdrawal

**Result:** Granted 50% for GAD secondary to back pain. Combined rating increased to 70% ($1,759.19/month).

**Key Factors:** Clear nexus to existing condition, documented panic attacks, significant functional impairment, strong lay evidence.

### Case Study 2: GAD Secondary to PTSD - 70% Rating
**Background:** Marine Corps veteran, 70% PTSD rating, untreated generalized anxiety.

**Strategy:**
- Finally sought treatment for overwhelming worry beyond PTSD symptoms
- Psychologist diagnosed GAD as separate condition
- Documented excessive worry about health, family, finances (not just trauma-related)
- Unable to work due to combined mental health issues
- Multiple ER visits for panic attacks
- Filed as secondary to PTSD

**Result:** Granted 70% for GAD secondary to PTSD. Combined rating increased to 94%, rounded to 100% for pay ($3,831.30/month).

**Key Factors:** Differentiated GAD symptoms from PTSD, severe functional impairment, documented ER visits, clear inability to work.

### Case Study 3: Direct Service Connection - 30% to 70% Increase
**Background:** Air Force veteran, initially rated 30% for GAD related to deployment stress.

**Strategy:**
- Anxiety significantly worsened over 3 years
- Documented increased panic attacks (now 3-4 per week)
- Lost job due to anxiety-related absences
- Increased medications (added benzodiazepine for panic)
- Two psychiatric hospitalizations for suicidal thoughts from overwhelming anxiety
- Updated personal statement and spouse statement
- Filed for increase with 3 years of new records

**Result:** Increased from 30% to 70% ($524.31/month to $1,759.19/month).

**Key Factors:** Clear worsening documented, frequent panic attacks, job loss, hospitalizations, comprehensive new evidence.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I get separate ratings for both GAD and PTSD?
Possibly, but only if symptoms are clearly distinct. Often they overlap significantly and are rated under one diagnosis. Work with your provider to differentiate symptoms if they truly are separate conditions.

### How is GAD different from PTSD for VA rating purposes?
Both use the same rating criteria (38 CFR 4.130). The difference is in the diagnosis and symptom pattern. PTSD is related to specific traumatic stressors; GAD involves excessive worry across many areas of life.

### Will panic attacks automatically get me a higher rating?
Panic attacks are specifically mentioned in the rating criteria. Weekly panic attacks are consistent with 30%, more than weekly with 50%. However, overall functional impairment is the key factor.

### Can I work and still get VA disability for anxiety?
Yes, unless claiming TDIU. However, your ability to work affects your rating level. Higher ratings require significant occupational impairment.

### What if my anxiety is from medication side effects?
Anxiety caused by medication for a service-connected condition can be rated as secondary to that condition. Document the timeline and get medical opinion linking the two.

### Should I see a psychiatrist or psychologist for GAD?
Either can diagnose and treat GAD. Psychiatrists prescribe medication. Psychologists typically don't prescribe but provide therapy. Both can provide nexus opinions. See whomever you're comfortable with and can access.

### How do I prove GAD is secondary to my physical condition?
Get a medical opinion from your mental health provider stating that your anxiety is caused by your physical condition. Document how worry about the physical condition dominates your thoughts. Show temporal relationship.

### Will my anxiety rating be reduced if I get better?
Ratings can be reduced if your condition improves. However, continue appropriate treatment and document ongoing symptoms. Mental health conditions often wax and wane.

### What if I have anxiety but no panic attacks?
You can still receive a rating. Panic attacks are just one symptom. GAD is diagnosed based on excessive worry, physical symptoms, and functional impairment - panic attacks aren't required.

### Can I get 100% for anxiety alone?
Yes, if your anxiety symptoms cause total occupational and social impairment. This is rare but possible with severe, treatment-resistant GAD, especially with agoraphobia.

### How long does an anxiety claim take?
Initial claims average 130-180 days. Secondary claims may be faster if the primary condition is already established. Appeals can take 1-3 years.

### Should I stop my anxiety medication before the C&P exam?
Absolutely not. Never stop prescribed medication. The rating considers your condition with treatment. Stopping medication is dangerous and won't help your claim.

### What if VA says my anxiety isn't service-connected?
Appeal with additional evidence. Get an Independent Medical Opinion (IMO). Consider secondary service connection to another condition. Use a VSO or attorney for guidance.

### Does GAD count toward TDIU eligibility?
Yes. Mental health conditions, including GAD, are one of the most common reasons veterans qualify for unemployability benefits.

## Final Thoughts

Generalized Anxiety Disorder can be just as debilitating as PTSD or depression. If your anxiety is related to your military service or a service-connected condition, you deserve compensation and treatment.

**Keys to Success:**

1. **Get treatment** - For your health and to document your condition
2. **Be thorough** - Document all symptoms, especially physical ones
3. **Track panic attacks** - Frequency matters for ratings
4. **Document avoidance** - What you can't do because of anxiety
5. **Consider secondary connection** - Often easier to prove
6. **Don't give up** - Many claims succeed on appeal
7. **Seek help** - VSOs and attorneys can guide you

**Remember:** Anxiety is a real medical condition. Seeking treatment and filing for benefits you earned is appropriate and necessary. You are not weak for having anxiety - you're strong for seeking help.

**If you're in crisis:**
- Veterans Crisis Line: 988 then press 1
- Text: 838255
- Chat: www.veteranscrisisline.net

You earned these benefits. Don't leave them on the table.
