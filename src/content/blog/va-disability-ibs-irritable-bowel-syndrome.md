---# VA Disability Rating Guide: Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

## Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)

**Possible Ratings:** 0%, 10%, 30%

**Evidence Needed:**
- Medical diagnosis of Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
- Documentation of symptoms (diarrhea, constipation, pain, distress)
- Treatment records showing medications and interventions
- Symptom diary tracking frequency and severity
- Nexus linking IBS to service or service-connected condition
- Personal statement describing functional impact

**Average Monthly Compensation (2025 rates, veteran without dependents):**
- 10%: $171.23
- 30%: $524.31

**Lifetime Value at 10%:** $82,190 (over 40 years)
**Lifetime Value at 30%:** $251,669 (over 40 years)

**Key Fact:** IBS ratings are based on frequency and severity of bowel disturbance, distress, and functional impact.

---

## Understanding IBS VA Ratings

Irritable Bowel Syndrome is rated under 38 CFR § 4.114, Diagnostic Code 7319. Ratings are based on symptom severity and frequency, particularly disturbances of bowel function and level of distress.

### Rating Criteria Breakdown

#### 0% Rating
**Criteria:** Mild IBS symptoms with occasional episodes of bowel disturbance with abdominal distress.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Diagnosed IBS
- Occasional symptoms (less frequent than moderate)
- Minimal functional impairment
- Can control with diet/over-the-counter medications
- Doesn't significantly impact work or daily life

**Monthly:** $0

**Note:** Many veterans with IBS receive 0% initially, especially if symptoms are mild or well-controlled.

#### 10% Rating
**Criteria:** Moderate IBS with frequent episodes of bowel disturbance with abdominal distress.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Regular IBS symptoms (multiple times per month)
- Frequent diarrhea, constipation, or alternating
- Abdominal pain and cramping
- Bloating, urgency
- Some functional impact on work and activities
- Requires medications
- Some dietary restrictions
- Occasional interference with social/work activities

**Monthly:** $171.23

**Most Common Rating** for veterans with regularly symptomatic IBS.

**"Frequent" generally means:**
- Symptoms occurring regularly throughout the month
- Multiple episodes per week
- Consistent impact on daily life

#### 30% Rating
**Criteria:** Severe IBS with diarrhea, or alternating diarrhea and constipation, with more or less constant abdominal distress.

**What This Looks Like:**
- Daily or near-daily symptoms
- Severe, persistent abdominal pain/distress
- Frequent diarrhea affecting quality of life
- Significant urgency and fear of accidents
- Major dietary restrictions
- Significant impact on work (absences, need for bathroom access)
- Social isolation or limitation due to symptoms
- Multiple medications with limited relief
- Functional impairment in multiple areas of life

**Monthly:** $524.31

**Key Differences from 10%:**
- "More or less constant" abdominal distress (not just frequent episodes)
- "Severe" level
- Diarrhea specifically mentioned (or alternating diarrhea/constipation)
- Greater functional impairment

**Important:** 30% is maximum rating for IBS alone.

## What Is IBS?

**Irritable Bowel Syndrome** is a chronic functional GI disorder characterized by:

**Primary Symptoms:**
- Abdominal pain or cramping
- Diarrhea (IBS-D)
- Constipation (IBS-C)
- Mixed/alternating diarrhea and constipation (IBS-M)
- Bloating and gas
- Mucus in stool
- Urgency
- Feeling of incomplete evacuation

**Subtypes:**
- **IBS-D:** Predominantly diarrhea
- **IBS-C:** Predominantly constipation
- **IBS-M:** Mixed (alternating)
- **IBS-U:** Unsubtyped

**Triggers:**
- Certain foods (dairy, gluten, FODMAPs, fatty foods, caffeine, alcohol)
- Stress and anxiety
- Hormonal changes
- Infections (post-infectious IBS)

**Important:** IBS is a "diagnosis of exclusion" - other conditions must be ruled out first.

## Evidence Requirements

### 1. Medical Diagnosis

**Required:**
- Formal IBS diagnosis from physician (gastroenterologist or primary care)
- Documentation that other conditions ruled out (colonoscopy, imaging, labs)

**Diagnostic Criteria (Rome IV):**
- Recurrent abdominal pain at least 1 day per week in last 3 months
- Associated with 2+ of:
  - Related to defecation
  - Change in stool frequency
  - Change in stool appearance
- Symptoms for at least 6 months

**Tests to Rule Out Other Conditions:**
- Colonoscopy (rules out inflammatory bowel disease, cancer)
- Blood tests (celiac disease, inflammation)
- Stool tests (infection, parasites)
- Imaging (CT, ultrasound)

**Important:** Normal test results support IBS diagnosis (shows no other cause found).

### 2. Symptom Documentation - CRITICAL

**Track Symptoms:**
- **Frequency:** Daily? Multiple times per week?
- **Bowel movements:** Number per day, consistency (using Bristol Stool Scale), urgency
- **Abdominal pain:** Severity (1-10), location, duration, what relieves it
- **Distress:** Impact on daily life
- **Triggers:** Foods, stress, situations
- **Functional impact:** Work missed, activities avoided, social limitations

**Keep IBS Diary:**
- Daily symptoms for 2-3 months
- Bowel movement frequency and type
- Pain levels
- Activities affected
- Medications taken
- Work/social impacts

**Example Entry:**
```
Date: 1/15/2025
BMs: 6 times (Bristol type 7 - watery diarrhea)
Pain: 7/10, cramping, lower abdomen, lasted 4 hours
Urgency: Had to rush to bathroom 4 times, afraid of accident
Missed: Morning work meeting, couldn't leave house until noon
Medications: Imodium (limited help), Bentyl
Distress level: High - constant worry about symptoms
```

### 3. Treatment Records

**Document:**
- Medications tried:
  - **For diarrhea:** Loperamide (Imodium), diphenoxylate
  - **For constipation:** Fiber, laxatives, Linzess, Amitiza
  - **For pain:** Antispasmodics (Bentyl, Levsin), tricyclic antidepressants
  - **Others:** Probiotics, rifaximin (Xifaxan)
- Dietary modifications:
  - Low FODMAP diet
  - Gluten-free
  - Dairy-free
  - Food elimination trials
- Treatment response (helps or doesn't help)
- Side effects
- Multiple failed treatments (shows severity)

**Gastroenterology Evaluation:**
- Specialist diagnosis strengthens claim
- Colonoscopy report
- Treatment recommendations

### 4. Functional Impact Documentation

**Employment:**
- Work absences due to IBS
- Tardiness from morning symptoms
- Need for bathroom access (reasonable accommodation)
- Difficulty with jobs requiring travel or limited bathroom access
- Job loss or inability to work certain jobs

**Daily Life:**
- Activities avoided (social events, restaurants, travel)
- Fear of leaving home
- Anxiety about bathroom availability
- Impact on relationships
- Sleep disruption from symptoms

**Social Impact:**
- Social isolation
- Avoiding restaurants, parties, events
- Fear of embarrassment
- Impact on intimate relationships

### 5. Service Connection Proof

**Direct Service Connection (Less Common):**
- IBS diagnosed during service
- Service medical records documenting GI symptoms
- Continuity from service to present

**Secondary Service Connection (Very Common):**

IBS secondary to another service-connected condition:

**Common Primary Conditions:**

**1. PTSD (Most Common):**
- Gut-brain axis
- Chronic stress worsens IBS
- Hyperarousal affects GI function
- Very strong medical connection
- High success rate

**2. GAD/Depression:**
- Anxiety directly impacts gut function
- Medications can affect GI system
- Similar to PTSD connection

**3. GERD:**
- GI conditions often co-occur
- Shared pathophysiology
- Medications affect entire GI tract

**4. Medications:**
- NSAIDs for pain conditions
- Antibiotics for infections
- Other medications affecting GI tract

**5. Infectious Causes:**
- Service-connected infection → post-infectious IBS
- Deployment dysentery → chronic IBS
- Gulf War gastrointestinal exposure

**Gulf War Presumptive:**
- Functional GI disorders are presumptive for Gulf War veterans
- Chronic symptoms with no identifiable cause
- Service in Southwest Asia during Gulf War era

**Burn Pit Exposure (Proposed):**
- VA considering IBS as presumptive for burn pit exposure
- Check current status

### 6. Nexus Opinion

Medical opinion linking IBS to service or service-connected condition (unless presumptive).

**Strong Nexus Includes:**
- Review of service and medical records
- Medical explanation of connection
- Timeline analysis
- "At least as likely as not" statement
- Supporting medical literature

**Example Nexus (Secondary to PTSD):**
"It is at least as likely as not that the veteran's Irritable Bowel Syndrome is secondary to service-connected PTSD. Medical literature extensively documents the gut-brain axis and the relationship between chronic stress/anxiety and IBS. PTSD-related hyperarousal, chronic stress, and altered autonomic nervous system function directly impact gastrointestinal motility and sensitivity. Additionally, the veteran's PTSD medications may contribute to GI symptoms. The temporal relationship supports this connection, as IBS symptoms began after PTSD onset."

**Example Nexus (Gulf War Presumptive):**
"The veteran served in Southwest Asia during the Gulf War era and has chronic functional gastrointestinal symptoms consistent with IBS. This qualifies as a presumptive condition under Gulf War regulations. No additional nexus is required."

## C&P Examination Preparation

### Before the Exam

**1. Prepare Detailed Symptom Description:**
- Frequency of bowel movements (per day/week)
- Type (diarrhea, constipation, mixed)
- Urgency and accidents
- Abdominal pain (severity, frequency, duration)
- Bloating, gas, cramping
- How constant the abdominal distress is

**2. Bring IBS Symptom Diary:**
- At least 2-3 months of daily tracking
- Shows pattern and frequency
- Critical evidence

**3. Document Functional Impact:**
- Work absences, missed opportunities
- Social activities avoided
- Travel limitations
- Relationship impacts
- Anxiety about symptoms

**4. List All Treatments:**
- Medications (current and past)
- Dietary modifications
- What works, what doesn't
- Complementary approaches tried

**5. Bring Supporting Documents:**
- Gastroenterology records
- Colonoscopy report
- Medication list
- Personal statement
- Symptom diary

### During the Exam

**Examiner Will:**
- Review medical records
- Ask about symptom frequency and severity
- Ask about functional impact
- Review treatment history
- Physical exam (abdominal palpation)

**DO:**
- Describe frequency honestly and specifically
- Use the word "frequent" or "constant" appropriately
- Emphasize abdominal distress level
- Explain functional limitations in detail
- Give specific examples of impact
- Mention anxiety about symptoms (compounds problem)
- Discuss all dietary restrictions
- Explain connection to service or service-connected condition

**DON'T:**
- Say symptoms are "occasional" if they're frequent
- Minimize impact on daily life
- Forget to mention abdominal distress (not just diarrhea)
- Exaggerate (be honest and specific)

**Critical Points to Emphasize:**

**For 10% Rating:**
- "I have frequent episodes of IBS symptoms - multiple times per week."
- "I have abdominal pain and cramping regularly."
- "I have diarrhea [or constipation, or both] frequently."
- "This impacts my work and social life."

**For 30% Rating:**
- "I have more or less constant abdominal distress - nearly every day."
- "I have severe IBS symptoms."
- "I have diarrhea [or alternating diarrhea and constipation] very frequently."
- "I'm constantly worried about having an accident."
- "I've missed significant work time."
- "I avoid social situations because of my symptoms."
- "The pain and discomfort are with me most of the time."

**Specific Examples to Provide:**
- "Last month I had to leave work early 8 times due to IBS."
- "I go to the bathroom 6-10 times per day when symptoms are active."
- "I can't travel because I need frequent bathroom access."
- "I've turned down job opportunities because they required travel."
- "I don't go to restaurants anymore because of fear of symptoms."

## Claim Strategy

### Initial Claim Strategy

**Step 1: Get Proper Diagnosis**
- See gastroenterologist if possible
- Get colonoscopy (rules out other conditions)
- Ensure IBS diagnosis clear in records
- Document subtype (IBS-D, IBS-C, IBS-M)

**Step 2: Document Symptoms Thoroughly**
- Keep detailed symptom diary for 2-3 months BEFORE filing
- Track frequency, severity, functional impact
- Critical evidence for rating level

**Step 3: Try Treatments**
- Shows severity when symptoms persist despite treatment
- Document medications tried
- Document dietary modifications
- Shows you're doing everything you can

**Step 4: Determine Service Connection Path**

**Gulf War Presumptive (If Applicable):**
- Service in Southwest Asia during Gulf War
- Chronic unexplained GI symptoms
- Presumptive - no nexus required

**Secondary to Service-Connected Condition:**
- Most common: PTSD, anxiety, depression
- Also: GERD, medications, infectious causes
- Get nexus opinion

**Direct Service Connection:**
- IBS during/shortly after service
- Less common but possible

**Step 5: Obtain Nexus (Unless Presumptive)**
- Gastroenterologist or primary care
- Provide information about primary service-connected condition
- Medical literature on gut-brain axis for PTSD connection

**Step 6: File Comprehensively**
Submit:
- VA Form 21-526EZ
- Medical records with IBS diagnosis
- Colonoscopy/imaging reports (showing no other cause)
- Medication records
- Symptom diary (critical)
- Nexus letter (unless presumptive)
- Personal statement with functional impact
- Employment records if relevant
- Lay statements from family

### Secondary to PTSD Strategy (Most Common)

**Very Strong Medical Connection:**
- Gut-brain axis well-established
- Chronic stress affects GI function
- PTSD medications can contribute
- High success rate

**Evidence:**
1. Service-connected PTSD
2. IBS diagnosis
3. Symptom diary
4. Nexus: PTSD stress/hyperarousal → IBS
5. Timeline: IBS developed after PTSD

**Example:**
Marine Corps veteran with 70% PTSD develops IBS. Gastroenterologist provides nexus explaining gut-brain axis connection. Granted 10% for IBS secondary to PTSD. Combined rating increases from 70% to 73%.

### Increase Rating Strategy

**From 0% to 10%:**
- Document increased frequency of symptoms
- Show symptoms are "frequent" not just "occasional"
- At least 2-3 months of symptom diary showing regular symptoms

**From 10% to 30%:**
- Document "more or less constant" abdominal distress
- Show severity level
- Emphasize diarrhea (or alternating)
- Document significant functional impairment
- Work absences
- Social limitations
- Multiple failed treatments
- Symptom diary showing daily/near-daily symptoms

**Evidence for 30%:**
- Updated symptom diary showing near-constant distress
- Multiple bowel movements daily
- Work absence records
- Updated personal statement emphasizing severity
- Gastroenterology records noting severity
- Failed multiple treatment attempts

## Success Stories

### Case Study 1: IBS Secondary to PTSD - 10% Rating
**Background:** Army combat veteran, 70% PTSD, developed chronic GI symptoms.

**Strategy:**
- Service-connected PTSD
- Developed IBS symptoms 3 years after PTSD diagnosis
- Gastroenterologist diagnosed IBS-D
- Colonoscopy normal (ruled out IBD)
- Symptom diary: 4-5 days per week with diarrhea, abdominal pain, urgency
- Taking Bentyl and Imodium with limited relief
- Psychiatrist provided nexus: "IBS is secondary to PTSD through gut-brain axis and chronic stress"
- Personal statement: frequent bathroom urgency affecting work

**Result:** Granted 10% for IBS secondary to PTSD ($171.23/month). Combined rating increased from 70% to 73%.

**Key Factors:** Gastroenterology evaluation, symptom diary showing frequent symptoms, nexus from treating provider, documented functional impact.

### Case Study 2: IBS Increased from 10% to 30%
**Background:** Marine Corps veteran, initially rated 10% for IBS, symptoms worsened significantly.

**Strategy:**
- Initially granted 10% in 2018
- Symptoms progressively worsened despite treatment
- 2022-2023: Kept detailed diary showing:
  - Daily abdominal pain and cramping
  - 6-10 bowel movements per day (watery diarrhea)
  - Constant worry about symptoms
  - Missed 25 days of work in one year
- Tried multiple medications: Bentyl, Viberzi, Xifaxan, low FODMAP diet
- Gastroenterologist letter: "Severe IBS with daily symptoms despite maximal medical therapy"
- Updated personal statement emphasizing "constant" distress
- Spouse statement corroborating impact on daily life

**Result:** Increased from 10% to 30% ($171.23 to $524.31/month).

**Key Factors:** Detailed symptom diary showing near-daily symptoms, documented functional impairment (work absences), failed multiple treatments, medical support for severity.

### Case Study 3: Gulf War Presumptive - 10% Rating
**Background:** Army Gulf War veteran, chronic IBS since deployment.

**Strategy:**
- Service in Saudi Arabia/Kuwait 1990-1991
- Chronic GI symptoms began during deployment
- IBS diagnosis 1995
- Filed as Gulf War presumptive (chronic functional GI disorder)
- No nexus required
- Symptom diary showing frequent diarrhea, cramping, bloating
- Taking daily Imodium and dicyclomine

**Result:** Granted 10% as Gulf War presumptive condition. No nexus required.

**Key Factors:** Gulf War service, chronic unexplained GI symptoms, presumptive service connection (simplified claim).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the maximum rating for IBS?
30%. There is no higher rating for IBS alone. However, if IBS causes other complications or if you have other service-connected GI conditions, those can be rated separately.

### How do I prove IBS is secondary to PTSD?
Get a nexus letter from a doctor (psychiatrist, gastroenterologist, or primary care) explaining the gut-brain axis connection. The medical literature strongly supports the relationship between chronic stress/PTSD and IBS.

### Do I need a colonoscopy to get an IBS rating?
Not strictly required, but helpful. Colonoscopy rules out other conditions (IBD, cancer) and supports IBS diagnosis as a diagnosis of exclusion. Strengthens your claim.

### What's the difference between 10% and 30%?
10% is "frequent episodes" with "abdominal distress." 30% is "severe" with "more or less constant abdominal distress" and specifically mentions diarrhea. The key is near-daily symptoms vs. multiple times per week, and constant distress vs. episodic.

### How do I prove "constant" abdominal distress for 30%?
Keep detailed symptom diary showing daily or near-daily symptoms for at least 2-3 months. Document pain levels, frequency, and how it affects you every day. Show it's not just occasional flare-ups.

### Can IBS be service-connected for Gulf War veterans?
Yes. Functional GI disorders are Gulf War presumptive conditions. If you served in Southwest Asia during Gulf War era and have chronic IBS, it's presumptive - no nexus required.

### Will my IBS rating be reduced if symptoms improve?
Possibly, if your symptoms significantly and consistently improve. However, IBS is typically a chronic condition with waxing and waning symptoms. Continue treatment and document ongoing symptoms.

### Can I get separate ratings for IBS and GERD?
Yes. They are different conditions rated under different diagnostic codes. Both can be secondary to the same primary condition (e.g., both secondary to PTSD).

### What if I have IBS-C (constipation)?
Can still be rated. The diagnostic code mentions "diarrhea, or alternating diarrhea and constipation" for 30%, but IBS-C with severe constant distress could also qualify. Document severity and functional impact.

### Does stress make IBS worse?
Yes, and this is relevant for service connection. If you have service-connected PTSD/anxiety, stress from that condition directly worsens IBS, supporting secondary service connection.

### Can medications for service-connected conditions cause IBS?
Yes. Some medications affect GI function. This could support secondary service connection. Document timeline of medication use and IBS symptom onset.

### How important is the symptom diary?
Extremely important. It's the best evidence of frequency and severity. VA can't just take your word that symptoms are "frequent" or "constant" - the diary provides objective documentation.

### Should I mention anxiety about IBS symptoms?
Yes. Fear of having an accident, anxiety about bathroom availability, and resulting social isolation are all part of the functional impact. This can support higher rating and may warrant separate mental health claim if severe.

### Can IBS prevent me from working?
If IBS is severe enough that combined with other service-connected conditions it prevents substantially gainful employment, you may qualify for TDIU. Would need one condition at 60% or combined 70% with one at 40%.

## Final Thoughts

IBS is a chronic, often debilitating condition that significantly impacts quality of life. The constant worry about symptoms, need for bathroom access, and social limitations are real and should be recognized.

**Keys to a Successful IBS Claim:**

1. **Get proper diagnosis** - Gastroenterologist, colonoscopy to rule out other conditions
2. **Document symptoms meticulously** - Detailed diary for 2-3 months is critical
3. **Establish service connection:**
   - Gulf War presumptive (if applicable - easiest path)
   - Secondary to PTSD/anxiety (very common, strong medical support)
   - Secondary to medications or infections
   - Direct if symptoms during/after service
4. **Emphasize functional impact** - Work, social, daily life limitations
5. **Try treatments** - Shows severity when symptoms persist despite treatment
6. **Get strong nexus** (unless presumptive) - Gut-brain axis for PTSD connection
7. **Be specific about frequency** - "Frequent" for 10%, "constant" for 30%

**Remember:** IBS may only rate up to 30%, but it often combines with other conditions (PTSD, GERD, depression, etc.) significantly increasing your combined rating.

IBS is not "just in your head" - it's a real medical condition with a recognized gut-brain axis connection. If your IBS is related to service-connected conditions like PTSD, or if you're a Gulf War veteran with chronic GI symptoms, you deserve compensation.

**File your IBS claim. You earned it. Track your symptoms. Build your evidence.**
