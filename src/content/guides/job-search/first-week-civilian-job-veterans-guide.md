# First Week at Your Civilian Job: Veteran's Survival Guide

## Bottom Line Up Front

Your first week at a civilian job sets the tone for your entire tenure. Veterans often face culture shock—different expectations, communication styles, and workplace norms. This guide walks you through day-by-day what to expect, how to navigate common challenges, and how to make a strong first impression. Approach this week as a reconnaissance mission: observe, learn, and adapt. The veterans who succeed are those who prepare for the transition, not just the job.

## Why This Matters for Veterans

The first week is challenging because:
- Everything is different from military structure
- You don't know the unwritten rules yet
- You want to prove yourself but don't know how things work
- Small missteps can create lasting impressions
- Culture shock is real and disorienting

What's at stake:
- First impressions are hard to change
- Relationships formed early matter throughout your tenure
- How you handle the learning curve signals your adaptability
- Getting off to a good start builds confidence

Veterans have advantages:
- You're used to new environments
- You've started new roles before
- Discipline and attention to detail serve you well
- You know how to observe and adapt

## Before Day One

### Mental Preparation

**Expect culture shock:** It's normal. The pace, formality, and priorities will be different.

**Embrace beginner's mind:** You're experienced, but you're new here. Be humble and curious.

**Plan your logistics:** Know exactly how to get there, where to park, what to wear, who to meet.

**Prepare your introduction:** Have a brief (30-second) intro ready that translates your background.

**Get rest:** First week is exhausting. Start well-rested.

### Logistics Checklist

- [ ] Confirm start time and location
- [ ] Know who to ask for when you arrive
- [ ] Plan your commute (do a test run if possible)
- [ ] Prepare appropriate outfit (research company dress code)
- [ ] Gather any required documents (ID, tax forms, etc.)
- [ ] Set up your phone with key contacts (HR, manager)
- [ ] Prepare notebook and pen
- [ ] Have lunch plan (bring food or know nearby options)

## Day-by-Day Guide

### Day 1: Orientation and Setup

**What to expect:**
- HR paperwork and benefits enrollment
- IT setup (computer, email, access)
- Facility tour and introductions
- Meeting your manager and immediate team
- Possible welcome lunch or gathering

**Your priorities:**
- Complete all paperwork carefully
- Set up all systems and access
- Learn people's names (take notes)
- Ask about immediate expectations
- Observe the environment

**Tips:**
- Write down everyone's name and role
- Don't be afraid to ask "what's the best way to [X]?"
- Arrive early but not too early (10-15 minutes)
- Bring everything you might need

**Common challenges:**
- Feeling overwhelmed by information
- Not knowing where things are
- Forgetting names immediately

**How to handle:**
- Take notes constantly
- It's okay to ask people their names again
- Focus on the most important information first

### Day 2: Learning the Landscape

**What to expect:**
- More setup and onboarding activities
- Beginning to understand your actual work
- Additional introductions
- Starting to learn processes and systems

**Your priorities:**
- Continue learning names and roles
- Understand team structure and key stakeholders
- Start understanding your actual responsibilities
- Identify who to ask for different types of help

**Tips:**
- Draw an org chart for yourself
- Identify who the "go-to" people are for different things
- Pay attention to informal dynamics, not just formal structure
- Notice how people communicate (email, chat, in-person)

**Questions to ask:**
- "Who should I go to for questions about [X]?"
- "What's the best way to communicate with the team?"
- "Are there any resources I should be reading?"

### Day 3: Getting Into the Work

**What to expect:**
- Starting actual job-related tasks
- More independence expected
- Understanding workflows and processes
- Continued introductions and relationship building

**Your priorities:**
- Complete any assigned tasks well
- Ask clarifying questions before starting work
- Continue building relationships
- Start understanding team priorities

**Tips:**
- Underpromise and overdeliver on first assignments
- Ask for feedback on your work
- Don't wait until you're stuck—ask questions early
- Take initiative where appropriate

**Common challenges:**
- Uncertainty about how to do tasks
- Different processes than you're used to
- Not knowing if you're meeting expectations

**How to handle:**
- Ask before you guess
- Document what you learn
- Request feedback proactively

### Day 4: Building Momentum

**What to expect:**
- Settling into routines
- More complex work may be assigned
- Team meetings and collaboration
- Better understanding of rhythm

**Your priorities:**
- Contribute in meetings (but don't dominate)
- Deliver quality work on any assignments
- Identify quick wins you can achieve
- Solidify key relationships

**Tips:**
- In meetings, listen more than you speak initially
- Offer to help when you see opportunities
- Start identifying where you can add value
- Learn the team's priorities and pain points

### Day 5: Reflection and Planning

**What to expect:**
- Possibly a check-in with your manager
- Starting to feel more comfortable
- Understanding of where you fit
- Preparation for week 2

**Your priorities:**
- Have a productive check-in with manager
- Identify questions and gaps for next week
- Build on relationships started
- Set goals for week 2

**Questions for your manager:**
- "How do you think my first week went?"
- "What should I prioritize next week?"
- "Is there anything I should do differently?"
- "What does success look like in my first 30 days?"

## Navigating Culture Differences

### Communication Styles

**Military vs. Civilian:**
- Military: Direct, acronym-heavy, formal structure
- Civilian: Often indirect, professional but casual, less formal

**What to do:**
- Avoid military jargon completely
- Soften direct communication slightly
- Use "I think" or "In my experience" rather than declarative statements
- Observe how others communicate and mirror it

**Example translation:**
- Military: "The plan is flawed. We need to fix it."
- Civilian: "I'm wondering if we might want to reconsider this part of the plan. What do you think?"

### Hierarchy and Authority

**Military vs. Civilian:**
- Military: Clear rank structure, respect for position
- Civilian: Flatter structure, authority from expertise and relationships

**What to do:**
- Don't assume rank equals competence (or incompetence)
- Respect everyone regardless of title
- Build credibility through contribution, not position
- Ask how decisions get made here

### Pace and Urgency

**Military vs. Civilian:**
- Military: Everything is urgent, hurry up and wait
- Civilian: More measured pace, different priorities

**What to do:**
- Match the team's pace (not ahead or behind)
- Don't assume your sense of urgency is shared
- Ask about timelines rather than assuming
- Remember: fast isn't always valued as much as thorough

### Social Dynamics

**Military vs. Civilian:**
- Military: Automatic camaraderie, shared identity
- Civilian: Relationships built individually over time

**What to do:**
- Invest in relationship building
- Participate in social opportunities (lunch, events)
- Don't expect instant camaraderie
- Be friendly and approachable

## Making a Strong First Impression

### What People Notice

**Positive signals:**
- Shows up prepared and on time
- Asks thoughtful questions
- Listens more than talks
- Friendly and approachable
- Takes notes
- Follows through on commitments
- Humble but confident

**Negative signals:**
- Talks too much about military experience
- Seems to know it all already
- Criticizes how things are done
- Doesn't engage with colleagues
- Overly formal or stiff
- Dismissive of non-military perspectives

### Your 30-Second Introduction

When meeting people, be ready with a brief intro:

**Template:**
"Hi, I'm [Name]. I just joined the [Team] as a [Role]. I'm transitioning from [X years] in the [Branch], where I worked in [civilian-translated function]. I'm excited to be here and learn about [specific area]."

**Example:**
"Hi, I'm Sarah. I just joined the Operations team as a Program Manager. I'm transitioning from 8 years in the Navy, where I led logistics and project management. I'm excited to learn about how [Company] handles its supply chain."

### Building Early Relationships

**Key people to connect with:**
- Your direct manager (obviously)
- Teammates you'll work closely with
- Administrative support (they know everything)
- People who've been there a while
- Other veterans (if any)

**How to connect:**
- Express genuine interest in their work
- Ask for advice on navigating the organization
- Offer to help when you can
- Remember personal details they share

## Common First-Week Challenges

### "I feel like I don't know anything"

**Reality:** You're not supposed to know everything yet.

**What to do:**
- Embrace the learning curve
- Focus on what you can learn, not what you don't know
- Recognize that asking questions shows engagement
- Trust that competence will come with time

### "I'm not sure if I'm meeting expectations"

**Reality:** First week expectations are usually low.

**What to do:**
- Ask your manager directly what success looks like
- Focus on the basics: show up, pay attention, complete tasks
- Don't assume silence means displeasure
- Request feedback proactively

### "Everything is so different from the military"

**Reality:** Yes, it is. That's expected.

**What to do:**
- Acknowledge the culture shock to yourself
- Don't try to make the new environment like the military
- Focus on adapting, not comparing
- Find one veteran or mentor to talk to

### "I'm exhausted"

**Reality:** First weeks are draining for everyone.

**What to do:**
- Get extra rest this week
- Don't schedule other demanding activities
- It will get easier as you settle in
- Be patient with yourself

### "I made a mistake"

**Reality:** Everyone makes mistakes, especially when new.

**What to do:**
- Own it quickly
- Fix it if possible
- Learn from it
- Don't dwell on it
- It's rarely as big a deal as you think

## What Not to Do

### Don't Talk Too Much About the Military

Excessive military references:
- Can alienate civilian colleagues
- May come across as not committed to civilian career
- Creates distance rather than connection

**Instead:**
- Share military background when relevant or asked
- Translate experiences to civilian context
- Focus on present and future, not past

### Don't Critique Before You Understand

You might see things that seem inefficient or wrong. But:
- You don't have full context yet
- There may be reasons you don't understand
- Premature criticism damages relationships

**Instead:**
- Observe and understand first
- Ask questions to learn why things are done this way
- Save suggestions for after you've built credibility

### Don't Isolate Yourself

It's tempting to keep your head down and work, but:
- Relationships matter as much as work output
- You need allies and information sources
- Isolation signals disengagement

**Instead:**
- Participate in social activities
- Have lunch with colleagues
- Engage in casual conversation
- Be approachable

### Don't Compare Everything to the Military

Constant comparisons:
- Suggest you wish you were still there
- Can come across as critical
- Don't help you adapt

**Instead:**
- Accept the civilian environment on its own terms
- Notice differences privately, adapt publicly
- Focus on what works here, not what's different
