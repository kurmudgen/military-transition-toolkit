# Navy IT to Software Developer: How Bootcamp Changed Everything

## Quick Facts

**Background:**
- Branch: Navy
- Rank at Separation: E6 (Petty Officer First Class)
- MOS/AFSC/Rating: IT (Information Systems Technician)
- Years of Service: 10 years
- Separation Type: Voluntary Separation
- Location: Norfolk, VA → Remote (Seattle company)

**Challenge:**
Despite 10 years of IT experience, Sarah felt stuck in support roles and wanted to transition into software development but lacked formal programming education.

**Outcome:**
Completed coding bootcamp via SkillBridge, landed software developer position earning $105,000 with fully remote work at a Seattle-based tech startup.

**Timeline:**
10 months from bootcamp enrollment to job offer

## Background Story

### Military Career

Sarah joined the Navy at 21 after completing two years at a community college. As an IT specialist, she spent a decade managing networks, troubleshooting systems, maintaining servers, and supporting communications aboard ships and at shore installations. She deployed twice aboard an aircraft carrier, ensuring the ship's IT infrastructure operated 24/7 in demanding conditions.

Her work was essential but increasingly felt repetitive. She configured user accounts, reset passwords, imaged computers, and maintained legacy systems. While she developed deep expertise in network administration and cybersecurity basics, she envied the developers who created the applications her team supported.

During her second deployment, Sarah started teaching herself Python through free online courses during downtime. She built small automation scripts that saved her team dozens of hours monthly. Her chief noticed and encouraged her: "You're solving problems, not just maintaining systems. That's what developers do. You should pursue this."

As her 10-year mark approached, Sarah faced a decision: reenlist for another contract or take the leap into software development. She had a family, a mortgage, and the security of military benefits. But she also had a growing conviction that she was capable of more.

### The Challenge

Sarah's primary obstacle was the catch-22: most software developer jobs required a CS degree or professional development experience. She had neither. Her Navy IT experience was impressive for infrastructure roles but wasn't seen as relevant for software development positions.

Her second challenge was financial. Bootcamps cost $15,000-$20,000, and she couldn't afford to quit her job to attend full-time. She also had two kids and couldn't relocate to San Francisco or New York for bootcamp programs that required in-person attendance.

The third challenge was credibility. She worried that bootcamp graduates were viewed as "lesser" than CS degree holders. Would companies take her seriously? Would she be able to compete against 22-year-olds with fresh CS degrees who knew the latest frameworks?

Finally, she battled imposter syndrome intensified by gender dynamics. Tech was male-dominated, and she worried about facing bias as a woman veteran trying to break into software development.

## Key Tactics That Worked

### Tactic 1: SkillBridge for Full-Time Bootcamp

**What They Did:**
Sarah used SkillBridge to attend Code Platoon full-time during her last 4 months of service. This meant she received her military paycheck while attending bootcamp—effectively getting paid $4,000/month to learn to code. She graduated from bootcamp and separated from the Navy in the same week.

**Why It Worked:**
Full-time immersive bootcamps have significantly better outcomes than part-time programs. Students who can focus 60-70 hours/week learn faster and build more impressive portfolios. SkillBridge eliminated the financial risk, allowing Sarah to commit fully without worrying about income. She also graduated with military benefits intact (GI Bill, VA healthcare) while having new career credentials.

**How You Can Apply It:**
If you're within 180 days of separation, apply for SkillBridge. Search for coding bootcamps that participate (Code Platoon, Vets Who Code, Skill Distillery). Apply 6-9 months before your separation date (competitive programs fill up). Treat bootcamp like a deployment—full commitment, no distractions. The intensity is temporary, but the career change is permanent.

### Tactic 2: Portfolio Over Perfect Résumé

**What They Did:**
Instead of obsessing over her résumé, Sarah focused on building 5 strong portfolio projects that demonstrated real capabilities. Each project was deployed (live on the internet), had clean code on GitHub, and solved a specific problem. She linked to these projects from her résumé, LinkedIn, and GitHub profile.

**Why It Worked:**
Hiring managers for developer roles care more about what you can build than where you worked. Sarah's portfolio demonstrated that she could: write clean code, deploy applications, work with databases, implement user authentication, and build responsive interfaces. When interviewers asked, "Do you have professional development experience?" she responded, "Let me show you what I've built," and walked through her projects.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Build 3-5 substantial projects during bootcamp or self-study. Each should demonstrate different skills (one frontend-focused, one backend-focused, one full-stack). Deploy everything (use Heroku, Netlify, or Vercel—all have free tiers). Write clear README files explaining what the project does and technologies used. In interviews, reference specific projects: "In my veteran resource app, I implemented JWT authentication and integrated the VA benefits API..." Concrete examples beat abstract claims.

### Tactic 3: The Women Who Code Network

**What They Did:**
Sarah joined Women Who Code early in her journey and actively participated: attended virtual events, contributed to discussions, volunteered to help organize local meetups. She built relationships with female developers who offered advice, reviewed her projects, and made introductions to hiring managers.

**Why It Worked:**
Tech can feel isolating, especially for women and career changers. Women Who Code provided community, mentorship, and accountability. When Sarah faced self-doubt, her network reminded her she belonged. Several members gave candid feedback on her portfolio that dramatically improved its quality. One connection led directly to an interview. The network amplified her efforts.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Join relevant communities early: Women Who Code (for women), VetsWhoCode (for veterans), 100Devs (for bootcamp grads), local developer meetups. Be an active participant, not a lurker—introduce yourself, ask questions, offer help when you can. Networking isn't transactional; it's relational. Build genuine connections. Many jobs are filled through referrals before they're publicly posted. Your network becomes your net worth.

### Tactic 4: Daily Algorithm Practice

**What They Did:**
Starting in month 7, Sarah solved one LeetCode problem every single day. She focused on easy and medium problems across common patterns: arrays, strings, hash maps, two pointers, recursion, dynamic programming. She kept a notebook of patterns and solutions, reviewing them weekly.

**Why It Worked:**
Most technical interviews include algorithmic coding challenges. Companies use these to assess problem-solving ability and coding fundamentals. Daily practice transformed Sarah's performance from "panicked and stuck" to "calm and systematic." She learned to recognize patterns quickly and articulate her thought process clearly. By interview #5, she felt confident even when encountering unfamiliar problems.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Start LeetCode or HackerRank early—don't wait until you have interviews scheduled. Focus on easy/medium problems (hard problems rarely appear in interviews for junior roles). Learn patterns, not just individual solutions. Use the "REACTO" method: Repeat the question, Example inputs/outputs, Approach (pseudocode), Code it, Test, Optimize. Practice explaining your thought process out loud while coding. Consistency beats intensity—one problem daily is better than 10 problems once a week.

### Tactic 5: Applied to "Reach" Positions

**What They Did:**
Sarah applied to jobs even when she didn't meet 100% of the listed qualifications. If a job description said "3+ years of React experience" and she had 6 months, she applied anyway, emphasizing her rapid learning ability and portfolio projects using React.

**Why It Worked:**
Job descriptions are wish lists, not requirements. Many companies hire based on potential, especially for early-career roles. By self-selecting out of "reach" positions, candidates eliminate themselves before companies can. Sarah's military background and clear demonstration of learning ability made hiring managers willing to overlook the experience gap. She received offers from companies whose job descriptions she didn't fully match.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Apply if you meet 60-70% of qualifications. Focus your application on what you DO have and how you've demonstrated rapid learning. Use phrases like "Quickly mastered [technology] as demonstrated in [project]." Let the company decide if you're qualified. The worst outcome is no response (which happens anyway). The best outcome is an interview and offer. Advocate for yourself—no one else will.

## Obstacles Overcome

### Obstacle 1: Imposter Syndrome as Female Veteran in Tech

**The Problem:**
Sarah constantly questioned whether she belonged. During bootcamp, when she struggled with JavaScript concepts, her first thought was "Maybe I'm not cut out for this." In job interviews, when male interviewers asked technical questions, she worried they were judging her more harshly as a woman. The tech industry's reputation for being unwelcoming to women compounded her self-doubt.

**How They Overcame It:**
Sarah's Women Who Code mentor gave her a reframe: "Imposter syndrome means you're challenging yourself. Comfortable people don't feel it. Struggling means you're learning." She also connected Sarah with three other women veterans in tech who shared similar feelings and coping strategies.

Sarah started keeping a "wins document"—every time she solved a difficult problem, received positive feedback, or learned something new, she wrote it down. When imposter syndrome struck, she reviewed the document as evidence of her capability. She also practiced positive self-talk: instead of "I can't do this," she said "I can't do this YET."

**Lesson Learned:**
Imposter syndrome doesn't mean you're an imposter—it means you're growing. Everyone in tech feels it, regardless of gender or background. The difference between those who succeed and those who quit is action. Document your progress, build a support network, and remember: struggling is part of learning, not evidence of inadequacy.

### Obstacle 2: First Technical Interview Disaster

**The Problem:**
Sarah's first technical interview was a disaster. Asked to write a function to reverse a string, her mind went blank. She typed, deleted, typed again. She apologized repeatedly. The interviewer ended the call after 20 minutes. Sarah called her partner in tears, convinced she'd made a terrible mistake leaving the military.

**How They Overcame It:**
Her bootcamp instructor normalized the experience: "Everyone bombs their first technical interview. It's a rite of passage. The point isn't to do well—it's to learn what to expect." He had Sarah schedule mock interviews with three people from the bootcamp's alumni network.

Sarah also changed her approach. Instead of trying to solve problems silently in her head, she practiced thinking out loud: "Okay, I need to reverse a string. Let me think about the approach. I could use a loop to iterate backwards, or I could convert it to an array and use the reverse method..." Verbalizing her thought process reduced anxiety and helped interviewers follow her reasoning even when her final solution wasn't perfect.

By her fourth technical interview, Sarah felt conversational rather than interrogated.

**Lesson Learned:**
Technical interviews are a learnable skill separate from coding ability. Failure is information, not identity. Every bad interview teaches you what to expect and how to improve. Schedule mock interviews early and often. Practice thinking out loud—communication is as important as correct answers. The first interview is always the hardest; it gets easier.

### Obstacle 3: Choosing Between Two Offers

**The Problem:**
Sarah received two offers simultaneously. Company A offered $95K at an established company with great benefits and work-life balance. Company B offered $105K at a startup with equity, but less job security and longer hours. She agonized over the decision for three days, making pro/con lists and losing sleep.

**How They Overcame It:**
Sarah reached out to veteran mentors in tech for advice. One asked: "Which role will teach you more in the next two years?" That question reframed the decision. Company B's startup environment meant she'd wear multiple hats, work on diverse problems, and accelerate her learning. Company A was stable but potentially slower growth.

She also negotiated. She asked Company B to match Company A's 401(k) match and add an extra week of PTO. They agreed. She accepted Company B's offer, but the negotiation practice was valuable regardless of the outcome.

**Lesson Learned:**
When choosing between offers, prioritize learning and growth early in your career. Higher salary at a startup with equity upside and faster skill development beats stability in the first 3-5 years. Always negotiate—companies expect it and respect it. Reach out to mentors who've faced similar decisions; their outside perspective clarifies priorities.

## What They'd Do Differently

### Looking Back

18 months into her development career, Sarah reflected on her transition journey.

**Would Change:**

1. **Start Learning to Code Earlier**: "I wish I'd started teaching myself Python and JavaScript 12-18 months before separation instead of 6 months. The additional foundation would have made bootcamp easier and potentially shortened my job search. Start learning now, not when you're ready to transition."

2. **Build in Public Earlier**: "I kept my learning private for months, worried about looking foolish. I should have shared my progress on LinkedIn and Twitter from day one. Other veterans would have connected, I'd have built an audience, and recruiters would have noticed me sooner."

3. **Apply to More Startups Initially**: "I focused on big tech companies at first because I thought they were more stable. Startups actually hire veterans more readily because they value adaptability over credentials. I should have targeted startups from the beginning."

**Would Keep:**

1. **Choosing Veteran-Focused Bootcamp**: "Code Platoon understood military culture and built cohorts of veterans. We supported each other through challenges and hold each other accountable. A civilian bootcamp wouldn't have provided the same camaraderie and understanding."

2. **Investing Time in Networking**: "I initially thought networking was schmoozy and fake. It's actually about building genuine relationships. The time I spent in communities like Women Who Code and connecting with veteran developers paid massive dividends—connections led to interviews, advice, and ongoing support."

3. **Prioritizing Portfolio Quality**: "Some bootcamp grads rush through projects to check boxes. I spent extra time making my projects polished and deployed. That quality showed in interviews and differentiated me from other junior candidates."

## Advice for Others

### Who This Approach Works For

This path is ideal for veterans with some technical background (IT, signal, cyber) who want to transition into software development.

**Best fit if you:**
- Have basic computer literacy and comfort with technology
- Are within 180 days of separation (to use SkillBridge) or recently separated
- Can commit 60-70 hours/week to intensive learning for 3-4 months
- Enjoy problem-solving and logical thinking
- Are willing to start at entry-level (~$80K-$110K) with rapid growth potential
- Can tolerate ambiguity and continuous learning
- Want remote work flexibility and modern career options

**Might not work if:**
- You need immediate income and can't afford 3-4 months of training
- You strongly prefer hardware/infrastructure over software development
- You're not comfortable with constant change (tech evolves rapidly)
- You're locked into specific location without remote work options
- You expect immediate senior-level salary without building experience

## Key Takeaways

### For You to Remember

1. **SkillBridge Makes Bootcamp Risk-Free**: Get paid your military salary while attending full-time bootcamp. This eliminates financial risk and allows full commitment. Apply 6-9 months before separation.

2. **Portfolio Beats Pedigree**: Companies hire based on what you can build, not where you went to school. Five strong portfolio projects outweigh a CS degree for entry-level roles.

3. **Community Accelerates Success**: Join developer communities early (Women Who Code, VetsWhoCode, local meetups). Networking leads to opportunities, mentorship, and support during difficult moments.

4. **Technical Interviews Are Separate Skills**: Coding ability ≠ interview performance. Practice algorithms daily, do mock interviews, and learn to think out loud while coding. First interviews are always hardest.

5. **Start Before You're Ready**: You'll never feel 100% prepared. Start learning now with free resources. Apply to jobs even when you don't meet all requirements. Advocate for yourself.

### Success Factors

What made this work:
- **Full-time immersive bootcamp through SkillBridge**: Allowed complete focus and rapid skill development
- **Building quality portfolio projects**: Demonstrated capability beyond résumé claims
- **Active networking and community involvement**: Led to referrals, advice, and opportunities
- **Daily algorithm practice**: Prepared for technical interviews systematically
- **Applying broadly without self-selecting out**: Created more opportunities for success
- **Choosing startup for growth over stability**: Accelerated learning and career progression

### Warning Signs

What could have derailed them:
- **Part-time bootcamp while working** - Split focus reduces effectiveness. Solution: Use SkillBridge for full-time commitment.
- **Analysis paralysis choosing bootcamp** - Weeks spent debating delayed progress. Solution: Set decision deadline and commit.
- **Giving up after first interview failure** - Early failures are normal. Solution: Treat first interviews as learning experiences; schedule mocks.
- **Only applying to "perfect fit" jobs** - Limited opportunities unnecessarily. Solution: Apply to reach positions; let companies decide.
- **Isolation during learning** - Solo learning without community leads to discouragement. Solution: Join communities immediately; stay engaged.
