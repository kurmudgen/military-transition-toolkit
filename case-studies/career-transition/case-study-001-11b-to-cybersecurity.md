# From 11B Infantry to $95K Cybersecurity Analyst in 8 Months

## Quick Facts

**Background:**
- Branch: Army
- Rank at Separation: E5 (Sergeant)
- MOS/AFSC/Rating: 11B (Infantryman)
- Years of Service: 6 years
- Separation Type: ETS (End of Term of Service)
- Location: Fort Benning, GA → Austin, TX

**Challenge:**
Coming from a combat arms background with no formal IT experience, Marcus faced skepticism about transitioning into a technical field like cybersecurity.

**Outcome:**
Landed a cybersecurity analyst position at a mid-sized tech company earning $95,000 annually, plus benefits and remote work flexibility.

**Timeline:**
8 months from separation to job offer

## Background Story

### Military Career

Marcus enlisted in the Army at 19, driven by a desire to serve and gain discipline. As an 11B Infantryman, he completed two deployments to Afghanistan, leading fire teams and eventually serving as a squad leader. His responsibilities included tactical planning, risk assessment, threat analysis, and ensuring his team's security in hostile environments.

During his service, Marcus developed strong analytical skills—constantly assessing threats, identifying vulnerabilities in perimeters, and making quick decisions under pressure. He also became proficient with various communication systems and tactical networks, though he never considered himself "technical."

As his ETS approached, Marcus felt anxious. He loved the structure and mission of military life but knew he wanted something different. The idea of a desk job felt suffocating, yet he had a family to support. A chance conversation with a veteran recruiter at a transition seminar changed everything: "Your job in the infantry? You were doing risk assessment and threat analysis every single day. That's literally what cybersecurity analysts do."

### The Challenge

Marcus's biggest obstacle was imposter syndrome. He had no college degree, no IT certifications, and couldn't code. When he researched cybersecurity jobs, the requirements seemed impossible: "Bachelor's degree in Computer Science, 3+ years of experience with SIEM tools, proficiency in Python..." He didn't even know what a SIEM was.

His second challenge was time. With only 6 months until separation and a family depending on his income, he couldn't afford a 4-year degree or an extended job search. He needed a fast, proven path that wouldn't require significant upfront investment.

The third challenge was location. His wife had family in Austin, Texas, but he worried about competing against University of Texas CS graduates with perfect résumés. How could an infantryman with no technical background compete in one of the nation's hottest tech markets?

## The Strategy

### Month 1-3: Foundation Building

**What They Did:**
- Enrolled in the DoD SkillBridge program for the final 6 months of service (3 months before separation)
- Started studying for CompTIA Security+ certification (2-3 hours daily after duty hours)
- Joined the VetSec Slack community and Reddit's r/cybersecurity
- Created a LinkedIn profile highlighting transferable military skills
- Researched Austin's cybersecurity job market and company landscape

**Tools/Resources Used:**
- Professor Messer's free Security+ videos on YouTube
- CompTIA Security+ study guide from Jason Dion (Udemy, $12)
- Anki flashcard app for memorization
- FedVTE (free cybersecurity training for veterans from CISA)
- VetSec Slack workspace for community support

**Mistakes Made:**
- Initially tried to study everything at once (Security+, Python, Linux) and burned out after 2 weeks—learned to focus on one thing at a time
- Spent first 3 weeks applying to jobs before having any certifications—got zero responses and realized he needed credentials first
- Used military jargon in initial LinkedIn profile ("led kinetic operations against enemy combatants") that civilians didn't understand

**Key Breakthrough:**
A VetSec member reviewed Marcus's LinkedIn and résumé, translating his military experience into cybersecurity terms. "Risk assessment during combat operations" became "threat analysis and vulnerability assessment in high-stakes environments." "Maintained communications security" became "ensured operational security and protected sensitive communications infrastructure." The reframe was eye-opening.

### Month 4-6: Certification & SkillBridge

- Passed CompTIA Security+ exam on first attempt (scored 798/900)
- Started SkillBridge internship with a cybersecurity managed services provider in Austin

- Shadowed incident response team during active security events
- Built a home lab using VirtualBox to practice penetration testing

**Tools/Resources Used:**

**Key Breakthrough:**
During his SkillBridge internship, Marcus identified a security misconfiguration in a client's firewall rules during a routine audit. His infantry training—attention to detail and constant security mindset—made him spot something others had missed. The client avoided a potential breach, and Marcus's supervisor wrote a glowing recommendation highlighting his "natural security mindset and attention to detail."

## Key Tactics That Worked

### Tactic 1: Translate, Don't Apologize

**What They Did:**
Marcus stopped apologizing for his lack of IT experience and started confidently translating his military skills. He created a "translation document" mapping infantry tasks to cybersecurity concepts:
- Perimeter security → Network defense
- Threat assessment → Risk analysis
- After-action reviews → Incident post-mortems
- Communications security → Information assurance
- Battle drills → Incident response procedures

**Why It Worked:**
Cybersecurity is fundamentally about the same things infantry soldiers do: identify threats, assess vulnerabilities, protect assets, and respond to incidents. By reframing his experience, Marcus helped civilian hiring managers see the direct connection instead of viewing him as a career changer.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Create your own translation document. List 10-15 things you did regularly in your MOS. For each one, research the civilian/technical equivalent. Practice explaining your experience using industry terminology. Don't say "I don't have experience with X, but..." Say "In my role, I conducted [civilian equivalent], which directly translates to [the skill they want]."

### Tactic 2: The SkillBridge Shortcut

**What They Did:**
Marcus used the DoD SkillBridge program to gain 3 months of full-time, real-world experience before separating. This meant he could list actual cybersecurity work experience on his résumé before he even left the military.

**Why It Worked:**
The #1 barrier for career changers is the "you need experience to get experience" catch-22. SkillBridge broke that cycle. Marcus graduated from the Army on a Friday and started his civilian job on Monday with 3 months of industry experience already on his résumé. More importantly, his SkillBridge supervisor became a professional reference who could vouch for his technical skills.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Apply for SkillBridge 6-9 months before your ETS. Choose opportunities in your target career field, not just any available position. Treat it like an extended job interview—your SkillBridge host might hire you directly (20-30% do), but even if they don't, you'll have experience and references. Search for opportunities at dodskillbridge.com.

### Tactic 3: One Certification, Done Right

**What They Did:**
Instead of collecting multiple certifications or starting a degree program, Marcus focused intensely on passing Security+ on his first attempt. He studied 2-3 hours daily for 90 days using free/cheap resources, took detailed notes, and drilled weak areas until he could explain concepts to others.

**Why It Worked:**
Security+ is the "baseline" certification that opens doors to 80% of entry-level cybersecurity jobs. It's specifically required for any DoD cybersecurity role (thanks to DoD 8570 requirements). By getting it done quickly and well, Marcus made himself eligible for thousands of positions. Multiple certifications would have looked good but delayed his job search by months.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Identify the ONE certification that unlocks the most doors in your target field. For cybersecurity: Security+. For cloud: AWS Cloud Practitioner or Solutions Architect. For networking: Network+. Focus exclusively on that single cert. Use free resources (Professor Messer, FedVTE). Pass it. Then job search aggressively. Get more certs later when your employer pays for them.

### Tactic 4: The Home Lab Portfolio

**What They Did:**
Marcus built a simple home lab using free tools (VirtualBox, Kali Linux, Metasploitable2, Windows VMs). He documented 5 projects: setting up a vulnerable machine and exploiting it, analyzing malware in a sandbox, configuring firewall rules, creating a SIEM dashboard, and conducting a penetration test. He added these to his LinkedIn and résumé.

**Why It Worked:**
Hiring managers constantly worry that career changers have "book knowledge" but can't do the actual work. The home lab proved Marcus could apply concepts hands-on. During interviews, when asked about practical experience, he walked through specific projects: "In my home lab, I configured Splunk to ingest Windows logs and created alerts for suspicious PowerShell execution..." This distinguished him from candidates who only had theoretical knowledge.

**How You Can Apply It:**
You don't need expensive equipment. A laptop with 8GB RAM and free software is enough. Follow YouTube tutorials to set up basic labs. Document everything with screenshots. Write up 3-5 projects as LinkedIn posts or blog entries. In interviews, reference specific things you built. Even simple projects like "set up a Linux server and hardened it" or "configured a pfSense firewall" demonstrate hands-on capability.

### Tactic 5: The Veteran Network Backdoor

**What They Did:**
Marcus joined VetSec Slack, attended veteran-focused tech meetups, and specifically reached out to veteran hiring managers on LinkedIn. When job searching, he filtered LinkedIn for "veteran" in company profiles and targeted those organizations. He also asked every veteran he met: "Do you know anyone hiring in cybersecurity?"

**Why It Worked:**
Veterans preferentially hire veterans. It's not just sentiment—it's confidence in shared values like reliability, work ethic, and ability to handle pressure. Marcus's job offer came from a veteran hiring manager who immediately understood his background. Even companies without formal veteran programs often have individual veterans in leadership who advocate for veteran candidates.

**How You Can Apply It:**
Make your veteran status visible on LinkedIn (add "U.S. Army Veteran" to your headline). Join veteran tech communities (VetSec, Vets in Tech, Veterati). Research companies' veteran hiring programs before applying. In cover letters, lead with military experience. During networking, connect with veterans first—they're more likely to respond and help. Use your network to get warm introductions rather than cold applying.

## Obstacles Overcome

### Obstacle 1: Imposter Syndrome and Self-Doubt

**The Problem:**
For the first 4 months, Marcus constantly felt like a fraud. In VetSec Slack channels, people discussed tools and concepts he'd never heard of. During his SkillBridge internship, he worried that asking "basic" questions would expose him as unqualified. He almost quit twice, convinced he wasn't "technical enough" to succeed.

**How They Overcame It:**
His SkillBridge mentor noticed Marcus's hesitation in meetings and pulled him aside: "You know what you bring that these CS graduates don't? You understand consequences. You've made decisions where lives were at stake. Cybersecurity is the same—we protect businesses, customer data, critical infrastructure. Your military experience is relevant, not a disadvantage."

Marcus started reframing his self-talk. Instead of "I don't know what SIEM means," he thought "I don't know what SIEM means yet, but I can learn it in a week." He also discovered that many successful cybersecurity professionals were self-taught career changers. The imposter syndrome didn't vanish, but he learned to act despite it.

**Lesson Learned:**
Imposter syndrome affects everyone, especially career changers. The difference between those who succeed and those who quit is action. You don't need to feel confident—you need to act despite lacking confidence. Skills come from doing, not from feeling ready.

### Obstacle 2: Resume Black Hole (No Responses)

**The Problem:**
Marcus's first 15 job applications went into a black hole. Zero responses. Not even automated rejections. He spent hours customizing cover letters and tweaking his résumé, but nothing worked. The silence was demoralizing and made him question whether he'd made a terrible mistake leaving the military.

**How They Overcame It:**
A veteran career counselor reviewed his résumé and identified the issue: Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) were filtering him out before humans ever saw his application. His résumé lacked keywords from job descriptions, used tables and graphics that confused parsing software, and didn't include a "Technical Skills" section.

Marcus rebuilt his résumé from scratch using an ATS-friendly template. He created a master résumé with every possible skill and experience, then customized each application by copying key phrases from the job description. He also added a Technical Skills section: "Security+, SIEM (Splunk), Vulnerability Assessment (Nessus), Incident Response, Risk Analysis, Network Security." After the redesign, his response rate jumped from 0% to 22%.

**Lesson Learned:**
Most large companies use ATS software to filter applications automatically. If your résumé doesn't have the right keywords, no human will ever see it. Use tools like Jobscan to optimize your résumé for each application. Match the language in the job description. Add a dedicated skills section with relevant tools and technologies. Consider the ATS robot as your first interview.

### Obstacle 3: Technical Interview Anxiety

**The Problem:**
Marcus made it to a technical phone screen with a Fortune 500 company. The interviewer asked, "Explain the difference between symmetric and asymmetric encryption and give examples of each." Marcus's mind went blank. He knew the answer—he'd studied it for Security+—but pressure made him freeze. He stammered through a partial answer and didn't get called back.

**How They Overcame It:**
Marcus realized he needed interview repetition, not just more studying. He joined Pramp for free mock interviews with peers. He created flashcards with common technical questions and practiced explaining answers out loud. He recorded himself answering questions on video and identified filler words and confidence issues.

Most importantly, he developed an interview framework: "That's a great question. Let me break it into parts..." This bought him 5 seconds to organize his thoughts and signaled competence to the interviewer. By his 5th interview, he felt conversational rather than interrogated.

**Lesson Learned:**
Knowing information isn't the same as being able to explain it under pressure. Interview skills are separate from technical knowledge. Practice mock interviews until explaining concepts becomes natural. Develop frameworks for answering (STAR method for behavioral, define-explain-example for technical). The more interviews you do, the less nervous you become.

## What They'd Do Differently

### Looking Back

After 18 months in his cybersecurity role, Marcus reflected on what he'd change and what he'd keep the same.

**Would Change:**

1. **Start Networking Earlier**: "I waited until I had Security+ before reaching out to people. I should have started networking 6 months earlier. The relationships matter more than the timing. If I'd built those connections earlier, I might have landed a job even faster."

2. **Document the Journey Publicly**: "I wish I'd started a blog or LinkedIn series about my transition while it was happening. I could have built an audience and helped others, and it would have made me more visible to recruiters. Now I'm doing it in reverse, but starting earlier would have compounded."

3. **Negotiate the First Offer**: "I accepted the first offer immediately because I was so excited to get one. I should have negotiated. They probably would have gone to $100K or added more sign-on bonus. I left money on the table out of fear and gratitude. Next job, I'm negotiating."

**Would Keep:**

1. **Focusing on One Cert First**: "I saw other people collecting certifications like Pokemon cards. Security+ was enough to get me in the door. Now my employer pays for additional certs. Focusing on one was absolutely the right move—it got me employed fast."

2. **Using SkillBridge**: "Best decision I made. Those 3 months of experience and the relationships I built were worth more than any certification. If you're separating and not using SkillBridge, you're making a mistake."

3. **Joining VetSec Early**: "The VetSec community gave me confidence, connections, and practical advice that no career counselor could provide. Being around other veterans making the same transition reminded me I wasn't alone. Community matters."

## Advice for Others

### Who This Approach Works For

This path is ideal for veterans from non-technical MOSs who want to break into cybersecurity quickly without spending years in school.

**Best fit if you:**
- Have 4-6 months before separation or recently separated
- Are disciplined enough to study 2-3 hours daily
- Have basic computer literacy (comfortable with Windows, email, web browsers)
- Can articulate how your military experience translates to security work
- Are willing to start at entry-level (~$75K-$95K) with growth potential
- Have access to SkillBridge or can intern/volunteer to gain experience

**Might not work if:**
- You want immediate six-figure salary (that typically requires 3-5 years experience)
- You're not willing to study technical material (certification is essential)
- You need income immediately (plan requires 4-8 months of preparation/job search)
- You're locked into a specific location with limited tech industry presence
- You're not comfortable with continuous learning (cybersecurity evolves constantly)

## Key Takeaways

### For You to Remember

1. **Your Military Experience IS Cybersecurity Experience**: Stop viewing your background as a disadvantage. Infantry soldiers conduct threat assessment, vulnerability analysis, perimeter security, and incident response daily. Reframe your experience in civilian terms.

2. **One Certification Opens Doors**: You don't need multiple certifications to get started. Security+ is enough to make you eligible for thousands of entry-level positions. Focus, pass it, then start applying.

3. **SkillBridge Is Your Secret Weapon**: Three months of real-world experience before separating breaks the experience catch-22. It's the single most valuable program available to transitioning service members.

4. **Network > Applications**: More jobs come from connections than applications. Join veteran tech communities, attend meetups, and reach out to veterans in your target field. Your network is your net worth.

5. **Action Beats Confidence**: You'll never feel 100% ready. Imposter syndrome doesn't go away—you just learn to act despite it. Start before you're ready. Apply before you're perfect. Interview before you're comfortable.

### Success Factors

What made this work:
- **Clear timeline and plan**: Marcus worked backward from his separation date to create a realistic timeline
- **Focus over breadth**: One certification done well beat multiple half-learned skills
